Question: What if one member of our household is sick with the flu? Should the rest of us stay home as well?

Answer: Public health officials ask that if you are sick with an influenza-like illness, self-isolate at home and stay home until you are symptom-free. If you can isolate yourself from the rest of the household, other family members can go about their usual activities. Everyone in the household should observe good infection control practices (wash your hands often with soap and water, clean shared surfaces frequently). If the sick person requires a care-giver, that care-giver can go about their usual business as long as they are symptom-free. Remember, the majority of residents who contract H1N1 will only experience a mild flu, lasting three to four days.

Seven more deaths linked to the H1N1 virus were confirmed Monday, as Health Canada announced a webcast to be held Tuesday to discuss how one of the most vulnerable populations -- First Nations people -- can best combat the illness.

Beginning today at the former Robert Usher Collegiate, several high-risk groups will be able to receive H1N1 vaccinations.

These groups include:

- Severely immunocompromised individuals of all ages including patients on dialysis,

- Those undergoing chemotherapy or radiotherapy,

- People who are HIV positive,

- Patients with leukemia/lymphoma as well as people with other severe immunodeficiency or on immunosuppresive therapy, and,

- Organ or stem cell transplant recipients.

Also qualifying for the H1N1 shot today are people under 35 with underlying medical conditions such as chronic heart disease, diabetes, asthma and chronic lung disease, kidney disease, blood disorders, severe obesity, liver disease and those treated for long periods with acetylsalicylic acid (ASA).

Primary caregivers (up to a maximum of two) of children under six months of age will also be eligible to roll up their sleeves.

School officials with the public and Catholic school systems reported that H1N1 immunizations went smoothly on Monday -- the first day Regina children from kindergarten to Grade 6 began getting the shots.

"Everything seems to be going fine," said Regina Public Schools spokesman Terry Lazarou.

He didn't know how many public school students were vaccinated. Schools sent information and consent forms home with children, but the school divisions don't track the results; the Regina Qu'Appelle Health Region does.

"If they were our consent forms, we would have tallied them. But since they aren't, we don't know," said Regina Catholic Schools spokeswoman Jennifer Cartwright. "We haven't heard of any problems at the division level and, typically, we would if there was a problem with the process."

Mark Torjusen, spokesman with the Regina Qu'Appelle Health Region, said there was a strong demand for the vaccinations at the schools.

"There was a very strong uptake in terms of children being vaccinated," Torjusen said. "We had to shuffle some staff around, but there was enough people to carry out the vaccinations."

At Usher Collegiate, nurses will continue to vaccinate the groups started on Nov. 2. These include children six months to less than five years, pregnant women, women up to four weeks post partum, and children aged five years through to Grade 6. Torjusen was not aware of lengthy waits at Usher on Monday.

This week, clinics will run from 9 a.m. to 7:45 p.m. except for Wednesday -- Remembrance Day.

On Saturday, a clinic will be held at Robert Usher from 9 a.m. to 7:45 p.m. and at the Core-Ritchie Neighbourhood Centre, 445-14th Ave., from 9 a.m. to 4 p.m.

People are asked to bring their Saskatchewan health cards to the clinics. As more vaccine becomes available, vaccinations will be extended to other high-risk groups, and then the general public.

Information about pandemic H1N1 influenza vaccination clinics in Regina and the region's rural communities is now available through the region's H1N1 influenza information line. The public can call the toll-free line at 1-888-354-8111 for dates, times and locations of immunization clinics in the region. People under 65 on Montreal Island with a chronic medical condition can get their inoculation against H1N1 immediately rather than wait another week or two, health authorities announced yesterday.

Authorities rolled out the vaccination welcome mat for adults with cardiac, lung or other chronic problems 15 days ahead of schedule.

They cited shorter than anticipated weekend lineups at clinics, combined with an adequate supply of the vaccine.

For children with chronic conditions, age 5 to 18, the shots are being made available eight days earlier than anticipated.

"There is now some space available in our clinics to proceed a little more quickly than we had forecast, for some of the at-risk groups," said Dr. Robert Lessard of Montreal public health services.

"We have for Montreal (Island) about 150,000 vaccine doses currently available," Lessard told a media briefing.

"We want to distribute them as quickly as possible."

Also starting today, women who are fewer than 20 weeks pregnant can receive an adjuvant-free version of the vaccine.

As well, Quebec might move up the vaccination schedule for healthy children age 5 to 18 by two weeks, said Marie-Ève Bédard, press attaché to Health Minister Yves Bolduc.

As it stands, this group is scheduled for shots beginning Dec. 7.

Yesterday's shift for people under 65 with chronic conditions represents a significant acceleration in vaccine deployment.

Before it was made, those age 5 to 18 with a chronic illness were to have become eligible for H1N1 vaccination next Monday. For those age 18 to 65 with a chronic illness, the original date for inoculation was Nov. 23.

(Adults with immune deficiencies became eligible for their shot Nov. 5.)

In a separate announcement yesterday, Quebec said it intends to send so-called "flying squads" of nurses into public and private long-term care residences to inoculate seniors with reduced mobility, when they become eligible.

But seniors who are able to get around on their own will be expected to go to regular vaccination clinics with the rest of the population, Bédard said.

Under the current schedule, healthy adults won't be eligible for shots until Dec. 7.

Echoing Bédard, however, Lessard suggested that an accelerated vaccination schedule is possible for others who don't yet qualify.

Decisions could be made quickly, he added. "We're having a meeting about that" this morning.

The announcement about "flying-squads" was applauded by Luc Vallerand, director-general of the Association québécoise des retraité(e)s des secteurs public et parapublic.

"A lot of the residents can't get around very well and are sick" in what are known as Centres d'hébergement et de soins longue durée, or CHSLDs, Vallerand said.

His group had been lobbying to bring the vaccination campaign into long-term care residences.

Neither Vallerand nor Bédard were able to provide more specific details, but Vallerand said his group will continue to lobby the health minister so that seniors with chronic illnesses will be vaccinated sooner.

Meanwhile, officials at the vaccination site at Place Alexis Nihon said so few people turned up for shots yesterday that they began to vaccinate even those who were not on the priority list, according to news reports.

So far, about one million Quebecers have been vaccinated against the flu strain, which has killed at least seven people in the province.

Profits will be down more than 20 per cent this year across a range of industries analyzed by the Conference Board of Canada.

The economy is recovering, and profit growth is expected to resume, but the recovery will be shaky in several sectors, the Conference Board said in a report Monday.

The H1N1 virus could put additional pressure on the recession-battered food services and accommodation sectors, rising fuel costs are hurting transportation industries and the high Canadian dollar is a boon for importers, but a headache for exporters, the Conference Board said in a report compiled in collaboration with the Business Development Bank of Canada.

“All industries are grappling with the effects of reduced demand and downward pressure on prices as a result of the recession,” said Michael Burt, the Conference Board's associate director responsible for industrial economic trends research. “On the upside, the recession has lowered input prices and limited wage growth, reducing cost pressures.”

Here's a sector-by-sector breakdown:

Wholesale trade: Profit growth expected to resume in 2010

“This industry is closely linked to the performance of the overall economy,” the Conference Board said. “Wholesalers dependent on the auto sector and construction industry have suffered badly. However, sectors such as food, apparel and pharmaceuticals are less sensitive to business cycles and actually expanded during the recession.

“Profits will fall for the second consecutive year in 2009, from $14.5-billion to $11.2-billion. But as the recession fades, profit growth will resume in 2010.”

The good:The strong dollar has been a major source of cost-savings, as a large portion of the goods sold by this sector are imported. Also, wholesale sales to automotive sector are rebounding with several temporarily-shuttered plants coming back online. Location is a key advantage for Canadian wholesalers, as they are the link between buyers and sellers. This reduces the threat of foreign competitors.

The not-so-good: The industry is highly competitive, the Conference Board said. This means that recent cost savings resulting from the strong Canadian dollar are being passed through to customers, limiting profit margins. Also, demand for wholesale services depends on the ability of other industries, such as retail, to sell their products. Wholesalers have little power to stimulate sales at the retail level, the Conference Board said.

Schabas said there were early signals H1N1 would be the dominant strain this flu season.

A phenomenon known as "strain replacement," where the new virus replaces old ones that had been circulating, is a classic characteristic of a pandemic virus.

"That's what happened in 1918, it's what happened in 1957 and it's what happened in 1968," says Schabas, now medical officer of health for Ontario's Hastings and Prince Edward Counties Health Unit.

Most provinces and territories have delayed all or parts of their regular seasonal influenza programs until after the H1N1 vaccination campaign is complete.

In the Southern Hemisphere, where the flu season runs from April to September, H1N1 began to be prevalent in May, and then increasingly became the predominant strain.

"There was strong reason to believe that there would be strain replacement, that we would not see significant levels of seasonal flu activity this year," Schabas said. He said enough seasonal flu vaccine could have been produced to offer immunization to people over 65, and those with chronic diseases who are at high risk of flu complications, before shifting production to targeting H1N1.

"The best time to immunize is before the outbreak starts. We're not going to actually be able to even offer the vaccine to healthy people until the outbreaks are largely over, at least in many parts of the country," Schabas said.

Separating the news from the noise

On Aug. 31, 1997, I was the producer of what was to be a new, edgy, open-line talk show just about to launch on one of the national news networks. For weeks, we planned topics, booked guests, wrote intros and planned witty 'surprises' that would make the show pop. The morning of our launch, the day of our debut, was the day Diana died.

People were sad, angry, full of blame. Our best-laid plans were cancelled, we opened the phone lines and let people talk, opine, tell their stories--it went on for weeks, no other conversation existed. As 'caller on line 1' wailed, causing everyone in the control room to snicker and have to throw to commercial, "there is no good left in the world." It was clearly a hysterical response.

While of course the story is different, this is nonetheless what I am reminded of as I watch the H1N1 coverage evolve. It has been a story with legs, projected in no small part by the media, which must accept some responsibility for the hoopla.

The "News" bombards you with worst-case scenarios, leading with every permutation and combination of the H1N1 story, real and hypothetical. Clever cut lines and animated graphics create doughnuts around alarming "SPECIAL REPORTS." 'Kid with hockey stick' photo is run front page, creating a mass Pavlovian response in parents: get thee to a lineup, fight wildebeest and wait time. In the guise of keeping you informed, the media highlights the five-hour wait, which, played enough times, becomes everyone's experience. The no-wait example is on the cutting room floor. Sound bites and quotes are taken from "streeters," not exactly informed sources, and played as if they represent the norm. Do that enough times, and soon enough they do. "The mood of the people," journalists call it. This is the mood of people from whom you wouldn't under normal circumstances take advice on pet-bathing.

In Alberta's case, add to this a government already on shaky legs, and you've practically got La Boheme. It's a good story.

We, as human beings, are responding, internalizing the hype as if it was fact. "Have you had your shot" has replaced "How 'bout the weather" as most ubiquitous opening line. I have a friend who answers her phone with a terse: "Don't even ask me if I've had my shot . . . Hello." Burly firefighters, policemen, even cab drivers are so moved by fear they lobby for a better place in line at the vaccine buffet like the shrimp is going to run out.

The H1N1 shot has become the cabbage patch doll of vaccines, with people who didn't even want one, now so frightened they're going to be without-- they're scrapping to grab-up a piece of "Nervous Nellie" before she's all gone. When I tune in now, I half expect to see live cameras following the vaccine in a white Bronco speeding down the highway as it's being delivered. It's become a ridiculous spectacle, with the panic completely overrunning the risk.

Fortunately, you in turn have a choice on how you respond to the news. Look beyond the coverage of the table thumping and calls for inquiries. Those are 'good viz' (visuals) as we say in the television biz, but not the most important story that's relevant to you right here and right now. We've buried the lead, again, as we say.

Separate the noise from the news. Tell the difference between views and the news. If you hate the government, plot your revenge for when it's meaningful, instead of obfuscating. In the meantime, get the message, not the medium. Don't lose the story. This is a bad virus, different from the rest. Get the shot when your time comes, sneeze into your sleeve, wash your hands, stay home when you're sick and watch for the signs that you need a doctor. That's the nub of it.

Take the rest of it with a grain of salt, not a spoonful of sugar.

Karin Klassen Is A Calgary Television Producer. Her Column Runs Every Second Monday.

I am a family physician and I am going to get my H1N1 flu shot and so are my children. I hope that my fellow Canadians will too.

Based on what occurred in the Southern Hemisphere during their winter this year, it is expected that about 80 per cent or more of the influenza virus circulating this year will be H1N1 and about 20 per cent seasonal flu.

There is a lot of misinformation being passed from person to person about the flu vaccine. And, unfortunately, many are simply accepting what they hear as fact.

Why do people who would never use Google or the opinions of friends and colleagues to decide that they knew more than their lawyer, engineer or architect, decide that they know better than their physicians? Or the PhDs in immunology and microbiology and public health staff who research these issues for months or years and have the scientific background to be able to truly evaluate the research and safety data?

How could all the scientists and physicians and governments in every province, state and country in the world who are all looking at the data and recommending vaccination be wrong?

Here are the facts: The seasonal flu vaccine and H1N1 vaccine are called subunit vaccines which do not introduce live or whole flu virus into the body. The flu vaccine delivers a minute quantity of a sterilized, purified portion of the influenza virus into the body. The immune system is then stimulated to respond and, in about two weeks, this results in immunity.

When someone gets the actual flu, they have had whole, live, influenza virus enter their body through their nose and mouth. The influenza virus attaches to the nasopharynx cells, then enters and reproduces within them. Millions and millions of live viruses are then released, teaming throughout the person's body and bloodstream. This is an overwhelming attack and, as the battle ensues and the immune system fights, the temperature rises, the body fatigues and the symptoms of influenza result. Most cases of H1N1 have been mild but, as we have seen, for some -- and sometimes randomly -- the battle is severe and death results.

Why would you choose for yourself, or much more importantly for your child, to come into contact with the live virus and submit to millions and millions of live, replicating viruses circulating for days and days? Does it not make more sense to, instead, get a vaccine that injects a sterilized and killed tiny portion of that same virus? I have chosen this option for my children and for myself (including during my pregnancies) for over a decade and have no regrets.

The problem with subunit vaccines is that since they contain only a part of the virus, they often cannot stimulate a complete immune response, especially in those with weak immune systems such as young children, the elderly, or those with chronic diseases. For that reason, scientists add adjuvants to vaccines. Adjuvants are immune-system stimulators that enhance the response to the vaccine. (The October 2009 issue of Scientific American has a thorough article on vaccines and adjuvants.) The H1N1 vaccine contains an oil-in-water adjuvant called squalene. Squalene is a naturally occurring sterilized oil. Oil-in-water adjuvants have been studied and approved as safe and are used in various European vaccines, including their flu shots.

Something that really puzzles me is how quickly people will put into their own and their children's bodies anything labelled as an immune stimulant if it is sold in a natural products store. These products do not have long-term safety data, are much less rigorously tested for safety than vaccines and adjuvants, and claims are often supported by very poor quality studies that were done by the very companies selling the product.

There is a question I am frequently asked: If the nurses, paramedics and hospital workers are not getting vaccinated, doesn't that suggest that I should be concerned about getting vaccinated?

It is interesting that the rate of vaccination among health care workers is similar to the rate in the general population. The reason for this seems to be that health care workers obtain their information about the vaccine and influenza in exactly the same way as the general public. They Google, read newspapers and talk among themselves. There are no requirements for health care workers to participate in any accredited educational programs related to vaccines or influenza.

To protect those at high risk from influenza, in whom the vaccine does not protect well due to their weak immune response, it is important that their caregivers be vaccinated so that they do not infect them. It is particularly concerning that health care workers could infect or kill our most vulnerable by choosing not to be vaccinated. Research indicates that H1N1 is infectious one day before symptoms, and up to seven to 10 days afterward.

H1N1 is now present in our community and will be spreading quickly. It's time for all of us to come together and protect ourselves and our families, and help to protect the vulnerable in our community. One way to do that is to get vaccinated. The vaccine takes two weeks to stimulate immunity, so it's important to be vaccinated as soon as it is available to you. That's what my family is planning to do.

BACKGROUND

The H1N1 flu virus is a new strain of pandemic influenza that is different from the seasonal flu. People have no natural immunity to protect against this virus. The H1N1 flu virus emerged in April 2009 and surveillance of its spread shows that it is affecting more young and healthy people than the regular seasonal flu, which normally affects seniors and young children. People with underlying medical conditions, and pregnant women, may be at a greater risk for severe illness.

What is it?

The H1N1 flu virus -- also known as human swine influenza -- is a respiratory illness that affects the nose, throat and lungs. This virus usually affects pigs, but has been transferred to humans.

The H1N1 flu virus has caused primarily mild illness in Canada, but Canadians need to be prepared to respond to a potentially more severe form of the virus that may emerge this fall.

How is it spread?

The H1N1 flu virus is contagious and is spread the same way as regular seasonal influenza. This happens when an infected person coughs or sneezes and their germs enter the nose, eyes, or throat of another person. The germs can also rest on hard surfaces like counters and doorknobs, and can be picked up on hands and transmitted to the respiratory system when someone touches his mouth and/or nose. It is not possible to catch it by eating pork or pork products or through blood transfusions.

Contagiousness

More research is being done on how long a person can be infectious (be able to spread the virus to others), but it is believed that this period is for one day before the onset of symptoms and continues for approximately seven days after symptoms have started. The time it takes between being infected and experiencing symptoms is between two and seven days.

Symptoms

- Almost always

Cough and fever

- Common

Fatigue

Muscle aches

Sore throat

Headache

Decreased appetite

Runny nose

- Sometimes

Nausea

Vomiting

Diarrhea

Prevention

- Wash hands often.

- Keep common surfaces disinfected.

- Cough and sneeze into your arm, instead of into your hand.

- If you are sick, stay home until your symptoms are gone and you feel well enough to participate in all activities.

- If you get flu-like symptoms and are pregnant, have underlying health problems, or if your symptoms get worse, contact your health-care provider.

Treatment

If you get flu-like symptoms and are pregnant or have underlying health problems contact your health-care provider.

If you get flu-like symptoms and are otherwise healthy, you should stay home to recover. If your symptoms worsen, or you experience difficulty breathing or serious shortness of breath, it is important to seek medical attention.

Antivirals are drugs used for the early treatment of influenza. If taken shortly after getting sick (within 48 hours), they can reduce influenza symptoms, shorten the length of illness and potentially reduce the serious complications of influenza. Antivirals do not prevent you from getting sick.

Canada has a National Antiviral Stockpile of 55 million doses of two antiviral drugs -- Tamiflu and Relenza. Both are effective in treating H1N1 flu virus. This stockpile is enough for all Canadians who will need and want treatment.

Antivirals are recommended for the treatment of moderate to severe illness, and for people at risk of severe disease. Your doctor will decide if treatment is right for you.

Vaccination

Influenza vaccines (also called flu shots) help you to prevent getting sick by introducing your body to a weakened or dead version of the virus to teach your body to build immunity to it.
