Progressive Conservative party members gave Premier Ed Stelmach a strong show of support Saturday, defying public sentiment and bolstering his leadership as he enters a trying political period.

Seventy-seven per cent of convention delegates approved Stelmach's leadership. That passes the informal threshold of 70 per cent set by former Premier Ralph Klein and will likely quiet some of Stelmach's most ardent critics.

"We have a very big tent party," Stelmach told party supporters. "And I know that with such a large tent, a large party, there will be a variety of opinions."

Stelmach said he heard one message in particular from delegates.

"Improve your communication. Policies are right for Alberta, but you're not getting the message out."

Still, resounding support from the party is different than resounding support from the public, and Stelmach has faced an autumn of discontent.

From the province's $6.9-billion deficit, to a byelection loss in Calgary, controversial health-care reforms and problems with the H1N1 vaccination program, the premier has had little good news lately.

Most recently, an Environics poll showed his party has fallen from 55-per-cent support across the province to 34 per cent today.

Meanwhile, the Wildrose Alliance Party, under its new leader, Danielle Smith, has seen its support take off.

Saturday's result won't immediately change the poll or budget numbers, nor will it vaccinate people, but it should give Stelmach some political breathing space as he tries to put together another budget in the midst of a recession.

Stelmach has said an election will take place in March 2012.

By then, he's hoping commodity prices will have turned around and the province will be running surpluses again.

Stelmach's opponents said the victory does little to change the political picture in the province.

"The premier has survived this leadership review, thanks to his ability to motivate his tightly-knit cadre of supporters. But I'm afraid that this won't help him win back the support of the wider public," Liberal Leader David Swann said in a statement.

"His administration has dropped the ball too often for Albertans to forgive."

Smith, the Wildrose party leader, said she won't be doing anything different after the vote.

"This is exactly what we expected, but it goes against what I am hearing across the province," Smith said.

"Albertans are upset with this government for destroying investor confidence in our energy industry, for mishandling our health-care system and for taking us back into deficits."

Going into the convention, there was some griping among Tories that Stelmach was dragging the party down and needed to be replaced in time for the next election.

Even during the convention, there was obvious division over policy, including a tie vote on Bill 50, a piece of legislation to streamline the approval process for power lines.

But in the end, party members said the convention brought them together and hardened their resolve in tough times.

"I think what's happened this weekend is this party has been galvanized around itself and around the leader," Aboriginal Affairs Minister Gene Zwozdesky said.

Tourism Minister Cindy Ady said Stelmach showed this weekend that he can and will listen to grassroots Tories. "We think that he is the right premier in the spot right now," Ady said. "I think the party's saying we trust him as a leader and we're going to work with him."

In total, 1,191 ballots were counted in the mandatory leadership review. Of those, 922 voted against plunging the party into a leadership race.

Edmonton MLA Thomas Lukaszuk said Saturday's result shows they are on the right track, "but there's room for sort of retuning your radar and readjusting some of your policy."

Indeed Stelmach said he is contemplating bigger changes in his government, possibly including a cabinet shuffle. But he won't do it right away. "I need time to think it through," he said. "Again, it will be my timeline."

Passengers are streaming through the glass doors and into the muted light of the public hall at Pearson's Terminal One. Some march, almost head down, toward the exits and waiting limousines. Others linger, searching for loved ones among the welcoming crowd.

Nearly everyone sports the universal airport face, that strange, half-asleep look of apprehension, as if they're inhabiting some kind of in-between world, suspended.

Above them, a giant electronic board lists the scheduled arrivals of planes from Amritsar, London, Houston, Frankfurt ... the list goes on.

As Toronto struggles through H1N1 influenza amid shortages of vaccine, it is here, at the international arrival gates of Pearson, that every subsequent pandemic will also arrive, borne from every imaginable corner of the planet.

By one estimate, Toronto is the third most vulnerable city in North America, surpassed only by New York and Los Angeles in its potential exposure to diseases that develop elsewhere and quickly travel the world's airlines, unseen.

Many of those disembarking at this point in the afternoon have arrived on Etihad Flight 141 from Abu Dhabi, but that's only the last leg of their journey. Kelly Surette and Kane Riley-Henderson started theirs 30 hours earlier in Sydney. Neelima Dahata began hers 22 hours earlier, in New Delhi.

It's those types of trips that set Dr. Kamran Khan, an infectious disease specialist at St. Michael's Hospital, on another kind of voyage five years ago, in the wake of SARS.

That disease follows humans might seem obvious, but Khan dreamed of a loftier construction.

More than 2 billion passengers shuttle between 3,500 international airports each year.

Could an intricate knowledge of their travel patterns allow him and his colleagues to predict how and when an emerging disease would reach Toronto?

And could that information help contain the spread of disease?

Doing graduate work in New York, Khan had watched the arrival of West Nile disease first-hand. He was there for the anthrax scare, too, and when he returned to Toronto, it was just in time for SARS, the virus that killed 44 people.

"It was very clear what an emerging epidemic could do to a city and a country," says Khan. "I think it was always understood that this could happen, it's just that this was right in our face."

He remembers looking at maps showing where new cases were arising, and realizing that a kind of social networking was at play, as if international airports had their own group on Facebook.

Or, as Khan puts it: "Pearson knows Reagan knows JFK knows Charles de Gaulle."

Academics have long been interested in the relationship between air travel and disease, but Khan wanted to develop a tool that could help handle real-time outbreaks of disease and pandemics. "We can't stop air travel, but we can try to understand it."

The result was the Bio.Diaspora project, centred at St. Michael's, beginning in early 2004. It would grow to include 11 other academics, with specialties ranging from geography to mathematics, and spend hundreds of thousands of dollars acquiring detailed flight data from the world's airline industry.

Pearson is by far Canada's busiest airport. Last year, 32.3 million passengers passed through it - including just under 10 million international travellers, more than Montreal and Vancouver combined.

But as Khan's project gathered steam, it was Pearson's central position in the architecture of world travel - where and how people move - that really started to stand out.

With 133 direct connections to cities outside the country last year, Pearson would rank 17th in the world, well behind London, with 362.

Flights between cities in the European Union, however, are effectively domestic. If you remove those from the equation, Toronto moves up to eighth in the world, just slightly behind New York, the only other North American city among the top 10.

Another measure Khan's group studied was "closeness" - how accessible a city is to all other cities, based on calculations around flight times and passenger loads.

Last year, Toronto ranked ninth in the world.

Calibrating those and other measures, Khan argues that Pearson is the third most vulnerable city in North America, after New York and Los Angeles, even though Miami receives more international passengers.

That's because Miami's international exposure is so heavily skewed to the Caribbean and Central America.

Not surprisingly, Pearson's central place in the global network shone through when Khan's group starting running simulations, tracking how a disease that emerged in, say, Cairo or Hong Kong, could be expected to travel the world in a first wave of 21 days.

"Our system has been developed with sufficient ability that you could call me and say, 'There's something happening in this city of the world today,' and we could look at that location and come up with a prediction of where (the disease) is likely to go and where it's unlikely to go in the first wave," says Khan.

A range of other factors could still temper the spread, including the specific nature of the disease and how effective the local response has been in the originating city or region.

But "once the cat's out of the bag, then we can start making some reasonably good predictions about where it's going to go and when," says Khan.

And in any disease outbreak, forewarned can mean forearmed.

In the bowels of the Greater Toronto Airports Authority building at Pearson is a large, grey-walled room. A massive wooden table dominates, with 14 chairs around it, the table lined with telephone consoles, each with a microphone on a long stem.

One of the key tools used by medical authorities to get ahead of a virus as it races through a population is mathematical modelling, which uses computers and a whole host of data to mimic the behaviour of the virus in human populations.

As you take your simple model and add layers of complexity to it, you gain the power to predict a variety of outcomes based on variables such as when the vaccination program starts, how many people are vaccinated before the virus begins to spread widely or who you decide to vaccinate first -- super-spreaders such as school children or low-spreaders such as nursing home clients.

Modelling used today is based on the pioneering work of British epidemiologists W.O. Kermack and A.G. McKendrick, who coined the ideas of the reproductive number, the number of new cases that result from each infection; and the generation time, how long it takes new cases to occur.

The resulting model predicts an attack rate -- how quickly a virus will spread through a given population until those spreading the virus start running into more people with immunity than people who have none. At that point, the pandemic starts to wane and we transition to herd immunity, in which even those without immunity are protected because it is so difficult for the virus to spread.

Armed with these numbers, scientists led by the B.C. Public health officials are in a difficult position. Their objective is to react proportionately to the virus but they can't appear to take a threat too lightly. What if H1N1 turned out to be worse than expected and they had chosen not to produce a vaccine? So, they err on the side of extreme caution.

Nothing wrong with being better safe than sorry, right? Unfortunately, there is a cost to overreacting. The Bank of Montreal predicts that a mild pandemic would cut Canada's economic growth by two per cent. But just the threat of H1N1 is already causing a change in consumer behaviour and employee productivity. Workers are distracted. They're more likely to stay home if either they or their kids have the sniffles. Consumers are said to be going out less often. It's like round two of the bus strike.

Some stories have suggested that businesses are underprepared. An Ontario Chamber of Commerce survey found that only 22 per cent of companies have an emergency preparedness plan.

Another study by the Bank of Montreal showed that 82 per cent of small businesses don't have a health-related continuity plan. And an American Express survey showed that 80 per cent of small-business owners put preparing for disruption low on their priority lists.

But none of that proves that businesses are avoiding or refusing to prepare for H1N1. It just shows that in many cases they can't.

If you have a retail operation with three employees and two of them are sick, no amount of planning will prepare you for that. Instead, you do what small businesses do: Hope for the best and adapt when necessary.

Even in a larger company, which might have the resources to prepare exhaustive plans, there's little you can do to change public reaction and the potential impact on your business. Your only choice is whether to ride it out or look for opportunities arising from the crisis.

If there is a silver lining, it's that you don't have to be a manufacturer of hand sanitizer to be opportunistic.

A change in consumer behaviour is often an opportunity to improve market share.

If you can differentiate yourself from your competitors based on a new priority for your customers, you can profit.

The H1N1 situation is a chance to show your customers that you are serious about health and cleanliness.

A grocery store, for example, could introduce new protocols for handling produce that would separate it from its rivals.

More importantly, it's a chance to partner with employees on a healthier work environment.

Putting bottles of hand sanitizer around the office might once have been a wasted effort.

Maybe now, more employees will take advantage of them regularly and you'll see fewer sick days from the seasonal flu next year.

Until recently, you were a hero if you showed up for work even if you were suffering from flu-like symptoms.

Now, you might be considered a jerk for bringing your germs to the office.

Some employees, especially hourly workers, may come in anyway because they can't afford to miss a day's pay. And others may still feel guilty about burdening their co-workers with the extra load.

But there is a new consciousness about protecting the work environment that didn't exist before.

With the right input from management, that could be a good thing for a business long after H1N1 is old news.

A crisis is often the only time that significant change in behaviour occurs.

Some medical experts have argued for years we should do away with the handshake, but it's hard to change a custom without a huge incentive.

And while getting people to regularly bow or tap elbows may still seem highly unlikely, it wasn't that long ago that people were coughing and sneezing into their hands and not their elbows.

Change is possible when fear is a motivator.

To a level-headed business person who is used to measuring risk, the response to H1N1 may be an overreaction that is frustrating and damaging.

But you can't control the hysteria.

Instead, treat it like any other crisis: Be glad it's not bigger and try to profit from it as best you can.
