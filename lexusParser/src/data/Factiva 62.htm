It was pandemonium. People ducked for cover. It was like a grenade had gone off in the movie theatre.

Close: Someone at the back of the house sneezed, and folks as far away as the front of the house recoiled. Some gasped.

Many will suggest this swine-flu frenzy is media-induced. But pity the plight of the poor media: We're damned if we report and we're damned if we don't report. So please, people, get a grip. That's a grip - not a grippe.

It's starting all over again. The germaphobes are back! The germaphobes are back! The swine flu - like C. difficile, the Norwalk virus and SARS before it - has otherwise sensible humans reverting to their inner Howard Hughes.

But at least now we know why humans have been endowed with elbows - other than for dastardly use in the hockey arena. You, too, may have been noticing of late other allegedly evolved primates entering public washrooms and bathroom stalls using the hands-free elbow method. Or, more surreal still, elbow high-fives and shakes. Natch, the two-cheek buss is pretty much verboten these days.

In the same multiplex where that simple sneeze created such anxiety among patrons, the swine-flu hysteria took on even more hysterical proportions in the lavatory.

Forget the elbow brigade. There was a fellow singing Happy Birthday, seemingly to himself, while washing his hands. This inquiring mind wanted to know if it was the gentleman's birthday. Oh, no, he responded: He had just seen a report on CNN that said to ensure one washed one's hands properly, one had to sing Happy Birthday - not once, but twice - to oneself. Or for at least 30 seconds.

A real-life physician actually counselled people to sing Happy Birthday twice? No, the singer said, it was an expert in the field of personal sanitation.

Ah. Fair enough. But what was the warbler going to do after turning off the germ-

infested faucet with one of his washed hands? He then began to utter an obscenity that could only come to the lips of an exasperated germaphobe who had just spent the last few minutes lathering and washing his hands for nothing. He grabbed some paper towels from the dispenser, opened the faucet with one towel, then pressed down on the soap dispenser with it, lathered, washed (but did not sing Happy Birthday twice this time), closed the faucet with the paper towel and dried his hands with another paper towel.

Swell. But just as he was ready to chuck the paper towels into the garbage, I figured it would be only considerate to inform him of the obstacle that lay ahead of him: the germ-infested washroom-door handle. He thanked me, then mentioned that he prefers the multiplexes without washroom doors and with sensor-equipped faucets that turn on simply by placing one's hands underneath the tap.

According to usually reliable sources, though, the bizarro scene in the men's washroom positively pales to that which sometimes plays itself out in the women's washroom. Apparently, there are women who display the dexterity of medal-winning gymnasts in avoiding close contact with certain lavatory facilities. Which is kind of funny, after reading a study suggesting that toilets are often cleaner than computer keyboards, cellphones and kitchen counters.

By the by, best to have paper towels handy if you plan on pressing buttons in an elevator or holding on to handrails on the escalator. Or for opening any doors, for that matter.

And, to be on the safe side, best not to lean back too far and have your head touch the back of your theatre or airplane or bus seat. And watch out for that gas pump at your friendly service station. Cuz you never know.

And let's not even talk about our cash. There have been plenty of reports that paper currency is covered with oodles of bacteria and gunk you don't want to know about, not to mention traces of drugs (some illicit) you may want to know about.

All of which is enough to drive one to much drink. But don't be sticking your digits into that bowl of beer nuts on the bar, because who knows whose germ-infested digits had been mining there previously.

Really, it's a wonder humankind has made it this far. Astonishing to think that back in the bad old days - when most of us weren't packing Purell - some of us rolled around in the mud with abandon, touched other humans and actually lived to tell of our outrageous adventures.

Whatever nomenclature is used -- "swine flu" or H1N1 influenza A -- the fear of its spread is discombobulating.

Already struggling with a sick economy, employers now must countenance a new pandemic with additional deadly financial and reputational liability.

Employers should now audit their readiness for crisis.

The courts require employers to provide a safe workplace.

If there is any hint that an employee may be a carrier and that employee is not immediately forbidden to attend work , the damages could be incalculable. A class action against such an employer by infected employees, suppliers and customers could decimate the strongest balance sheet. Apart from a lawsuit, imagine the impact of a news report on the breakout of swine flu on, say, a retail chain's business? Employers should implement policies relating to infectious diseases. Those policies must prohibit employees with any symptoms from attending the workplace and require them to seek immediate medical attention.

A notice should be circulated delineating the disease's symptoms and requiring any employees experiencing them to self-report. An employee who fails to comply should be disciplined.

The employee should then be interviewed (by telephone or e-mail) as to who they encountered in the company during the preceding few days. Those employees, in turn, should be sent home and required to be tested immediately.

For the purposes of the investigation, to the extent there is a conflict, the rights of your remaining employees to a safe workplace supersede the right of the ill employee to confidentiality.

Some employers have voluntarily continued paying employees to remain at home if their disability benefit policies do not cover their wages, which it will not if the imposed absence is precautionary.

Other companies have prophylactically not permitted any employee who has visited Mexico from entering the workplace until the incubation period of the disease elapses. In such circumstances, it is reasonable to provide additional work after those few days so they suffer no loss of income. Ensuring their income is kept whole not only reflects legal obligations in most circumstances but also encourages sick employees to remain home rather than risk an outbreak.

While it prevents liability, the concomitant absenteeism from this approach can also be debilitating. You should have employees trained to fill key positions to protect against this eventuality.

Employers should also be aware of the following: z Employees are generally entitled to take unpaid leave if they are sick, must care for sick family members or are quarantined. Unless there is a specific short-term disability benefit policy that covers their particular situation, there is no legal obligation to pay employees during such a leave. Benefits, however, must be continued and employees' jobs protected; z If a state of emergency is declared, employees unable to work as a result are generally entitled to an unpaid leave of absence until the emergency is lifted; z Employers also have a duty to accommodate. This means that if employees are able to work but must be absent from the workplace, permit them to work from home if that is practicable; z Unaffected employees can be required to work overtime without consent during such an emergency.

But beware. Employees may, opportunistically or irrationally, use the professed fear of contagion as an excuse not to work. Do not tolerate that. Employees cannot refuse to work if their workplace is certified safe by government inspectors.

Egypt began slaughtering its roughly 300,000 pigs as a precautionary measure against the spread of swine flu even though no cases have been reported in either humans or animals in that country. Underlying religious reasons led to this decision, but the policy is still considered by many to be extreme.

At least 20 countries, from China to Russia to Ukraine, have set bans on all pork product imports. Even Canada has seen its share of panicky assessments. Pork inspections have been increased in an attempt to ease any concerns consumers may have in light of the global influenza A/H1N1 outbreak.

And while the headlines this week are full of news that a herd in Alberta seems to have contracted the flu, there is still no evidence that swine flu can be transmitted through eating meat from infected animals. None.

In the meantime, hog futures are down and the hog industry is losing millions.

From an epidemiological standpoint, the menace is real. But, when it comes to food safety, the "swine flu" story is really nothing more than a sign of the times. All around the world, panic occasioned by outbreaks of the influenza A/H1N1 virus is incommensurate with the risks of contracting it by eating pork. Public officials are making unfounded decisions based on trivial information at best.

Many elements have triggered this phenomenon to occur. The obvious culprits, for lack of a better term, are the media, but before we point too many fingers, there is a lot more going on than merely fear-driven reporting.

Many criticize the media for the insufficient information and/or misinformation with which they bombard the general population in times of crisis. One should understand that misapprehension of risk by media and news audiences is not exclusively responsible for disproportionate fear or panic, but panic is more likely to occur when a threat receives substantial media coverage.

However, currently in Canada, no public authority can play a more effective role than the media in times of public health crisis. Media can operate virtually in real time these days, whereas public authorities must painstakingly react to incidents. Unlike the media, from whom the public is willing to accept a certain degree of error and backtracking, public health authorities simply cannot prematurely release information that could have so devastating an effect as to, for example, prompt the unnecessary slaughter of hundreds of thousands of perfectly safe and healthy livestock.

What has been interesting, at least in the Canadian media, is the frequent repetition that pork is safe to eat. And yet, pork futures are dropping and healthy pigs are being slaughtered in unaffected parts of the world as a "precautionary measure."

Why this is so may, in fact, have less to do with real panic over the epidemic and more to do with simply the way that food trades work.

Considering food trades, borders are usually only selectively opened. Borders serve important roles in modern economies. They safeguard the sovereignty of nations, impose economic protectionism, and assure the screening out of undesirable products. Regrettably, embargoes and restrictions occur almost on a daily basis for all sorts of reasons, justified or not. H1N1 is the latest excuse some countries are using.

It is, in fact, just part and parcel of the normal trading process.

Nonetheless, these measures foster market fear, which leads to unwise behaviour. Pork is the cheapest protein available and many developing countries depend on its accessibility and affordability to feed their populations. Decisions to restrict pork imports will have a devastating impact on food retailers, the transportation sector, restaurants, and most importantly, consumers. Economic multiplier effects, coupled with public panic concerning the infectivity of the flu in people, may also create a pervasive atmosphere of fear and pessimism that could aggravate the current economic downturn felt in the global economy.

What really triggered this public health panic was simply the fact that the public is now more prone to accept risk prevention measures than ever before, especially in matters involving food. BSE, foot and mouth, major salmonella, listeria and E. coli outbreaks have forever altered risk perceptions of consumers worldwide. Even the succession of less serious food recalls and food-related nuisances have taken their toll on the world population, and public authorities know this.

It is difficult to blame anyone, therefore, for the hog industry's woes. The media, the public, and the food trades are all inclined to respond aggressively and perhaps prematurely to such events. The baseless correlation between the current pandemic and the pork meat simply exists in people's minds, and it may be too late to avert major losses. Chicken farmers in some parts of the world had to face the same problem a few years back with the avian flu.

Media do have a role to play in food crises, but global events related to food are inherently fluid and the message should constantly change. The disconnect between pork and influenza A/H1N1 should become evident in a relatively short time, as the epidemic itself seems to be mellowing.

Whether such revelation will occur in time to prevent serious damage to agriculture sectors worldwide remains to be seen.

Dr. Sylvain Charlebois is associate dean in the Faculty of Business Administration at the Kenneth Levene Graduate School of Business, University of Regina.

The outbreak of H1N1 influenza in Mexico has rekindled concerns about a global influenza pandemic. The response has been all too predictable. The World Health Organization has raised the level of pandemic alert and announced that "all of humanity" is under threat. The Public Health Agency of Canada has advised people not to travel to Mexico. And with a (mild) case appearing in Ottawa yesterday, the media are once again feasting on the story.

The facts, however, are more prosaic. Admittedly, when first reported two weeks ago, the events in Mexico were quite alarming. It seemed a new influenza virus was causing serious disease, particularly in young people. This is precisely the scenario we would expect with the beginning of a new pandemic.

Since then, however, almost all the news has been good news, even if it hasn't been presented that way. The most important events have been the ones that haven't happened. It's not entirely clear exactly what is going on in Mexico, but one thing is certain: H1N1 is not behaving like a new pandemic strain.

We now know that the first cases of H1N1 date from early March. This should put Mexico at or even past the peak of its first wave of infection. According to standard pandemic models, Mexico should be seeing about 1,000 deaths each day. Obviously, this is not happening -- not even remotely close. The illness caused by this virus seems to be very mild. And the virus itself may not be so "new" after all.

Some tourists returning from Mexico have brought the virus back with them to Canada and elsewhere. This is hardly surprising. What is more important is that the virus is showing no ability to transmit efficiently here, at least at this time of year. The WHO's notion that the disease is "spreading" every time a visitor brings it to a new country is misleading.

The story is not over yet. We need better epidemiology from Mexico and continued surveillance here. But, overall, the news is now very encouraging. While predictions about the future are inherently hazardous, two scenarios seem most plausible. The virus may die out completely because it is just not quite infectious enough to survive in human populations. Alternatively, it may become established as our dominant seasonal strain. This really shouldn't worry us, however, because the disease is so mild.

So why isn't this message getting out? The sad truth is that there are strong vested interests flourishing from the fear of pandemics. Over the past five years, pandemic planning has mushroomed at all levels of government. Public health agencies have received substantial additional funding for pandemic preparations. Some professionals are building careers as pandemic experts. Good news about pandemics is simply not welcome.

Now is a good time to re-examine the underlying premises of these pandemic fears.

Influenza is an important infectious disease. It regularly kills more Canadians than any other infectious disease. Influenza pandemics do happen -- triggered by the appearance of new strains for which we have no immunity -- but many of our fears are based on misconceptions.

We should dispel the idea that an influenza pandemic necessarily means a catastrophe. The last two pandemics -- the 1957 "Asian" flu and the 1968 "Hong Kong" flu -- were not very different from seasonal influenza except that more people were ill. The 1918 influenza pandemic was worse, but much of the information about it is very sketchy. More to the point, a lot has changed since 1918. We have vaccines, antiviral drugs, antibiotics (most influenza deaths are from secondary bacterial pneumonia), intensive care units and, most importantly, far better general health. Even a 1918-style pandemic should not be a cause for undue alarm.

We should also discard the perception that an influenza pandemic is an emergency. Pandemics unfold on a time-scale of weeks to months; not hours to days. A pandemic can't be "contained," only responded to. In other words, public health authorities should learn to take a little more time to gather and analyse information before raising pandemic alerts or launching pandemic plans.

We need to understand that influenza viruses are not going to suddenly change and become more virulent. This open-ended threat -- usually couched with the term "mutation" -- is a theoretical possibility with any infectious disease. In practical terms, however, it is so rare that it doesn't merit serious consideration. It's the stuff of science fiction.

Once we correct our misconceptions, an influenza pandemic loses much of its terror. It is something we need to be prepared for -- and generally are prepared for -- but not something we should lie awake at night worrying about.

What lessons should we draw from this latest false alarm?

First, we need more and better information before reacting. The Mexican situation looked alarming at first. A little digging revealed a very different and more reassuring picture. There is no point in acting in haste.

Second, public health needs to stick to its principles, regardless of public and political pressures. Canada protested strongly when it was the subject of a WHO travel advisory during the 2003 SARS outbreak. We argued, correctly, that these advisories accomplish nothing except to spread fear and damage the economy of the country targeted. So why did Canada rush to issue a travel advisory on Mexico? It was irrational and unfair. Let's not make that mistake again.

Third, the media must be more critical. How many pandemic false alarms do we need (remember "bird flu" -- the last great threat) before reporters start asking tough questions? How many times do the voices of catastrophe need to be wrong before we doubt their message?

Dr. Richard Schabas is the medical officer of health for the Hastings and Prince Edward Counties Health Unit. He was Ontario's chief medical officer of health from 1987-97.

Canada and the world well remember the SARS epidemic, and China paid a heavy price for its dismal initial response.

This time around, Beijing is not taking any chances with a potential outbreak of H1N1 influenza A, or swine flu, within its borders. Aggressive measures have been taken to combat the perceived danger, and both Canadians travelling in China and Alberta pork products are among the targets.

Although the virus did not originate in China, the authorities have been on high alert. Senior Chinese leaders have been on the case from the beginning, and the Chinese press has followed the flu story closely with a rather open attitude. President Hu Jintao and Premier Wen Jiabao have presided over meetings at the highest levels of the government and Communist party - an unusual move that signals how concerned the top leadership is.

When the first case of H1N1 was detected in China a week ago - a Mexican passenger who took a flight from Mexico to Shanghai and then to Hong Kong - he was immediately treated and isolated in a hospital. Then a large-scale operation no less than a manhunt was mounted: all the passengers on all Mexico-Shanghai-Hong Kong flights were located and put into involuntary quarantine; their family members also were required to undergo housebound health monitoring; and the Hong Kong hotel where the ill passenger stayed was sealed off and all the guests were put under close observation.

The Chinese government went further by rounding up some 40 Mexican nationals in major Chinese cities for observation, even though tests indicated they did not have the virus. This led to a protest from the Mexican government, which complained that its citizens were unfairly targeted. Beijing stood firm and now the two countries have agreed to send reciprocal chartered flights to bring back their citizens.

In an indication of China's commitment to block any possible transmission route for the virus, local officials in the northeast city of Changchun placed 25 newly arrived Canadian students from the University of Montreal under quarantine, even though they showed no signs of being infected. And China was among the first countries to declare a ban on pork products from Alberta, where the first case of H1N1 infection among a herd of pigs was found.

Facing the Canadian government's condemnation of the ban and its threat to go to the World Trade Organization over the matter, Beijing is not backing down. Foreign ministry spokesperson Ma Zhaoxu stated yesterday that what China did, based on its assessment of the situation, was in accordance with WTO and World Health Organization rules.

Has China's reaction been excessive or, as some have charged, has it taken discriminatory measures against the Mexicans and other foreigners? Not really - at least not in light of China's experience.

Beijing certainly has not been racially motivated in dealing with the current crisis. In fact, China was among the very first group of countries to deliver large-scale of aid to Mexico after the flu outbreak. Mexican President Felipe Calderon personally went to the airport to receive a $1 million (U.S.) donation from the Chinese ambassador and a cargo plane of supplies. And the Chinese authorities have put more Chinese nationals under observation than foreigners.

But more importantly, the Chinese reaction to the current flu crisis reflects the painful lessons it learned from the SARS crisis. First, there must be a swift and firm response to the earliest signs of a pandemic outbreak. Failure to do so in the early stages of the SARS outbreak caused loss of life in China and around the world. This may explain the high level of national mobilization this time.

Second, there must be a comprehensive national and international tracking system that can identify and trace every possible lead of the infection, in terms of both the regions and people involved. Failure to do so during the SARS crisis let the virus travel to Toronto and other parts of the world with deadly consequences. China is determined not to let such a thing happen again - this time potentially in reverse order.

Third, there must be forward-looking, better-safe-than-sorry measures in place in anticipation of the worst-case scenario. China was criticized for the initial cover-up and its less than thorough response during the SARS epidemic. Beijing now has an all-out war mentality to avoid facing similar accusations this time around.

Beijing's firm stand also is due to the fact that China's population is much more highly concentrated than that of most other countries. If there were an outbreak, the scale would be much larger and far more difficult to contain.

In addition, China is the largest producer of pork in the world, and consumes half of the world's pork products. It does not have a nationwide anti-virus vaccination program for its swine population. The complexity and uncertainty of the H1N1 virus may be behind Beijing's quick decision to ban pork from Alberta. We may charge that the decision is not "scientific," but a Chinese online opinion poll yesterday showed that over 97 per cent of the Chinese people support the government's ban.

So rather than threats and confrontational methods, Ottawa would be better advised to try to understand China's sense of insecurity over a potential deadly swine flu pandemic. When Foreign Minster Lawrence Cannon travels to Beijing next week for his scheduled visit, he would be wise to discuss this issue with persuasion, not threats - that is, if he is not quarantined upon arrival.

Wenran Jiang is associate professor of political science and the Mactaggart Research Chair of the China Institute at the University of Alberta.

There's panic over an outbreak of swine flu. "Projections are that this virus will kill one million Americans," the top U. S. health official has warned. But the date is 1976. Sadly, the hysteria over the current swine flu outbreak threatens much worse --devastation on a worldwide scale.

A reasonable way to look at the current outbreak of the virus now known as influenza A (H1N1) is to compare it to seasonal flu. On average, seasonal flu and its complications hospitalize about 20,000 Canadians a year and kill 4,000. That's about 140 hospitalizations and over 25 deaths per day during flu season.

Worldwide deaths are 250,000 to 500,000, according to the World Health Organization, or about 700 to 1,400 per day spread out over the year.

All indications are that swine flu is both less harmful and harder to transmit than seasonal flu. The symptoms are the same, and swine flu cases so far have been quite mild. As of this writing, the death rate is zero out of 534 confirmed cases in 20 countries aside from Mexico, with 101 in Canada. Mexicans account for 590 cases and all of the world's 26 deaths.

There's no "Mexican puzzle." All infectious diseases strike much harder in underdeveloped countries, partly because of poorer health care but primarily because the people are less healthy to begin with.

Infectiousness is measured by what's called a "basic reproductive number." The higher the number, the more contagious a virus is. Mexico's National Center for Epidemiology and Disease Control has announced that preliminary models show that figure in the Mexico City metropolitan area is 1.5--a fairly low number. The basic reproductive number for seasonal flu seems to range from 1.5 to 3.0.

The moniker "swine flu" spooks many. But pigs routinely transmit seasonal flu to humans. "Swine flu" simply means the virus contains some pig flu genetic material.

Swine flu won't become "another

Spanish Flu of 1918-19," as pig flu panic purveyors claim. Nothing will. It's been 90 years. Back then we were hobbled by a world war, general health in developed nations was much poorer than in Mexico today and we've since developed antibiotics as well as antivirals. In all flu outbreaks, including the Spanish one, secondary bacterial infections cause the vast majority of deaths.

Mr. Ma said the students were healthy and being well cared for. “The relative Chinese officials have made considerable and appropriate arrangements for their accommodations, food and drink, and health checks. I can tell you that those students' temperatures are normal. They are healthy and in a good condition, and they are in a stable emotional state. They expressed satisfaction with what Chinese side has arranged for them.”

That assertion conflicted with the accounts given by some of the students, who described being singled out on their flight from Beijing to Changchun solely because they were Canadian. One student said conditions inside the hotel were dull but bearable.

“Everyone is feeling great, no one is sick. Spirits are good, but slowly declining,” Martin Deslauriers, a 26-year-old East Asian Studies major at University of Montreal, said in a telephone interview. “We can go outside, there's a ping-pong table. And they have given us some beers so that really helps.”

Mr. Deslauriers said the Canadian embassy had sent a representative to try and meet with the students Tuesday, but that he had been prevented from entering the hotel by Chinese authorities. “They told him that if he entered the facility to see is, he would have to be placed under quarantine too. It's strange, because the people working in the hotel aren't even wearing masks.”

Mr. Deslauriers identified the diplomat as Dial Singh, the first secretary and consul of the Canadian embassy in Beijing.

Embassy spokesman Patrice Cousineau said the embassy has asked the Chinese government to clarify why the students are being quarantined. “We want to understand why they're under quarantine. All we want to know are the exact reasons behind this,” he said.

The Chinese government on Tuesday also defended its decision to bar the import of Alberta pork and pork products after a herd 220 of pigs on a central Alberta farm was found to be infected with H1N1, the virus also known as swine flu. Ottawa has threatened to file a complaint with the World Trade Organization if Beijing leaves the measure in place, arguing that Alberta pork poses no risks since the virus cannot be transmitted from animals to humans, or through food.

“In order to protect the safety of our animal husbandry and the people's health, the Chinese government has no choice but to take temporary preventive and protective measures,” Mr. Ma said. “These urgent protective measures are ... consistent with WTO regulations on strict quarantine measures in emergencies and the principle of minimizing the impact on trade.”

“The way this flu works is still very unclear,” Mr. Ma added.

Both the students' quarantine and the Alberta pork ban are expected to be high on the agenda when Foreign Minister Lawrence Cannon arrives in Beijing this weekend for a five-day visit. The trip, expected to be largely a relationship-rebuilding process after years of rocky ties between Canada and China, was in the works well before the H1N1 crisis broke out.

The University of Montreal students aren't the only apparently healthy individuals being quarantined in China. More than 300 guests – including an unknown number of Canadian citizens – have been under lockdown in a Hong Kong hotel since last week after a Mexican man, who is the only confirmed case so far of H1N1 in China, briefly stayed there.

The United States embassy said Tuesday that at least four of its citizens had been quarantined in China in recent days, though two of those have reportedly since been released.

Meanwhile, the Mexican government, which has complained vociferously about China's “discriminatory” treatment of its nationals – on Tuesday chartered an AeroMexico plane that arrived in China to pick up dozens of Mexican citizens who had been placed under quarantine despite showing no flu symptoms.

At least one Victoria hotel has had a major tour group from Asia cancel its trip here because of fears over the human swine flu epidemic but hoteliers and industry analysts are unfazed, citing other windfalls related to the influenza that will keep tourism healthy.

The Hotel Grand Pacific lost a contract with an Asian tour company that was set to bring 100 students here for two nights at the end of May, said Brian Friesen, the hotel's director of sales and marketing.

"It was a considerable group so we're very concerned about that," Friesen said, adding the hotel has done business with the tour operator in the past.

A small group of three people cancelled its three-night stay at the Inn at Laurel Point this past weekend, said hotel manager Scott Hoadley, but he said the impact was fairly insignificant.

"It was a group from Japan that was advised [by government officials] that they couldn't come," he said.

Earl Wilde, president of the B.C. Hotel Association and the Victoria Regent Hotel's general manager said some hotels have had "minor cancellations" because of the H1N1 virus, especially from Asian tour groups "but it's certainly not widespread."

"The way it's reported in foreign countries is simply sort of 'don't go there' and the world has become a very small place" he said.

But a repeat of the SARS crisis that gave worldwide tourism a beating in 2003 is unlikely, said Chemistry Consulting Group partner Frank Burree.

SARS was responsible for 800 deaths worldwide, including 44 in Toronto and along with the 9/11 terrorism attacks and passport confusion contributed to a deadly cocktail for Canadian tourism.

The Canadian Tourism Commission estimates SARS cost the Canadian economy $722 million between 2003 and 2006 and contributed to thousands of jobs losses.

"We're not look at the kind of numbers that we were seeing with SARS," Burree said.

Tourism Victoria was just as bullish.

"At this point swine flu is a reason for concern for the tourism industry but not for alarm," said spokeswoman Kelsi Woodward.

Influenza scares have yet to threaten individual leisure travellers, Friesen said.

He pointed to the possibility that domestic travellers who have ditched their Cancun holidays might come to sunny Victoria instead.

And just last week the local tourism industry was beaming over the several thousand cruise passengers unloaded at Ogden Point by the two largest cruise ships to ever visit the city -- the Carnival Splendor and Royal Caribbean's Mariner of the Seas -- which rerouted from ports in

Mexico and will make a total of 21 stops this summer.

International travel to Mexico, the epicentre of the outbreak, has virtually ground to a halt over the human swine flu outbreak, and concern has been raised that the U.S. and Canada, the second and third hardest hit countries, could also be sworn off by panicked travellers.

Canada now has 140 confirmed cases of swine flu including the first severe case in Alberta.

Countries such as India, Malaysia and Vietnam have advised citizens to limit non-essential travel to countries with confirmed cases, including Canada.

The European Union advised member countries to caution against travel to both the Mexico and United States but has issued no such warning against Canada.

"These travel restrictions won't do anything to stop the spread of this virus," Friesen said.

The World Health Organization urged nations to avoid restrictions on international travel.

It's still premature to tell how swine flu will effect tourism in North American, said Wilde, but he pointed out that things here in Victoria could be a lot worse.

"We can be thankful we're not hoteliers in Mexico."
