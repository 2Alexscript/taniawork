As health experts scramble to understand the implications of a swine flu outbreak rippling across the globe from Mexico, one word is on many lips: pandemic.

Some might think it's an overreaction, but given that 149 deaths have been linked to the new strain of flu in Mexico, and there are confirmed cases in Canada, the U.S. and Europe, it's prudent to consider a "worst-case" scenario.

"We are preparing as if this is a pandemic. However, it may fade out. We're not sure," Saskatchewan's chief medical health officer, Dr. Moira

McKinnon, said Monday as she announced three suspected swine flu cases in the province.

Adding to the growing concern, The World Health Organization (WHO) raised its pandemic alert status to 4 (on a scale of 1-6), which "indicates a significant increase in risk of a pandemic, but does not necessarily mean that a pandemic is a forgone conclusion."

If human-to-human transmission is confirmed in at least two countries, the threat level would move to 5, indicating a pandemic was "imminent".

Statistically, the world is "due" a pandemic. They occur at least twice each century. In the 20th century there were three:

- 1918-19: Spanish flu killed an estimated 50 million.

- 1957-58: Asian flu claimed up to two million lives.

- 1968-69: Hong Kong flu killed about 700,000 people.

The world has had 40 years to prepare for the next pandemic, and the WHO says almost 150 countries -- including Canada -- have drawn up contingency plans. Anti-viral drugs that can mitigate the effects of a new flu strain have been stockpiled, though it's estimated it would take up to three months to produce a vaccine to prevent illness. The U.S. Centers for Disease Control is already working on a swine flu vaccine.

In addition to its planning, Canada's experience with the 2003 SARS virus, which killed 44 people and sickened hundreds, has given the health system practical experience that could prove useful if there is a pandemic.

The dark side of this picture is the fact that a global pandemic would hit the most vulnerable hardest -- in the Third World. In Africa, for example, there are generally poor medical services and a shortage of laboratory testing facilities.

While health concerns must take priority, the economic impact of a pandemic on a shaky global economy can't be ignored. Millions would be unable to work and travel would be curtailed.

There's no need for panic. But there is need for vigilance and preparation.

Ominous similarities exist between the swine flu and the 1918 pandemic virus that killed millions around the globe, from a shared "death profile" of 20-to-40-year-olds to an ability to spread rapidly among humans, experts say.

Some warn the swine flu outbreak has all the hallmarks of a pandemic. Others say it may be no worse than normal flu.

The confirmed death toll in Mexico is 20, with a likely total of 149 deaths, according to a national health ministry spokesman. There are now 40 confirmed cases in the United States, all mild, in five states. Six "mild" cases have been reported in Canada but that number could rise as officials await test results from several provinces.

No one can predict whether the swine flu is capable of morphing into a strain capable of unleashing a severe global pandemic.

"The bottom line is, we really don't know," says Dr. Brian Ward, an expert on viruses, vaccines and pandemics at McGill University in Montreal.

Experts don't know because important numbers are still missing. No one knows how prevalent the illness is. There could be millions of infected people in Mexico, with only the most severe cases ending up in a clinic or hospital, meaning what we're seeing is only the severe end of the spectrum.

"If it's 80 deaths on a background of 2,000 cases, that's really, really bad," says Ward, associate director of The Research Institute of McGill University Health Centre. "If it's 80 deaths and 1,300 hospitalized on a background of 10 million, it's still perhaps worse than the seasonal flu and a funny pattern. But it's not the same kind of hair-raising scenarios that the former numbers would lead you to believe."

Health authorities have been fearing an outbreak of H5N1 bird flu for years, and the only reason they haven't pressed the panic button is that it has never shown the ability to pass easily from human to human, Ward says.

The virus in Mexico, and now other places, "clearly has that capacity," meaning it has much greater pandemic potential than bird flu.

Swine flu normally spreads from pig to pig. Occasionally it will spread from pig to human. "This particular version has mutated so that it's able to transmit from person to person," says James Scott, a professor at the University of Toronto's Dalla Lana School of Public Health and an expert in the airborne movement of germs.

"If you always need proximity to a pig in order to catch the disease, I'd feel pretty safe in downtown Toronto because we don't have a lot of pigs. But if it starts transmitting from human to human, I get a little more worried."

Scott says the virus is the same type as the 1918 strain.

What is it?

It's a respiratory disease pigs get caused by type A influenza viruses. Humans are rarely infected.

How do you get it?

Through contact with an infected person or pig. It's unknown how easily it spreads person-to-person, but it's mainly through coughing and sneezing. People can also touch something with the virus on it and then touch their noses or mouths. You cannot get it from eating pork. It's not spread through food. It's transmitted through close contact with infected pigs or their environments such as barns or fairs.

What are the symptoms?

It's similar to influenza and includes fever, chills, cough, sore throat, body ache, headache, fatigue, lethargy and lack of appetite. Sometimes it also causes diarrhea, vomiting, nausea and a runny nose.

Who is at risk?

Reports suggest it is infecting previously healthy young adults. That is unusual compared to normal human flu which puts the elderly and very young children at highest risk.

Can you treat it?

Swine flu ranges from mild illness to severe and even deadly. Prescription antiviral drugs are used to treat it, particularly oseltamivir, more commonly known as Tamiflu, or zanamivir, also known as Relenza. It's best to start taking these within two days of getting symptoms.

Is there a vaccine?

There is no vaccine for humans, only pigs. The seasonal flu shot is believed to provide at least some protection.

How long can an infected person spread it?

Adults are contagious for up to seven days after the onset of illness and for the entire time they have symptoms. Children can be contagious for 10 days or longer.

How is it prevented?

The best protection is washing your hands often with soap and water or alcohol-based hand cleaners. Keep about one metre away from people and avoid close contact with anyone who is sick. Avoid touching your eyes, nose or mouth. Live healthy by getting plenty of sleep and exercise. Avoid stress. Drink plenty of fluids and eat nutritiously. If you are sick, cover your cough and stay home.

When should you go to the doctor?

Seek medical attention if you have visited Mexico, California or Texas in the last two weeks and are having any respiratory symptoms.

Facts about swine flu

What is swine flu?

Swine flu is a respiratory illness caused by a type A influenza virus, usually of the subtype H1N1, that triggers outbreaks in pigs fairly regularly. It can also transfer from pigs to humans, from humans to pigs and from humans to humans.

Four main virus subtypes -- H1N1, H1N2, H3N2 and H3N1 -- have been isolated in pigs, though the virus, like all influenza viruses, constantly mutates. When influenza viruses from different species infect pigs, the viruses can trade genes to create new versions that mix swine, human and/or avian influenza.

The swine H1N1 virus is not the same as the human H1N1 virus, so vaccines for the latter won't protect from the former.

All three come from animals, but SARS, a corona virus, and the avian flu are poorly adapted to infecting humans. As a result, it is fairly difficult for humans to catch either virus or pass them on to others, though the effects can prove deadly when infection occurs.

What is swine flu?

Today, medical researchers know the virus has nothing to do with the Big Dipper.

It's typically most brutal on the elderly and infants.

TORONTO

There's no denying it: The reputation of the flu shot has taken a bit of a beating over the past year.

So as flu season approaches and flu clinics open their doors, you may be wondering whether getting a shot is worth the effort, momentary discomfort and expense, if you are one of those folks for whom the vaccine isn't provided for free.

After all, two of the three strains in last year's vaccine did not offer terrific protection against the viruses that were actually making people sick. And a heated scientific debate over methods used to calculate how many seniors' lives are saved by flu shots seems to be coming to the conclusion that their life-saving effect in that group has been somewhat overestimated.

With the media attention these issues have received, some influenza experts suspect members of the public may be harbouring doubts about the value of rolling up a sleeve for a flu shot.

"Yeah, absolutely. I think that's true,'' says Dr. David Shay, a vaccine expert in the influenza division of the U.S. Centers for Disease Control in Atlanta, Ga.

Shay and other public health authorities still insist that flu shots are an important contribution to the fight against influenza, which makes millions of people seriously ill and is thought to kill between 250,000 and 500,000 people around the globe every year.

"The bottom line, I think, is that this vaccine is still the best protection that we have against circulating (flu) virus,'' says Dr. Danuta Skowronski, an influenza expert with the British Columbia Centre for Disease Control in Vancouver.

But no one in public health would argue that flu shots throw up an impermeable shield. The combination of some challenging features of influenza's makeup, plus the state of the science of flu shot production means some years the vaccine performs better than others.

And basic biological facts -- the immune system takes awhile to build up in early childhood, wanes as we head into later life and is weakened by conditions like HIV and heart disease -- mean that flu shots offer the least protection to those typically hit hardest by influenza.

"It's clear that (flu) vaccines do not work optimally under all conditions and in all age groups. This is just incontrovertible,'' says Dr. Keiji Fukuda, head of the World Health Organization's global influenza program.

"And I think that one of the big scientific challenges for influenza . . . is how do you take things like vaccines and make them a lot more effective for the groups of people that are most vulnerable? I think it's clear that we don't have vaccines right now that are optimal for the elderly. We don't have vaccines which are optimal for children less than six months.''

Research is underway in laboratories around the world aimed at improving flu vaccines.

The holy grail of this work is a flu shot that could protect against all subtypes of influenza. If that goal is reached, flu shots might be among the vaccines kids get in early childhood, offering years or even a lifetime of protection. But such a flu shot is not imminent.

For now we have vaccines the experts acknowledge don't work optimally. But there's a lot of ground between optimal and useless and most of the time flu vaccine is closer to the former's point on the spectrum. The U.S. Centers for Disease Control says that in 16 of the last 20 flu seasons, the strains in the vaccine have been well matched to those causing disease.

So if you get a shot and if you have a strong immune system and if the strains in the shot are well matched to the viruses causing disease, most years you will likely evade influenza's grasp. And even if a flu shot doesn't prevent you from catching the flu, the antibodies generated could make the difference between a prolonged bout of bed rest and a couple of days off work.

"At the end of the day what we're really trying to do is make sure people get harmed as little as possible,'' Fukuda says. "That's definitely trying to prevent deaths. But it's also trying to keep people out of hospitals, it's trying to keep people from developing serious complications.''

Protecting people against the flu is a complex task. And expecting that the same shot will offer equal and optimal protection to people of varying ages and health status in Fort McMurray, Fort Worth and Frankfurt in any given winter is asking a lot of one vaccine.

The problem is there isn't one static target, one type of flu virus that the vaccine can teach your immune system to be on guard against.

In fact, the vaccine is three shots in one, protecting against one influenza B virus and two influenza A viruses (of the H1N1 and H3N2 subtypes) that were predicted -- months earlier -- to be the major causes of disease for that flu season.

Sometimes those predictions aren't entirely on the money. Because it takes roughly six months to make, bottle and distribute flu vaccine, scientists have to choose next year's flu shot strains before this year's flu season is over. The strains covered in the 2008-09 season's vaccine were selected at a World Health Organization meeting last February, based on transmission patterns then.

But flu viruses mutate constantly, so while the vaccine was being made, the target kept moving. These constant changes explain why we can catch the flu multiple times in our lives. But they also can undermine the effectiveness of the vaccine. The more the viruses causing disease evolve away from those in the vaccine, the less protection the vaccine can offer.

In addition to being ever-changing, the spread of flu viruses is pretty unpredictable. So the viruses that are wreaking havoc in Ottawa in the third week of January may not be the same as those causing misery in Oklahoma City. But the same flu shot is being asked to protect folks in both locations and many more.

The regional variability in viruses -- some of which are better matched to the vaccine than others -- explains why some years some parts of the country or the continent or the hemisphere will be walloped by flu, while other communities will experience less disease.

Towards the end of last flu season parts of the U.S. were being hit by H3N2 viruses (moderately matched to the vaccine strain) while many parts of Canada were being attacked by influenza B viruses.

Because the B strain in the 2007-08 vaccine was a poor match for the circulating viruses, lots of Canadians fell ill at the end of last winter and in the early spring. But earlier in the flu season when H1N1 viruses were making the rounds in both countries, people who had received a flu shot had good protection; the H1N1 component of last year's flu shot was a good match for circulating viruses.

It just goes to show that the flu shot is trying to help people who get it dodge three bullets. Depending on the flu season and which viruses are circulating where you live, you could be in the line of fire for one, two, or all three over the course of the flu season.

"I think people need to know that it's very context-dependent and you can't predict that context going into the (flu) season,'' Skowronski says.

"The bottom line is, though, that you're going to get protection. . . . Even if you have suboptimal protection to one component, to another component it may be quite excellent.''
