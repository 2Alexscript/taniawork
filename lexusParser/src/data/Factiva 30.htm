The giant lineups and frustration that dominated the first week of the biggest immunization program in history is expected to get worse next week, as health officials concede that public demand is exceeding the number of doses and the staff available to administer the vaccine.

Provinces reported clinic shutdowns Friday and federal officials painted a gloomy outlook for the week ahead because they have received only one-fifth the amount of vaccine that they had expected to distribute nationwide.

The manufacturer, GlaxoSmithKline, has fallen behind because it is making a special vaccine for pregnant women, Chief Public Health Officer Dr. David Butler-Jones told a news conference.

Word of the production slowdown came as the World Health Organization reversed its earlier position and said Friday there is now no reason for pregnant women to favour the special version being made exclusively for them.

GlaxoSmithKline has switched its one production line back to making the regular vaccine, but it will take some time to ramp up to the target of producing three million doses per week.

Butler Jones said that officials learned Thursday they will have only 400,000 new doses to ship out next week and another 225,000 doses reserved for pregnant women -- far short of the six million that have been distributed thus far.

Butler-Jones, who had maintained until the last few days that there would be plenty of vaccine for everyone, cautioned that the H1N1 campaign is being delivered in "real time" so there are many unprecedented challenges that were not anticipated.

"We're producing it, testing it, distributing it, giving it to people all at the same time," he said.

"It is something that nobody has ever actually had to deal with before in this kind of context. It's the largest immunization campaign in history."

The goal, he said, is to have Canadians who want the H1N1 shot immunized by Christmas.

His appeal for patience was little comfort in many quarters, where Canadians were turned away by the hundreds as clinics ran out of time and supply on Friday and provinces scrambled to revamp their strategies.

Ontario's chief medical officer said plans to open up clinics to the general public next week have been put on hold and that the province will continue next week to administer shots only to at-risk residents -- children under six, pregnant women, seniors, health workers and people with chronic conditions. He speculated that because the federal government received its vaccination supply earlier than anticipated, the provinces were perhaps "caught off-guard" in terms of how to distribute it.

At the same time, the death of two Ontario children created a mass panic that prompted Canadians to turn up in clinics in droves -- including those who are not considered to be in high-risk groups.

"It obviously alarmed a lot of people and contributed to demand for the vaccine that otherwise might not have occurred."

Kumanan suggested that the biggest single road block has been the failure to administer the vaccine in schools.

Alberta health officials also confirmed another H1N1-related death, bringing the total reported number of Canadians who have succumbed to the illness to 95.

Calgary started out this week with four H1N1 vaccination clinics to serve a population of more than one million people, with wait times stretching to four and five hours. A fifth clinic was later added at the Olympic Oval for seniors, small children and pregnant women, all of whom might have trouble standing for such long waiting periods.

That's still not enough, and Calgarians have every right to know why receiving a flu shot in this city is as time-consuming as lining up for the last few loaves of bread at a Soviet-era grocery store. Winnipeg has 12 clinics serving a population of 650,000 and Heidi Graham, spokesperson for the Winnipeg Regional Health Authority, said that "the longest (wait)we've heard about . . . has been 90 minutes. Where there is a wait, it's usually about an hour."

Something is wrong with this picture, and it's up to Alberta Health Services to bring it into much sharper focus.

Health Minister Ron Liepert's feeble excuses just don't wash. Liepert said the government was caught off guard by the number of people who flocked to the vaccination clinics. But why should there be any element of unpreparedness?The government has been urging everyone to get a shot, yet it professes to be surprised when a fraction of those people do just what the government told them to.

Liepert also deflected blame for the lineups to the vaccine's early delivery coupled with problems putting medical staff in place and training them. But why weren't staffing logistics and training dealt with a long time ago? H1N1 did not suddenly appear; provincial officials knew months ago that the second wave was expected to arrive this fall. Nor did the Manitoba government seem to have any problem, either with vaccine delivery or with staffing and training personnel for the 12 Winnipeg clinics.

If Calgarians stay away in droves from the vaccination clinics because of their unwillingness to spend half a day standing in a lineup, the government will have only itself to blame. This organized chaos can only be the direct result of inexcusably poor planning. Manitoba got it right, and Alberta had the same opportunity. Liepert has said, "We're going to work toward making sure that we take corrective action." That should be unnecessary, Mr. Minister. Fine-tuning--such as making flu response units staffed by paramedics available for house calls to those Calgarians uncertain about their condition-- is acceptable;a major course correction is not.

It's also unacceptable that the lineups are being further clogged by people who were specifically asked to wait for their shots until those in high-risk groups--including those under 65 with chronic conditions and pregnant women --receive theirs in the early days of the vaccination program. Those who are not at high risk should wait until the vaccine is made available through doctors' offices and select pharmacists.

Vaccination clinics will open next week on college and university campuses, and clinics to assess people with flu symptoms will also open. In the end, it can only be hoped that the government's mismanagement does not have the effect of helping the pandemic to spread by inducing people to stay away from vaccination clinics, and that health officials take to heart the lessons learned so they can get it right next time.
