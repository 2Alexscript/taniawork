Fighting The Flu

Today: Is Alberta ready?

Alberta is preparing a wide-ranging pandemic plan that could see elective surgeries postponed, and retired doctors and nurses called in for help should the H1N1 influenza outbreak worsen and sicken large numbers of people.

Top provincial health officials say they are also prepared to create triage centres outside of hospitals, possibly in tents or large medical clinics, to take pressure off emergency rooms if there is a significant surge of H1N1 cases.

As Alberta braces for the return of the so-called swine flu virus this fall, officials are planning for the health system to face a dual challenge: demand for medical services will grow at the same time staff are at risk of becoming ill themselves.

The province's plan anticipates nurses and doctors will be off sick at the same rate as the general population--between five per cent and 20 per cent of workers may need to remain at home, depending on the severity of the outbreak.

The situation could force hospitals to scale back in nonessential areas to free up staff and resources to handle H1N1 cases. For example, hospitals could delay elective surgeries and clinics for chronic problems, such as high blood pressure.

"When we get a pandemic and we have lots of people sick, the health system won't necessarily work the way it usually does," Dr. Gerry Predy, senior medical officer of health for the province's superboard, said in an interview with the Herald.

"There will be some different ways of doing business, because we'll have more sick people and potentially fewer staff."

Predy and other public health officials say Alberta is ready for H1N1, which has infected at least 1,648 people in this province, to return this fall.

But those who have been affected by the virus question how the medical system--already short-staffed and strapped for space--will cope if the situation worsened significantly.

"The hospitals are really, really busy," said Stella Peeace, a longtime friend of Calgarian Vanessa Bluebell, a young mother of four who died in July after contracting the virus.

"There are already three-or four-hour( emergency)waits. I don't know what they'll do."

Indeed, experts say many health facilities in Calgary operate at 99 per cent occupancy during normal times. If H1N1 became a more serious pandemic, they say health officials will face some tough choices.

"There could be a series of quite unpleasant and difficult decisions . . . about who gets into hospital and who doesn't," said Dr. Jim Dickinson, a University of Calgary flu expert.

Debate about Alberta's readiness for a second wave of H1N1 comes as Canada has confirmed more than 10,000 cases of the virus, which has been associated with 66 deaths in the country.

H1N1, which first appeared in Mexico this spring and has since been declared a global pandemic, has so far been behaving similar to typical seasonal influenzas.

But physicians anticipate H1N1 will infect greater numbers of people than regular influenza because it's a new virus and the population has little immunity against it.

Alberta's chief medical officer of health said the province is preparing for worst-case scenarios, but it's hoping to prevent a significant increase in cases.

Dr. Andre Corriveau said Alberta Health is anticipating government will have enough doses of H1N1 vaccine for every Albertan who wants one by November.

Alberta Health Services is still working on plans for administering the shots, but it's expected the superboard will hold mass vaccination clinics.

"Most of our focus is going to be minimizing the probability (a worse-case) situation occurs," says Corriveau. "The ultimate strategy is making sure we have the vaccine and we're able to deliver it."

But if the second wave of H1N1 is more severe, Alberta's plan calls for reduced services in some areas so staff can be redeployed to care for H1N1 patients.

Alberta Health Services is developing training programs so nurses and other health workers can quickly learn a job if they are moved to a new unit.

Regulatory bodies for physicians and registered nurses, meanwhile, say they will consider allowing recent retirees or students who are nearing the end of their studies to assist, although they may have restrictions on how they can practise.

The Alberta Medical Association said that, in a worse-case scenario, generalist physicians like family doctors may be asked to work in hospitals.

The pandemic plan also calls for officials to open flu assessment and triage centres out of hospital if large numbers of patients become ill.

Predy said Edmonton has a triage tent that could be set up for this purpose and he said large medical clinics have come forward in other parts of the province to house the service.

In addition to health system planning, City of Calgary officials say they are well prepared for the fall.

The city has nearly$2 million worth of equipment such as surgical gloves, goggles and masks.

City officials from 31 municipal departments and agencies are also meeting regularly to devise a plan that will keep the city running even if the H1N1 virus spreads through the ranks of its 14,000 employees.

"We need to ensure that the essential components of these businesses are maintained, even if we have a 30 to 40 per cent absence," said Tom Sampson, deputy chief of the city's Emergency Management Agency.

As in health care, some departments will scale back if necessary and redeploy workers to where they are needed.

Pork farmers and one of the nation's biggest newsrooms, the CBC, just can't see eye to eye on what to call the H1N1 flu, popularly (but erroneously, say some farmers) dubbed swine flu.

So now, the corporation's ombudsman is being asked to settle the matter. On the farming side, the charge is being led by Gordon Sloan. He's a semi-retired provincial employee who lives in Guelph and has agricultural roots and farm relatives near Chatham. Sloan is attempting something that's eluded others - namely, getting farmer-friendly terminology accepted at the highest media level, in hopes other scribes will follow.

There's a lot at stake.

Most Canadians get health-related information from the media. If readers, listeners and viewers are constantly subjected to the term swine flu, it's likely to take hold in their psyche. And it has. Sloan says that's led to unjust bias against pork. In fact, he (and many others in agriculture) maintain the term swine flu is contributing significantly to the depressed pork market.

Farmers who raise pigs haven't made any money in at least two years, and Sloan says the prospects are dismal as long as the swine flu moniker, not to mention the affliction itself, lingers in the minds and bodies of Canadians. Sloan argues that because the World Health Organization adopted the term H1N1, media should do the same.

When the disease first appeared, confusion reigned supreme. Popular terminology was up in the air.

You may remember the search for an appropriate name even led to the possibility of calling it Mexican flu. But the World Health Organization is struggling to manage media activity on this disease, all the way from its source to its treatment.

For example, on its website, the organization says it knows some media have expressed concern about the flu vaccine safety. "The public needs to be reassured that regulatory procedures in place for the licensing of pandemic vaccines, including procedures for expediting regulatory approval, are rigorous and do not compromise safety or quality controls, it says. Gee. Doesn't that sound like "Trust me, I'm from the government?"

The media is supposed to be independent of the government, so the CBC and others are forging ahead.

Sloan sent me an email to him from Esther Enkin, executive editor of CBC News, in which she acknowledged the industry's many challenges. She says the CBC "would not deliberately set out to make it worse." But, she says, the pork industry's woes run deep. She cites soaring feed costs, the recession, new U.S. labelling regulations, currency fluctuations, changing consumption habits and import restrictions imposed after the flu outbreak, all having contributed to reduced demand for pork.

"It's not at all clear that the term ‘swine flu' has had a significant, or any, effect on what appears to be a longer-term trend resulting from substantial and quantifiable causes," she adds. I don't buy that.

There is nothing neutral about the term swine flu. If the country's major media outlet associates any emerging disease with a specific commodity, see what happens to sales. But in the news business, do you stop using a term because it injures? No. In theory, you are not beholden to anyone. You only stop it when the term is wrong.

The CBC's Enkin claims the term swine flu is accurate and clear, but acknowledges the disease does indeed contain some genetic elements of bird flu and human flu, too. Sloan disagrees with Enkin's interpretation of what's right, and has taken the matter to the CBC ombudsman.

At best, he says, the CBC's policy is undermining the reputation of the pork industry...and at worst, it may be the proverbial straw that takes the industry down.

No one doubts Canada could be a competitive player on international markets. But can hog farmers survive long enough to prove it?

"We've gone through over three years of a down cycle that has just brought this industry to its knees," said Curtis Littlejohn, a farmer southeast of Ayr and one of two Ontario representatives on the Canadian Pork Council. "The government is trying to control the crash."

You name it, hog farmers have encountered it: Soaring feed prices. A rising dollar that has crippled exports. New country-of-origin labelling rules in the U.S.

The arrival of the H1N1 virus and its initial "swine flu" moniker "was the straw that broke the camel's back," New Dundee-area farmer Stewart Cressman said.

"This year has been extremely discouraging," he said. "People are taking a long, hard look in the mirror and asking ‘Is this where my future lies?'"

Laurent Pellerin, president of the Canadian Federation of Agriculture, said hogs are now going for less than $1 a head when they are worth about $200 each.

Now, the federal government has stepped forward with a three-part plan that leaves farmers to make some tough decisions.

A $75 million transition program will help farmers leave the industry, in an effort to reduce a significant oversupply issue in Canada.

Long-term loans with government-backed credit will be available for those with viable operations who want to remain in business. The value of the loan program wasn't disclosed.

A $17 million marketing fund will promote pork products and try to recover lost markets and build new ones, particularly at the domestic level.

"We have to face the reality that some producers will leave the industry and we need to reduce our current over-supply," Agriculture Minister Gerry Ritz said on Saturday.

"Producers who weather this global economic storm will have to restructure their businesses to adjust to new and emerging market realities."

Ritz couldn't say how many farmers will leave the industry under the buyout or how much compensation individuals will get.

The restructuring plan was announced at press events in Winnipeg, Quebec, and at Clare Schlegel's farm near Shakespeare.

"It's a positive step in the right direction," Schlegel said. "Canada and southwestern Ontario have all the right resources to be a world player."

But he admitted it is a critical time.

If too many farmers throw in the towel in the short term, who will be left to rebuild?

"When the investment leaves, it doesn't come back overnight," he said.

"Canada risks losing the entire business."

Bob Hunsberger, chair of Breslau-based Progressive Pork Producers Co-operative Inc., said the federal funds will provide some incentive for things to change.

"But it's certainly not making up for the losses that hog farmers have incurred in the last couple of years."

And it's a far cry from the $800 million that the Canadian Pork Council was appealing for – and that was before the H1N1 virus reared its head.

Littlejohn said the government must act to ensure adequate funds are available within a matter of weeks.

"This industry cannot wait until October for cash," he said.

On his diversified farm near New Dundee, which includes beef cattle, Cressman's hog barns have sat empty since January, in an economic environment when raising pigs was "at best a break-even proposition."

Even so, he still thinks things may be "bleakest before the dawn.

"I have faith that we will eventually have a viable and very robust industry here," Cressman said.

OTTAWA -- You know those pesky political flyers that land on your doorstep?

The ones that we pay for as taxpayers so MPs can speak to us about what is going on in government but which political parties have started using as partisan tools to attack each other?

Get ready for them to go live.

The Winnipeg new media design firm, Mars Hill Group, has landed a contract with the federal Conservatives to "deliver their message to supporters nation-wide."

The company's website says they will be helping individual Conservative MPs and Senators make personal, customized videos. The first one will star former CTV host turned Conservative Senator Mike Duffy and will "start hitting inboxes early this fall."

Now don't worry. These aren't going to be paid for by John Q. Canadian. There is nothing in the parliamentary allowance to pay for video messaging. At least not yet.

But maybe they'll help the fledgling video creations already out there by MPs.

Like Manitoba's own James Bezan.

Bezan, the Conservative MP from Selkirk-Interlake, has launched his own YouTube channel and has three episodes so far.

The introductory video comes complete with Bezan riding up on his horse, Woody, and has him delivering an afternoon-nap inducing statistical profile of his riding.

But Bezan should get credit for trying. Politicians can't rely on a basic website to reach voters anymore.

They need to go all Barack Obama and hit computer screens and blackberries everywhere with tweets and Facebook messages and YouTube.

So Bezan might be onto something if he can remember the camera company is called "Kodak" not "Kodiak", convince Woody to stop trying to head-butt him out of the shot, and get some editing assistance from Mars Hill so he is no longer heard in the videos telling people when to start speaking.

It's been four years since Canada and the United States announced at the 11th hour of the opening of the Devils Lake outlet that they'd come to an agreement to have the U.S. build a permanent filter on the outlet.

In exchange Manitoba agreed to do something about the road along the U.S. border between Manitoba and North Dakota that acted like a dike and kept farm fields on the U.S. side sodden in the spring while the Manitoba side was high and dry.

The agreement has gone nowhere.

There is no filter on the outlet beyond the temporary gravel filter that was put in as a stop-gap measure. Manitoba said it won't do anything to the road until North Dakota and the U.S. live up to their promises.

Legislators from Midwestern states and Canada's prairie provinces are trying to light a fire under everyone yet again.

At the 64th annual meeting of the Midwestern Legislative Conference in Kansas last week they passed a resolution asking both sides to live up their agreements.

Winnipeg NDP MP Jim Maloway, who attended the event, said he was surprised the resolution passed. Maloway worked on the resolution but couldn't introduce it himself because he's not from the U.S. But two North Dakota state legislators agreed and the resolution went through by a vote of 41 to 28.

It's not overwhelming support since there are over 1,500 legislators in the conference and about one-third of them attended the event. And Maloway said the vote took place on the last day when everyone was packing to go home.

Either way, the resolution will go to the White House, Ottawa and the provincial and state governments and maybe this thing can finally get resolved once and for all.

With Manitoba first nations being ground zero of this flu's impact on reserves it is perhaps fitting that we had so many seats around that table.

But Manitoba Premier Gary Doer made an appearance twice and he wasn't anywhere near the nation's capital.

Doer -- who has always seemed to pride himself on coming up with the sound bite du jour for us short-attention span media types -- was quoted during the meeting by both Wasylcyia-Leis and Dr. John Maxted, from the College of Family Physicians of Canada.

His quote?

Comparing the H1N1 influenza to a "truck coming around the corner."

It wasn't Doer who had the quote of the day however.

That went to another Manitoban: Wasylycia-Leis, who fresh off an interview with Toronto's Citytv about federal stimulus cash for tourism being used to help bring former U.S. President Bill Clinton to a Toronto speaking engagement.

When she was criticizing the government for forcing the provinces to cover 40 per cent of the cost of H1N1 vaccine Wasylycia-Leis couldn't help but bring it up.

"You have money through this stimulus package to bring in Bill Clinton," she said. "Why don't you have money for the vaccine."

Some producers used stronger language yesterday, calling the package a “betrayal” of an industry that Ottawa and the provinces were encouraging farmers to enter only a few short years ago.

The federal package is a three-part plan: launch a $17-million marketing venture to sell Canadian pork internationally; offer government-backed loans to farmers with viable hog operations; and set aside $75-million to buy the worst-off farmers out of the business.

“We have to face the reality that some producers will leave the industry and we need to reduce our current over-supply,” Mr. Ritz said. “Producers who weather this global economic storm will have to restructure their businesses to adjust to new and emerging market realities.”

Trade restrictions place the federal government in a difficult position. By offering too much financial support, it risks igniting a challenge from American farmers arguing that a bailout amounts to subsidization. Too meagre a package, and it risks alienating an industry.

While appreciating that delicate balance, producers had been anticipating a larger bailout, closer in size to an $800-million proposal that the Canadian Pork Council submitted last month.

“We were hoping for considerably more,” said George Matheson, another Manitoba pig farmer. “There's definitely some disappointment out there.”

There's also confusion. Mr. Ritz couldn't elaborate Saturday on how many farmers would be bought out or how much cash they would receive.

“I really don't know how they plan to calculate this,” said Mr. Matheson, who, like other farmers, is currently losing about $40 on every pig he sells.

New country-of-origin labelling laws in the U.S., a strengthening Canadian dollar, high feed costs and groundless worldwide concerns that the H1N1 virus is spread through pork have combined to weaken prices and highlight a large glut in the North American market.

The apparent oversupply comes a decade after federal and provincial governments were encouraging farmer to invest in hog barns. Fifteen years ago, Canada produced 16 million pigs, a number that has since doubled.

“The feds felt they had all the trade agreements in place, so they offered technical expertise and loans,” said Ian Wishart, president of Keystone Agricultural Producers, a farmers' lobby group in Manitoba. “There were a lot of government staff saying that this was a good industry to get into. Ms. Thomas would like to see it made available (and affordable) to more people, with an expanded bandwidth.

“If we can't make it work in Grise Fiord, we can't make it work in Nunavut,” she says. “What options do you have for economic opportunities without the Internet? What options do you have for post-secondary education?”

“There is always the concern that if you do build a complete road system you will be impacting on the ecological system,” he says. “You have to balance: Do you keep it preserved as it is? … [But] most of the people I talk to talk about having some form of connection to Southern Canada.”

“A lot of the people that are living on social assistance right now would be able to work. … The young people that are graduating now, right now there's really nothing for them at the end of the tunnel. And this would be an opportunity for them.”

After completing my first year on campus, I've probably experienced just about every type of professor, in terms of how they use technology. This includes one who offers his lecture slides online, and another who podcasts all of her lectures, making them available throughout the term. The campus classroom has undergone a major high-tech overhaul. Professors no longer just lecture in the classroom - most now offer a full-blown media buffet. Universities appear to be adapting to this generation's multi-media craze, helping to turn possible distractions into grade boosters.

Podcasts

For those who aren't tech savvy, podcasts are downloadable audio and/or video files. Translation? Professors can record their lectures and then post them online. Sleeping in late is no longer a disaster. Just listen to a podcast of the exact lecture you missed. They make great study tools, since you can listen to a podcast whenever and wherever you want, however many times you need. Plus they're pause-able. If you want to jot down a valuable nugget in your notes, you won't miss a thing.

Clickers

This gadget is quickly gaining popularity in university classrooms, spreading to lecture halls everywhere. They resemble a mini T.V. remote and are used in class to answer questions about the material being presented. The professor will ask a multiple choice question, then students, each armed with their own clicker, can press a button on the clicker corresponding to their chosen answer. With the click of a button, Clicker questions show students what they know, what they don't know, and what they think they know but really had no clue about.

YouTube and Facebook

Some professors will use every type of media to help make concepts really stick. My health studies professor gave links to YouTube clips of movies like Pulp Fiction during the drugs and addictions module of the course. No one from that class will ever forget the symptoms of an overdose. Another professor started a Facebook group to discuss key points of the course that students were having trouble with.

Online notes

Key points, even entire lectures, can be outlined for the class on Power Point presentations or overhead slides. A lot of professors go a step further and have these notes posted online, available for students to print off and add to their notes. Almost every professor I had last year used this technique. If the professor has posted their slides before the lecture, students can print them off and bring them to class, using them as starter notes that can be added to.

What's really fabulous about this lecture extra is that you now have the freedom to sit back and actually absorb the lecture, instead of furiously scribbling every utterance that comes out of the prof's mouth. No more moments of pure frustration when you realize you've missed the last 20 minutes of the lecture while trying to copy down the overhead slide.

Websites

Textbooks will often have a website to delve further into subjects discussed in the text, and often provide practice tests to check your knowledge. Some professors are now starting to have tests and quizzes online using these sites. My genetics professor last year had ten optional bonus assignments to be completed online. If you finished them all, you received a 5-per-cent bonus at the end of the term.

Other aspects

Technology has a huge impact on the way university students learn today. And the reach of technology on campus doesn't stop in the lecture halls. It's now reaching into all areas of university. You can now enroll and drop courses online, pay your tuition, buy your books, fill out forms, submit essays, and even take tests online.

What will the future bring five, even 10, years from now? Who knows. But this fall, when the H1N1 virus hits full-force, some of these high-tech tools could give students the choice to complete lectures at home, far away from crowded lecture halls. The most important role of podcasts and online notes might be yet to come.

Re: Masks, handwashing slow spread of flu, Aug. 4.

What is so interesting to me about this news article on handwashing and facemasks slowing the spread of the H1N1 flu is that the lessons of basic hygiene are "in" again for the public to protect themselves. The basic lesson of washing hands with soap is being taught and retaught around the world.

Washing hands with soap isn't just important to stop the spread of H1N1, it's also the most cost-effective intervention that could save children's lives.

Diarrheal diseases and pneumonia kill almost 3.5 million children every year. According to UNICEF, handwashing could cut those numbers by 30 to 44 per cent. Yet the Canada International Development Agency (CIDA) spends very little on sanitation issues like personal handwashing -- an estimated 10 per cent of $70 million annually spent on broader water and sanitation issues.

In March, Results Canada, the organization I work for, took three Canadian MPs to Bangladesh to see how people in many rural villages and small communities are using simple, cost-effective solutions in the fight against tuberculosis. We saw how Canadian and international funds were helping people.

Our visit happened just before the monsoons when hospitals and communities were bracing for what comes with the rains -- diarrhea season.

I saw community health volunteers and doctors in hospitals getting ready for it, engaging in educating people and repeating the basic messages about how important handwashing is to stop the spread of diseases like pneumonia, to prevent diarrhea, reduce malnutrition and save lives.

Back home, at my workplace, handwashing signs have gone up recently. Antibacterial sanitizer pumps are now everywhere in offices and public places, not just hospitals and doctors' offices.

Because basic handwashing is such an important tool in fighting the No. 1 killer of kids and infectious diseases like H1N1, let's hope CIDA's spending on basic sanitation and hygiene goes up as quick as those handwashing signs and sanitizer pumps have.

GENEVA

The influenza A (H1N1) pandemic is now unstoppable. The world missed its chance, and containment is no longer a possibility. Efforts to contain the outbreak were impeded by the consistent downplaying of the extent and immediacy of the threat. Those influential "voices of reason'' that restrained the World Health Organization from issuing stronger, earlier warnings also lulled governments into complacency.

Yet even as the virus spreads to every corner of the world and the realization of its seriousness sinks in, those same voices are dampening the sense of urgency necessary to mount an effective response.

Most of all, the threat is being obscured by the misleading statement that only a few will develop serious symptoms and die. It is true that only a small fraction of those infected are likely to die; the global fatality rate is currently 0.45 percent, or four or five in a thousand. But what happens once the number of those infected rises into the millions? The fatality rate may remain low, but the number of fatalities will no longer be few.

Just how severe is the threat? Recently, Britain's chief medical officer reported that modeling studies indicated that up to 65,000 people in that country could die. Projected globally, this worst-case estimate suggests the potential for 6.5 million deaths. Except that Britain's current fatality rate, as well as the higher rate that yielded the 65,000 figure, are both lower than the average global rate right now.

To calculate the potential fatalities worldwide, one must estimate the number of people likely to become infected (the clinical attack rate) and the percentage among them likely to die (the fatality rate). So far, the observed attack rate has been 30 per cent, meaning one in three who are exposed to the virus becomes infected. This suggests that more than two billion people are likely to be infected through successive waves of the pandemic over the next few months. Based on the observed global fatality rate of 0.45 to 0.5 per cent, between nine million and 10 million could die.

This global fatality rate, however, reflects current conditions. Some favorable factors may have kept the numbers down to date.

First, the virus initially appeared in mostly developed, Northern Hemisphere countries experiencing summer conditions. As the pandemic has spread to the Southern Hemisphere, we are seeing cases such as Argentina, where the fatality rate is five times higher, around 2.5 percent.

Second, at this stage, health systems have had to cope with relatively low numbers of patients, though in many cases they have still been overwhelmed. As the trickle turns into a flood, the capacity of these systems to identify those most at risk and to provide the best treatments may diminish. Again, the experience in the countries most affected so far shows that not only the number of fatalities but also the fatality rate increases with the number of cases.

No doubt the development of a vaccine will greatly help to reduce infections. While it will not reduce the fatality rate - a vaccine is a preventive measure, not a treatment - it should reduce fatalities. A vaccine is expected to be ready by September, after accelerated clinical trials.

But one must also remember that we will be engaged in a race to produce and distribute a vaccine before the virus mutates into a more virulent form (with a higher fatality rate) or a subtype that the vaccine is ineffective against. Influenza's high rate of mutation is the reason the vaccine against the seasonal flu has to be produced annually. By the time the second wave of the influenza A pandemic hits next year, the vaccine developed this year may be ineffective. This is why it is crucial to monitor the virus by taking regular samples in different regions.

In all these considerations, we have not even touched on the impact of medical costs, absenteeism and the disruption of economic activities in the midst of a global downturn.

To mitigate the effects of the pandemic, we must understand that influenza's threat does not necessarily result from its severity but its ease of contagion, its rapid spread and its high rate of mutation. It is crucial to accurately measure the threat. While the likely severity of the pandemic may continue to be debated, this flu strain has spread further in six weeks than previous ones have in six months.

GUADALAJARA, Mexico -- Prime Minister Stephen Harper sits down today with President Barack Obama and Mexico's leader for a meeting that will test the value to Canada of the North American summit process.

Today's discussion is the centrepiece of the fifth summit since 2005, when then-U.S. president George W. Bush kicked off the annual trilateral talks aimed at promoting economic growth and security on a continent-wide basis.

The closed-door chats give the leaders a chance to share their thinking, but whether this setting can produce results for Canada seems more unclear than ever.

The most urgent regional issue for Canada is the recent rise of protectionism in the United States. Barring Canadian suppliers from bidding on federally funded urban renewal projects in the U.S. is resulting in lost contracts - and lost jobs - in Canadian factories. And the problem is getting worse as U.S. cities and states gear up to award more contracts on projects paid for out of Obama's $787 billion (U.S.) stimulus package.

Harper and Mexican President Felipe Calderon are expected to raise concerns about the troubling Buy American phenomenon during the talks here. But for Canada, nothing concrete is expected from Obama on this problem until Harper visits Washington on Sept. 16. At that time, the Prime Minister and Obama are expected to hold serious talks on how to curb the protectionist wave sweeping the U.S.

At this meeting, Obama, who chose to hold a separate, one-on-one talk with Calderon yesterday, is focusing on issues of immense joint concern to Mexico and the United States: border security, drug trafficking and immigration.

While Canada has a stake in all these issues, trying to solve them in a North American context might not always be in Canada's interest.

For instance, underlying the U.S. protectionist issue is the widespread belief that many American policy-makers would be happy to exempt Canada. The thinking is that the Buy American war cry is not really aimed at Canada. Instead, it is said to reflect the angst of U.S. workers who have seen their jobs disappear as American companies transferred employment to low-wage jurisdictions such as China - and Mexico.

Similarly, Canada's problems with increasing U.S. security at the border bear similarities to difficulties between the U.S and Mexico. But the magnitude of the drug and immigration problems vexing Mexican-U.S. relations makes the issues on the U.S. northern border seem minuscule by comparison.

For instance, Harper yesterday trumpeted a $400,000-plus support package to help train Mexican police to better cope with the drug war plaguing Mexico. Obama, in contrast, is trying to manage a $1.4 billion package to aid Calderon's anti-drug fight.

In the past few years, these North American summits have lost credibility, particularly since many observers saw the Bush-led meetings as a cloak for promoting a right-wing business and security agenda around the continent. So, Obama's willingness to carry on with the annual North American summits may reinvigorate the process.

Noting that this will be Obama's first Three Amigos summit, Mexican Foreign Relations Minister Patricia Espinosa said recently, "We are very pleased with the dynamics of this engagement." The meeting, Espinosa said, will give momentum to efforts to solve regional problems and "give North America greater influence on world topics."

If these annual chats continue, they will certainly provide cohesion and cooperation on such issues as climate change and swine flu. But whether the summits will effectively address Canada's most pressing problems with its continental partners remains to be seen.
