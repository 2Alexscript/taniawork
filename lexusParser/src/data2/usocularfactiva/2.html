A glimmer of hope has come to the Ford household, where Mitchell F. Ford wears his sunglasses at night, even when he watches television.

The Doherty High School freshman first profiled in a January 2003 story about the degenerative eye disease choroideremia wonders whether he'll be able to drive a car when he turns 16-1/2, his father, Worcester Police Officer Francis J. Ford, says.

There is no cure or treatment for the inherited eye disease. But because ultraviolet light is known to damage the choroid and the retina, two layers of cells lining the inside of the back of that eye that are affected by choroideremia, the sunglasses stay on, particularly when Mitchell rows crew for the Worcester city high school team.

Mitchell, his father, and his mother, Detective Janis A. Ford, whose father, George "Dave" Bianculli, became blind from the disease, look to London for their optimism. Researcher Dr. Miguel Seabra, head of cellular and molecular biology at Imperial College, has succeeded after four years of work in creating a mouse model of choroideremia.

With the disease present in mice, his team of investigators can study the cellular mechanism of how vision degenerates and possibly attempt gene therapy in the laboratory animals.

"It probably doesn't sound like much to the average person, but you should have been in the room and seen all these blind people crying," Mr. Ford said about a meeting of the Choroideremia Research Foundation during which the progress was reported. "It was quite emotional."

Mitchell's parents have organized a wine- and beer-tasting fund- raiser to benefit the Springfield-based foundation from 3 to 6 p.m. June 13 at the Castle Restaurant in Leicester. Last year's event raised more than $10,000 for research. The cost is $25; for more information, call (508) 756-2494.

People fear going blind almost as much as getting cancer.

One in 10 people over 65 will go blind, and one in four over 75 will lose their eyesight.

Loss of sight is so frightening that many of us can't imagine going on without our vision.

Older people, most prone to vision-threatening diseases like age- related macular degeneration, clamor for information on how to prevent, or stop, the progress of eye disease.

At a recent symposium, scientists from The Schepens Eye Research Institute in Boston detailed developments from stem-cell transplantation to gene therapy to retinal implants for a curious crowd at a Palm Beach luncheon.

Most of these studies are long-term, and while significant and encouraging for science, they don't offer much immediate impact.

So it wasn't surprising to me when the first question asked of the Schepens scientists was regarding vitamins and supplements for vision health.

Dr. Wayne Streilein answered with one word: Ocuvite. It's a vitamin and mineral supplement, sold without a prescription, that was found in a federally funded study to slow the progression of age- related macular degeneration, which has no cure. AMD is a gradual, progressive deterioration of the eye that is the leading cause of legal blindness in older Americans.

Produced by Bausch & Lomb, Ocuvite was found effective during a 10-year clinical trial known as the Age-Related Eye Disease Study (AREDS) by the National Eye Institute, a division of the National Institutes of Health.

A high-dose combination of vitamin C, E, beta-carotene and zinc, Ocuvite is recommended for people who are considered at high risk for advanced AMD, that is, those who have either intermediate AMD in one or both eyes, or advanced AMD in one eye, but not the other eye.

According to information released by the NIH in 2001, when the study results were made public, "For those study participants who had either no AMD or early AMD, the nutrients did not provide an apparent benefit."

(The disease can be diagnosed by a doctor through an eye exam.)

The Ocuvite formula is not a cure, nor will it restore sight already lost, but researchers and scientists were excited about its ability to slow the march toward more serious degeneration.

"Previous studies have suggested that people who have diets rich in green, leafy vegetables have a lower risk of developing AMD," said Dr. Frederick Ferris, director of clinical research at the NEI and chairman of the AREDS study, when the results were released.

But, he added, "The high levels of nutrients that were evaluated in the AREDS are very difficult to achieve from diet alone."

There is a word of caution: Those who think they might benefit from Ocuvite should talk to their doctors because of the high concentration of vitamins and minerals in the pills.

Such a concentration might interact badly with other vitamins or prescription drugs that a person is already taking. And self- medication, doctors warn, could do more harm than good.

LEXINGTON, Ky. - Eye researchers have identified mice with two unusual genes as the first animals likely to help crack the mysteries of the nation's leading cause of blindness for people 50 and older - age-related macular degeneration. "Lead">

Finding an animal with the eye disease - or something very similar, in this case - is a crucial first step to test potential treatments, said Dr. Jaya krishna Ambati, director of ophthalmic research at the University of Kentucky. Ambati led the research and wrote about the findings in November's issue of the journal Nature Medicine, published Oct. 19.

"In medical research and vision research this is a landmark finding," said Dr. Gerald Chader, chief scientific officer for the Maryland-based Foundation Fighting Blindness, which raises money for eye research, including some of Ambati's work. "This animal model is going to allow us to unravel all the factors in this disease."

The discovery of two genes that cause major features of the eye disease in mice is likely to stimulate drug development or other treatments, Ambati said.

There is no treatment for most cases of macular degeneration. "There's been no animal model to even test potential therapies on," Chader said. "It's been a roadblock, a terrible block."

Age-related macular degeneration causes loss of central vision, the type of eyesight needed for driving, reading and other activities.

With the new model, researchers in Ambati's laboratory are already studying the disease's molecular biology and looking at gene therapy and medications that could reverse the eye problems in mice.

PORTLAND - In what may be an important step toward preventing blindness in old age, scientists have identified a gene believed to be responsible for a degenerative eye disease that could strike millions of baby boomers as they grow older.

The gene is suspected of being the main cause of some cases of age-related macular degeneration, or AMD, a complex disease triggered by various factors. It typically affects people 65 and older.

Researchers at Oregon Health & Science University were able to pinpoint the gene by tracking it through a large extended family with a history of the disease.

"We were really lucky to get a single family that large with 10 affected members," said Dennis Schultz, an Oregon Health & Science University biochemist who led the research at the university's Casey Eye Institute.

In age-related macular degeneration, the most sensitive area of the retina breaks down, robbing a person of the fine vision needed to read a book or recognize a face. In severe cases, it can lead to almost total blindness.

About 6 million Americans suffer from AMD, a number that is predicted to double by the year 2030 as the baby boomer generation ages. There are treatments for the disease, but the goal is prevention.

Dr. Johanna Seddon, a Harvard researcher, said more studies will have to be done to establish whether the gene is the single source of some cases of AMD. If the gene proves to be the source, it is possible gene therapy could be used to delay or even prevent the disease, she said.

The study was published Tuesday in the online version of the journal Human Molecular Genetics and will appear in print in the December issue.

PORTLAND, Ore. - In what may be an important step toward preventing blindness in old age, scientists have identified a gene believed to be responsible for a degenerative eye disease that could strike millions of baby boomers as they grow older.

The gene is suspected of being the main cause of some cases of age-related macular degeneration, or AMD, a complex disease triggered by various factors. It typically affects people 65 and older.

Researchers at Oregon Health &Science University were able to pinpoint the gene by tracking it through a large extended family with a history of the disease.

"We were really lucky to get a single family that large with 10 affected members," said Dennis Schultz, an Oregon Health &Science University biochemist who led the research at the university's Casey Eye Institute.

In age-related macular degeneration, the most sensitive area of the retina breaks down, robbing a person of the fine vision needed to read a book or recognize a face. In severe cases, it can lead to almost total blindness.

About 6 million Americans suffer from AMD, a number that is predicted to double by the year 2030 as the baby boomer generation ages. There are treatments for the disease, but the goal is prevention.

Dr. Johanna Seddon, a Harvard researcher, said more studies will have to be done to establish whether the gene is the single source of some cases of AMD. If the gene proves to be the source, it is possible gene therapy could be used to delay or even prevent the disease, she said.

"But I think that's still quite a way off," Seddon said.

The study was published Tuesday in the online version of the journal Human Molecular Genetics and will appear in print in the December issue.

Genes that play a role in forms of macular degeneration that affect younger people have been identified. But this is the first solid evidence of a genetic cause for the age-related form, researchers said.

"I think it's really exciting," said Dr. Michael Gorin, a University of Pittsburgh eye specialist and leading AMD researcher. "This is an important step along the long and arduous path toward understanding the complexity of this disease."

But he warned there are many other factors that contribute to age- related macular degeneration, including other genes, as well as environmental factors, such as smoking.

In a related study announced Monday, a University of Kentucky researcher said he has created for the first time genetically modified mice that have virtually all the important features of AMD. That could advance research on the disease.

Researcher develops solution for condition that impairs sight

As a kid 50 years ago, Louis Dell'Osso refused to accept his doctor's learned opinion that no medical treatment could keep his eyes from darting side to side.

Dell'Osso, who was born with nystagmus, an uncontrollable eye wiggle that impairs sight, set out to prove the doctor wrong. Now he has. The 62-year-old Cleveland researcher has designed a surgery that minimizes the involuntary eye movement.

After results of a successful National Institutes of Health clinical trial are published this year, the procedure will become a new standard of care for some with nystagmus.

"Now there's a whole group of people who can receive an operation that will significantly reduce their nystagmus, give them an opportunity to see better and change their lives," Dell'Osso says.

But Dell'Osso is not an eye surgeon. In fact, he's not a medical doctor at all. Never even took a course in biology. He's an electrical engineer. And yet, says Dr. Robert Burnstine, head of pediatric ophthalmology at Akron Children's Hospital, "in my opinion, he's the world's leading authority on nystagmus."

Dell'Osso runs the Ocular Motor Neurophysiology Laboratory at the Wade Park branch of the Louis Stokes Cleveland VA Medical Center.

He also teaches neurology and biomedical engineering at Case Western Reserve University.

The 33-year-old laboratory was the world's first dedicated to the study of eye motion and remains the leader.

Eye doctors around the world send nystagmus patients to Dell'Osso for evaluations.

Dell'Osso proposed the surgery primarily for people with congenital nystagmus, which appears shortly after birth. The Department of Veterans Affairs, sponsor of Dell'Osso's research, is not particularly interested in those patients because their impaired vision is likely to make them ineligible for military service.

However, older people, veterans among them, can acquire nystagmus after suffering head injuries, strokes or brain diseases such as multiple sclerosis. The surgery can help some of them as well, Dell'Osso says. About one person in 3,000 has nystagmus.

Doing research 25 years ago, Dell'Osso noticed that an operation in which eye muscles were cut and reattached in different spots to realign the eye helped some nystagmus patients more than anyone expected. Somehow, he speculated, the surgery had altered the circuit that controlled the eye muscle's response to faulty eye- movement signals from the brain.

He suggested that simply cutting muscles and tendons away from the eye and reattaching them in the same spot would help certain other nystagmus patients by interrupting that electrical feedback loop. Eye doctors were skeptical.

"I'm an electrical engineer, which some ophthalmologists find mildly offensive," he said.

Even Dell'Osso's main research associate, Dr. Richard Hertle, suspected it would be a waste of time when Dell'Osso persuaded him to try the procedure on a dog with nystagmus. But the operation worked, and Hertle, then at the NIH's National Eye Institute in Bethesda, Md., later conducted the successful human trials in 2000 and 2001.

Now a children's eye doctor and researcher in Columbus, Hertle has been calling the surgery "the Dell'Osso procedure." If the appellation sticks, he thinks it might be the first time that a surgical technique was named for someone who is neither a surgeon nor a clinician.

Burnstine at Akron Children's also won clearance a few years ago to try the procedure on patients. He says the first 25 patients he treated showed an average 20 percent improvement in central vision and about 50 percent improvement in peripheral vision. Some teens and adults who could not get driver's licenses before the surgery now have them.

Nystagmus patients in other states and countries have discovered the Akron Children's program by researching their treatment options on the Internet, Burnstine said. The operation costs as much as $5,000.

On Monday, Burnstine performed his 26th Dell'Osso procedure, on Justin Runner, 5, of Green in Summit County. Justin's congenital nystagmus emerged when he was 6 weeks old, so he does not really know what normal sight is. Still, says his mother, Stefanie, "He'll say things to us that will just break your heart."

One day Justin was playing with Yu-Gi-Oh trading cards, popular with kids, when he came across one bearing the image of a bizarre character with eyes all over its arms and legs.

"Justin just looked at the card and said, 'I'll bet he can see a lot better than I can,' " his mother said.

Researchers soon will find a way to change the faulty feedback circuit without surgery, perhaps by giving a drug injection, Dell'Osso believes.

Meantime, the inventor of the Dell'Osso procedure is busy with another potentially ground-breaking project. He is teaming with researchers at the University of Pennsylvania on a gene therapy for Leber's congenital amaurosis, an inherited disease that causes severe vision loss in newborns.

Tests on a special breed of dog are under way, and clinical trials with infants could begin in a couple of years. Down the road, the lessons learned in Dell'Osso's lab could be applied to help blind veterans and others see again.

"The VA may save the sight of babies because of work done here," Dell'Osso says.

"This laboratory is the only such laboratory in the world, and it lives in a VA hospital."

Gene therapy research could help people with choroideremia

WORCESTER -- Mitchell F. Ford doesn't like sunglasses. Until a year and a half ago, he always wondered why his father insisted he wear them when he went outside.

His father, Worcester Police Officer Francis J. Ford, had a good reason. He knew his son had a 50-50 chance of developing a degenerative eye disease called choroideremia. There is no treatment and no cure. But ultraviolet light is known to damage the choroid and the retina, two layers of cells lining the inside of the back of the eye that are affected by choroideremia.

Choroideremia is a rare inherited disorder that gradually results in vision loss, starting with night blindness. Males can get it and females can carry it. Mitchell's mother, Detective Janis A. Ford, is a carrier of the gene mutation that gradually made her father blind.

People with choroideremia, their families, doctors and scientists all hope for the day when a treatment or cure may emerge from gene therapy, but achieving that goal is a tremendously difficult task complicated by last week's suspension of 27 gene therapy trials by the Food and Drug Administration. The Fords want to draw attention to the disease and find ways to move research forward.

The Fords now know that their son has choroideremia, but just after he was born, they contemplated whether to find out immediately if he would develop the disease or wait until symptoms surfaced. His grandfather, George Bianculli, had two pieces of advice: Wait and see, but make him wear sunglasses anyway.

"I wanted to know right away, but my father-in-law said it's not a good thing to do. You'd favor him. You'd say, `Poor kid, he couldn't catch the ball, " said Mr. Ford. "I agreed."

Mitchell, now 13, caught quite a few balls in his Little League career. This winter he skis and competes in two basketball leagues. He likes ska music, plays the trumpet and hopes to learn how to play the electric guitar.

"It really doesn't affect me that much right now," Mitchell said. "I always wondered why Dad made me wear sunglasses."

Mitchell and his maternal grandfather are among an estimated 3,000 men in the United States who have choroideremia. Choroideremia falls under the heading of retinitis pigmentosa and other hereditary night-blinding disorders. Together they occur in 1 out of 4,000 Americans.

The Fords are hosting a Feb. 7 fund-raiser to attract donations to the Choroideremia Research Foundation Inc. of Springfield. The 3- year-old 100-member volunteer group works with the Foundation Fighting Blindness of Owings Mills, Md., which also supports research into choroideremia and other eye diseases.

"What Mitchell has on his side is his youth," said Mr. Ford. "It's too late for my father-in-law."

Choroideremia first appears as night blindness in boys. In Mitchell's case, his family first noticed it two summers ago, when he stepped on a neighbor's dog in the waning light of an evening cookout. Everyone else saw the black dog but Mitchell didn't, Mr. Ford recalled.

When choroideremia is suspected, the diagnosis can be made by an eye doctor looking at the back of the eye, which shows a diffuse disturbance of the pigmented layer underlying the retina. The diagnosis is confirmed by recording an abnormal electroretinogram, or ERG, with a contact lens on the eye, much like an electrocardiogram for the heart.

As the condition advances, patients develop tunnel vision, like looking down a straw, said Dr. Eliot L. Berson, professor of ophthalmology at Harvard Medical School. He is also director of the Berman-Gund Laboratory for the Study of Retinal Degenerations at the Massachusetts Eye & Ear Infirmary in Boston. Men with choroideremia usually retain little if any central vision beyond the age of 60.

Female carriers of the disease can often be identified by an ophthalmologist looking in their eyes. While their vision is usually normal, they have a telltale patchy pigment disturbance visible on examination.

There is great variation in the severity of the disease at a given age even among families, Dr. Berson said. His research looks for risk factors in the patient's medical or dietary history that may be associated with a faster or slower course of the disease. Vitamin A supplements, for example, have been shown to slow the decline of remaining retinal function in patients with retinitis pigmentosa. No such treatment is known for choroideremia.

"We are trying to define more precisely the course of the disease on a year-to-year basis, and plan to see if the rates can be correlated with one or another mutation in the gene," Dr. Berson said.

Boys and men with choroideremia lack a protein made by one gene. That gene was cloned in 1993 by F.P.M. Cremers in Holland.

"They've already found the needle in the haystack with us," said Cory MacDonald, president of the Choroideremia Research Foundation. "Now we just need a way to fix it."

In the last 10 years many mutations have been identified in families around the world, said Dr. Ian MacDonald (no relation), visiting scientist at the National Eye Institute of the National Institutes of Health in Bethesda, Md. His goal is to find out where the actual protein that is made by the gene is expressed in the eye in the normal individual.

"It's very important not only for the science but also for the treatment of the disorder to know where the protein acts in the eye and how it might be replaced to enable a normal physiology," he said. "If we are finding that it's actually necessary and needs to be present in sufficient quantity in particular cells in the eye, then we need to target those cells to correct that protein lack."

The missing protein -- the RAB escort protein 1 -- affects protein trafficking inside the retina. Patients are usually healthy in every other respect, said Dr. Berson.

Dr. Miguel Seabra, head of cellular and molecular biology at Imperial College in London, has been working on creating a mouse model of choroideremia for about three years. Once his team does, they hope to understand the cellular mechanism of how vision degenerates. The ultimate goal would be to attempt gene therapy on mice, replacing the mutated choroideremia gene by adding a normal choroideremia gene to the cells in the eye.

"The more we know about the degenerative process, the more ideas we may have as to how to prevent the degenerative process from fully developing," he said. "I am hopeful that an effective treatment for choroideremia will be found, but as we learned from other diseases, the road to a cure is not predictable and is usually lengthy."

Dr. Seabra said a timeframe for success was impossible to predict, but he hopes some breakthrough might be achieved within 10 to 15 years.

Dr. MacDonald, who is also chairman of ophthalmology at the University of Alberta, said the impetus toward human clinical trials would change if the FDA's suspension of gene therapy trials is going to last long. The government agency's move was prompted after a second child in France developed a condition resembling leukemia after being treated with gene therapy to correct a fatal immune deficiency commonly known as bubble-boy disease.

"We would have to rethink how we're going to address a clinical trial if we're not using gene therapy," he said.

Because choroideremia is a biochemical disorder, he said, there may be other avenues that would open up as understanding of the disease improves. "There's lots of hope here. We don't necessarily have to stick the gene in."

Meanwhile, Mitchell Ford said having choroideremia is not really a big deal.

"It could be worse," he said. "I'm disappointed I have the disease but there are lots of people with worse things."
