Ontario's latest foray in health expenditure control and the targeting of drug expenditures is best understood by examining the recent evolution of health spending in the province.

Over the last 40 years, real per capita provincial government health spending in Ontario has grown faster than standard measures of the resource base -- an indicator that health spending is not sustainable.

For example, while inflation-adjusted spending per person on health in Ontario has grown at about four per cent per year, accompanying growth rates for GDP, federal transfers and provincial government revenues have grown well below that rate. This is not just an Ontario phenomenon -- this also holds for every other province.

Provincial government health spending in Ontario (in 2009 constant dollars) grew from $13.5 billion in 1975 to $45.2 billion by 2009 -- a more than threefold increase after inflation.

More importantly, as a share of provincial government program expenditures over this period, health went from 31 per cent to 46 per cent and is anticipated to account for over half of government spending in the very near future.

Clearly, unless health spending is reined in, there will be less money for other government programs in areas such as social spending, education, transportation and public security.

All this is made much more dire by the recent slowdown in the Ontario economy and the rising debt and deficit whose servicing costs will also eat into the government budget.

So given that health spending is on the radar screen, why is the Ontario government starting off by going after what amounts to a few hundred million dollars in savings from pharmacists?

The answer is best understood by realizing that while provincial government health spending faces a sustainability issue, some categories of health spending are more sustainable than others.

When the health expenditure categories used by the Canadian Institute for Health Information are examined for Ontario government health spending for the period 1975 to 2009, the largest average annual growth rate is for drugs at 10 per cent.

Next comes public health spending at about nine per cent, followed by other health (home care, ambulances, etc.) at eight per cent and then capital spending at seven per cent. Physicians are next at four per cent annual growth followed eventually by hospitals at a paltry two per cent.

The fastest growth in government health care spending over the last 40 years has not been in the traditional health sectors of physician and hospital spending but in these other sectors.

As a result, the share of government health spending in Ontario accounted for by physicians and hospitals declined from 77 per cent in 1975 to 60 per cent in 2009.

On the other hand, the share accounted for by all the remaining categories rose from 23 per cent to 40 per cent!

As for the remaining categories, it is public drug spending that has exhibited the greatest growth rate and as a result has become a target.

Much of this spending, however, is on drugs for seniors -- a politically vocal group. This explains why in the end the overall savings are only a few hundred million dollars despite the outrage being expressed by pharmacists who are bearing the brunt of the savings. Attempts to reach bigger savings are yet to come but political optics requires that the drug sector get a cut. The provincial government, however, is focusing on pharmacists rather than the true sources of spending -- consumer demand and drug manufacturers.

What will the future bring to health care in Ontario and by extension the rest of Canada's provinces? In the age of SARS and H1N1, public health will likely be a hands-off category.

Despite the fact that hospitals and physicians have exhibited among the lowest growth rates in spending, there will be no reward for good behaviour. Together, these categories still account for 60 per cent of government health spending and the sheer size of these sectors represent a substantial source of savings.

After some tinkering with home-care spending and a slowing of capital projects, expect to see a concerted effort to tackle labour-intensive physician and hospital services.

The proposed pay-for-performance contracts targeted at hospital CEOs in Ontario could be extended more broadly to the hospital sector as well as physicians. Moreover, a long-standing goal has always been to substitute less expensive nurse practitioners and nurses for some physician services and one can expect to see a renewed impetus in this direction.

The more interesting question is not what the Ontario government is going to try but how the public will react to these changes, especially if they affect access to medical care? The rest of the country will be watching.

Livio Di Matteo is a professor of economics at Lakehead University and recently published a study on the sustainability of provincial government health spending in the European Journal of Health Economics.

While I have much respect for Ipsos-Reid and John Wright, I have little respect for the claims surrounding this particular survey. If John were in my survey methods class, he would have received an "F" for this project.

The reason is fairly prosaic to anyone who reflects for a few moments on what such a survey actually tells us.

Suppose, for example, that Ipsos-Reid had asked Canadians, "Do you have family members, friends, or acquaintances who have been divorced?" The first question asks individuals about the people they know -- and who doesn't know someone who has been divorced? The other asks individuals about their own experiences. Awareness does not equal incidence. Both are important. But obviously they are two very different things.

Take teenage suicide as another example. Of considerable importance, if we were only having an academic debate, we could shake hands at the end of the session, and go out together for dinner, with the world continuing on pretty much the same.

Unfortunately, the results and their dissemination have the potential to have a significant impact on public perception, the Roman Catholic Church, and Catholics across the country. That's why a few hands like mine need to be raised.

It is also the day that Tina L'Hirondelle died in a High Prairie hospital. The 39-year-old had a history of asthma and diabetes. A little more than a week later, tests confirmed the woman had H1N1, making her the first infected person in Canada to die.

April 28 also proved to be a nightmare for Leslieville pig farmer Arnold Van Ginkel.

After arriving from Holland in 2003, the dream farm he had built with his family was now in tatters, his entire herd placed under quarantine.

Worried that some of his roughly 2,200 pigs were coughing, Van Ginkel had called the veterinarian. H1N1 was soon suspected.

The swine flu, as it is sometimes known, has been around since the 1920s in various forms, according to Alberta's chief veterinarian, Gerald Hauer. It's not unusual to have several cases in Alberta: last year five herds in the province contracted regular swine flu, and there were a further six suspected incidents.

What was different here was that Van Ginkel's herd was contracting this new strain -- the same that was setting off pandemic alarm bells.

Unable to ship out any of his pigs, Van Ginkel's barn was quickly overcrowding as his herd reproduced, he noted in a recent interview. Then, of course, there was the negative publicity his problem brought to the industry, even though the pork is still safe to eat.

The only solution, as he saw it, was to destroy his entire herd. "Pretty tough, especially in the beginning," he says.

Every morning, Dr. Andre Corriveau, Alberta's chief medical officer of health, receives an e-mail in his inbox from a service that scans the world for health news.

One morning in April, that e-mail brought with it a warning of "something odd" in Mexico. Reports of infections in the southern United States soon followed.

Within days of the reports, the province's health system had been put on alert. Of particular concern, Corriveau remembers, was that so many Albertans holiday in Mexico during that time of year.

Part of the alert meant that

samples were now being taken from Albertans with flu symptoms if they had recently returned from Mexico. Soon, the provincial lab was able perform a sequence of tests to isolate the novel strain and develop a tool kit to diagnosis the virus.

By June, Calgary intensivecare units began to see their first seriously ill H1N1 patients, recalls Dr. Tom Stelfox, an intensive-care specialist.

Other hospitals in the province had already admitted severely ill patients, including an Edmonton area girl in early May.

But that first wave ended by late July and a lull set in, Stelfox said, allowing officials to begin ramping up plans for the fall wave.

More ventilators were purchased with the expectation that severe cases of H1N1 would rise, Corriveau said.

Communication plans were finalized so information on the location of free intensive-care beds and ventilators could be quickly disseminated, Stelfox said.

That lull, of course, was not to last. As fall hit, the second wave crashed into Alberta. People began missing work, family doctors were receiving patients, emergency rooms began filling, as did intensivecare units.

Oct. 26, 2009, is also a date that should be etched in Alberta's H1N1 history.

On the same day the province flung open the doors of clinics to kick-start the mass-vaccination effort, a 13-year-old boy named Evan Frustaglio died at a Toronto hospital.

Some call his death a game-changer. The next day it was confirmed the previously healthy teen who enjoyed playing hockey had died from complications related to the flu virus.

Across Alberta, risk-averse parents took stock of that case and rushed to get their families vaccinated, according to Dr. Tom Noseworthy, a health policy expert at the University of Calgary.

Health officials had thought they would have to drag people to the clinics with a compelling message, Noseworthy said. Suddenly, lineups appeared far beyond what anybody expected.

"They weren't ready for an avalanche-like effect," he said.

If there's anything Albertans will remember about the early days of the H1N1 vaccine rollout, it's the enormous waits that greeted people looking to a get their shot.

The stories have become almost legendary: people in line for up to eight hours in the cold and reports of some even waiting overnight, as if an H1N1 needle was equivalent to coveted concert tickets.

But Corriveau suggests other factors were at play. Alberta, he said, was hit with H1N1 about two weeks earlier than Eastern Canada.

When the vaccine became available, people in this province saw a greater urgency to get immunized, given family members, friends and coworkers around them were getting sick.

Then, of course, there was the issue of who should receive vaccination first. In the early days, while encouraging high-risk groups to get immunized, all Albertans were accepted.

By the end of week, another significant problem emerged: the vaccine manufacturer couldn't produce enough vaccine to keep Alberta, and other provinces, well stocked.

The decision to temporarily close vaccination clinics was made over the Halloween weekend, Corriveau recalls.

After consultation with health officials, he made the call to then-health minister Ron Liepert to give him the bad news.

By early November clinics were up and running again. But this time strict rules regulating who was getting vaccinated were in place.

At the same time, intensivecare units in the city had reached capacity.

At the height of the pandemic, Stelfox estimates up to half the beds were taken by patients with H1N1.

Anyone suspected of having the flu virus was sequestered from non-infected patients.

Most had to be placed on some sort of life sustaining therapy, such as breathing tubes and ventilators. Several of the most severe cases were given a therapy rarely used in Calgary.

A special device took blood from the body, removing carbon dioxide and inserting oxygen, before transferring it back into the person.

"Many of these patients had very severe failures of their lungs," Stelfox said.

A couple of things stick out in Stelfox's mind. He remembers the "humanity" around him, of how hard doctors, nurses and other staff worked.

He remarks on the professionalism, given that, in the beginning, vaccine was not yet available and they were working with patients who were sick with an easily transmitted virus. There was the psychological and physical discomfort suffered by nurses and other staff who spent long hours in hot rooms dressed in clothing to protect themselves from contracting the virus.

The doctor also noted the bad moments. In one case, he had to tell a family that their loved one had passed away.

Then, there is the marvel of medical technology, which Stelfox says saved many lives.

"We could be fairly comfortable in saying that almost all of the patients that got really sick, and who were admitted to intensive-care units, none of those patients would have had a chance of surviving in previous pandemics," he said. "But because of the advances in medicine, they had a chance of surviving in the current pandemic."

The peak, Stelfox says, was late October and early into the next month. By mid-November, they sensed the worst was over.

Fewer people were getting severely sick. The lineups at vaccination clinics began to decline.

Today, things have returned to normal on Van Ginkel's farm. After his herd was culled, the barn had to be thoroughly cleaned and undergo inspections by the Canadian Food Inspection Agency.

By August, the farmer had bought more sows. He also received some compensation from the provincial and federal governments, although he declines to say how much.

His herd now numbers between 2,500 and 3,000, and is back to the strength it was a year ago, before H1N1 hit.

By December 2009, it was clear the second wave of H1N1 was dissipating. Corriveau took his first holiday since the outbreak began. He went to Yellowknife, where his wife still lives, enjoying the comforts of home and cross-country skiing.

In the early days of the vaccine rollout, Noseworthy was highly critical of the province. His stance had softened significantly.

In retrospect, he believes the health system in Alberta did a "damn good job."

By January, after a tortuous four months in hospital, Amburlea Carlson was finally well enough to be discharged.

She still remembers the September day she was taken to hospital by ambulance. Her coughing was so severe she went into premature labour, and only at hospital did the contractions stop.

Within just three days she was put in a coma, induced so her body would accept the life-support machines that were to keep her alive for months.

Even so, she remembers moments from that time. She recalls her father speaking to her and her mother playing her music.

Amburlea has sung since she was a child. But all those tubes down her throat have taken their toll. She won't be able to sing again.

When her daughter was born, the baby's prognosis was so touch and go that Amburlea says staff were hesitant to tell her she was alive, fearing she would soon die.

But things have changed so much since those dire days.

Just earlier this month, Briar was able to follow her mother's path and left hospital for home.

She still wears a breathing tube. Her mother says she will need surgery on her eyes and still requires checkups with specialists three or four times a week.

Amburlea credits her survival and that of her daughter to several things: the staff at the Foothills Hospital, the strength of her family and the prayers that were made.

"I still don't understand why we made it."

And with some control over your own life comes dignity. Income is a simple prerequisite baseline for both personal and population health, similar to exercise and good nutrition.

These value decisions did not destroy the economy, rather they strengthened it. If urgency is value driven, then we must act immediately to reflect our values in our government policies, the wages we pay in our businesses, and how we as individuals act to support an adequate income for everyone.

We don't need a perfect solution, we simply need a better one.

Poverty is an insult to our values.

Why does it still exist?

If one were feeling charitable toward the WHO, one could point out that it didn't know back in the spring of last year -- when it shouted "level 6!" from the rooftops -- that H1N1 would prove to be such a relatively innocuous bug. But it's precisely because it didn't know that the WHO should have been more cautious with its labelling. You don't shout "fire" in a crowded theatre just because it smells like the popcorn might be a little on the burnt side. It's not worth the chaos and alarm you might cause. (Or in this case, the run on vaccines and the resorting to quacks and sketchy "home" remedies.)

By the time the WHO realized that swine flu was not a pandemic in the layman's sense of that word (infecting a high percentage of a population and killing a high percentage of those infected), it should have issued a mea culpa for overstating H1N1's risks. It didn't. In fact, the closest the WHO has come to conceding any mishandling of the swine flu "pandemic" are the remarks made by Keiji Fukuda, the WHO's top influenza expert, on Monday. "Confusion about phases and level of severity [in the WHO's system for declaring a pandemic] remains a very vexing issue," he said. But as late as last week Fukuda was still vigorously defending the WHO's decision to sound the ultimate alarm over swine flu: "We have never had a moment's doubt of whether this is a pandemic or not."

Notice the use of the present tense? The WHO still hasn't officially declared the H1N1 "pandemic" over, since it insists on sticking with a torqued definition of pandemic that pays no attention to how serious the illness is.

The rest of the planet is pretty clear on the matter. H1N1 is not, and never was, a pandemic. By insisting otherwise, the WHO is diminishing its own credibility, which will be dangerous when the time comes that the world really does need to turn to it for advice.

Ms. Sander and her colleagues at the University Toronto, where she is a doctoral student, found that the universal program costs about $40-million a year, which includes cost of vaccine, promotion and physician compensation.
