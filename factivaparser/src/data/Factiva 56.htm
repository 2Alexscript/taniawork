Stephen Hume

Almost half the world's countries now report outbreaks of the H1N1 viral infection commonly called swine flu. Laboratory-confirmed cases approached 28,000 this week. Canada now ranks third after Mexico and the U.S. for infections.

And as the World Health Organization officially declared Thursday that the planet is finally in the throes of the influenza pandemic, comparisons to the Spanish Flu of 1918 are naturally being made.

In Manitoba, judging from news reports, the province moved Wednesday to begin mobilizing its own pandemic influenza plans as the number of patients developing severe symptoms continued to increase -- at time of writing, there were 56 confirmed cases and 27 patients required intensive care. Schools and daycares were closed in some communities and people presenting flu symptoms were being urged not to go to hospital emergency wards.

The Winnipeg Free Press quoted Dr. Ethan Rubinstein, a professor of infectious diseases at the University of Manitoba. He said the outbreak is expected to follow the same pattern as in 1918.

We can hope those parallels don't track too closely. So far there's no cause for undue anxiety. Most cases appear relatively mild. However, prudence suggests revisiting the experience of 91 years ago. Knowledge gleaned from what happened then might help inform our reactions today.

Much of the 1918 pandemic remains a mystery. The flu wasn't a reportable illness then and communications were poor; health infrastructure was limited and local; diagnostic techniques and lab technology were primitive. Yet the Spanish flu may still teach useful things.

One troubling parallel springs right to the foreground. The impact upon aboriginal people in 1918 was disproportionately high. In British Columbia, post-pandemic mortality estimates doubled when previously uncounted aboriginal deaths in remote communities were finally tabulated.

In Manitoba today, half the cases requiring intensive care are aboriginal, particularly worrying for public health authorities in B.C., which has one of Canada's largest aboriginal populations.

Another parallel that's worth thinking about is how the disease pulsed through the population. The 1918 outbreak occurred in three distinct phases. The Spanish flu first occurred in the early months of that year with a relatively mild wave of infections in the United States. It then appeared to abate during the summer months. That proved the lull before the storm. A second, much more virulent phase erupted in the fall.

The Spanish flu first arrived in B.C. at the small community of Corbin, just south of the Crowsnest Pass.

It reached Vancouver in October, subsided again and then returned for a third lethal phase in December and January 1919, before abruptly vanishing.

It's for just such a pattern of recurring waves of infection that today's public health and emergency measures planners will be watching closely as they prepare response strategies. Could this week's sudden heavy load of severe cases in Manitoba mark the beginning of a second, more dangerous phase? Let's hope not.

In 1918, Vancouver suffered among the highest urban mortality rates in North America. In late October, at the worst of the pandemic, 1,700 of the city's 109,000 inhabitants were in hospital. Historians think 795 of Vancouver's 4,890 reported cases died. The number is probably conservative.

Yet that outcome represented a public health triumph. Deaths would have been more numerous had the city not been blessed with a far-sighted genius in its medical health officer.

Dr. Fred Underhill had carefully observed and planned during the early phases of the pandemic. He quelled anxiety, encouraged public engagement and deployed effective responses when the worst arrived.

There's one more important lesson to learn from the Spanish flu.

The World Health Organization yesterday declared a Phase 6 pandemic alert.

What that means, practically, is that there is community-based spread of influenza A/H1N1, a novel virus, in more than one region of the world – in this case North America and Australasia. (Europe, South America and Asia are also on the verge of broad community spread.)

While the word “pandemic” carries all sorts of frightening connotations in common parlance, the WHO definition is technical.

That is why the announcement – the first pandemic in the world since the Hong Kong flu killed more than one million people in 1968 – was greeted largely with a shrug.

In fact, it was expected. A senior WHO official said six weeks ago that Phase 6 was “imminent.”

The international health group spent the time since then priming health officials, journalists and the public to ensure the announcement would be anticlimatic rather than panic-inducing.

As Keiji Fukuda, head of the global influenza program at the WHO, said in a briefing to journalists earlier this week: “The declaration of phase changes, the movement from one phase to another, is not simply getting up in front of press cameras and making an announcement. It is a way to prepare the world to deal with the situation.”

For all intents and purposes, Canada has been operating at Phase 6 since early May, when it activated the Pandemic Influenza Plan. Yesterday's announcement tells countries where A/H1N1 is not yet widespread to do the same.

Phase 6 also has some real practical implications for the WHO and the low- and middle-income countries that depend on its resources. Under the rules that govern the WHO, it can now move from containment operations to broader response measures. For example, it can give pharmaceutical companies the green light to proceed with production of an A/H1N1 vaccine. (Preliminary work such as growing vaccine strain is already under way, but a vaccine will not be ready until September at the earliest.)

In other words, the pandemic phases matter to much of the world.

The problem with the WHO pandemic phases, however, is that they send a confusing mixed signal.

“Phase 6 – the highest alert level” sounds worrisome. The public is left with the impression that a deadly killer is stalking the planet.

But the levels strictly reflect geographic spread and not severity. (It should be said that these phases are a new approach, developed after SARS and avian influenza, but there was an underlying assumption that a pandemic would necessarily be deadly.)

While influenza A/H1N1 is potentially severe, the reality is that, so far, the disease is pretty mild. As of yesterday, 74 countries had reported 27,737 laboratory-confirmed cases, including 141 deaths.

This is not in any way, shape or form, the Spanish flu of 1918, a pandemic that killed about 50 million people, although one cannot entirely rule out the possibility that A/H1N1 will become more virulent and deadly.

The WHO needs to find a simple way to transmit that message, something akin to the colour system used by U.S. Homeland Security, which sets the national threat level at blue, green, yellow, orange or red.

At the recent World Health Assembly, that is precisely what some countries demanded – a warning system that reflects both geographic spread and severity, a system with more subtlety.

The world is in pandemic Phase 6, but the number six should be coloured yellow, meaning it is a moderate and manageable threat.

A/H1N1 is a rapidly spreading virus, but it is not – and hopefully will not become – a red alert.

"When there is a disaster, then people start realizing," says Bob Klay, president of Klay Information Management Consulting Ltd.

But when companies are cutting costs or downsizing, preparing for any disaster--whether it's a pandemic, a natural disaster or any other disruption to business--tends to fall way down the list of priorities.

Craig Wilson, a strategic communi-cations consultant for Avaya Canada, says too often corporations focus on only the technology aspect of contingency planning.

"A lot of companies are focused (on) business continuity from an IT perspective,"he says, adding natural disasters and other emergencies are overlooked.

"Its financial impact can be much more huge, but unfortunately people are much more familiar with server failures and so see those as much more pressing,"he says.

With the recent concern about a potential pandemic, Klay says many employers just sent e-mails with links to websites of groups such as the World Health Organization. It's a start, but lacks much of a plan.

"The problem is that if you're an employee...it doesn't address the effect," says Klay.

"Most continuity plans lack depth," he says.

"They do scenario-based planning based on having no access to the building. That's certainly a very good start, but...I believe the biggest key in all planning is the aware-ness, the maintenance and ongoing communication about it."

The communication can't be a monologue. There is nothing more important than open communication with direct feedback from employees.

It's also not about fearmongering, he insists.

"What we need to do is get rid of arrogance."

"You really have to look at your essential services, critical processes and functions and how well your people are supported, trained and how they can sustain a blow," says Klay.

Employees who are given this kind of training, and a thorough understanding of a company's comprehensive contingency plan, are more likely to respond rationally instead of out of panic.

Wilson, speaking at a recent conference, asked the crowd of about 50 business leaders if they had a practised pandemic response plan. Less than a quarter put up their hand.

Flu cases at city schools continue to climb

With the world teetering on the edge of the declaration of a flu pandemic, the city's public health unit now says 37 schools have reported suspected cases of H1N1, up from 29 schools last Thursday.

As well, 107 people had tested positive for H1N1, almost double the 56 people who tested positive as of last Thursday. About half were under the age of 16.

The World Health Organization said Tuesday that the world was getting "very, very close" to a swine flu pandemic. Under WHO guidelines, a key criteria for declaring a pandemic is community spread outside the first region in which the disease was reported.

In this case, that is outside the Americas. So far, 1,211 cases have been recorded, for example, in Australia. In all, 26,563 infections including 140 deaths have been reported from 73 countries.

"One of the critical issues is that we do not want people to panic if they hear that we are in a pandemic situation," Feiji Fukuda, WHO's acting assistant director-general, said on Tuesday.

As of Monday, a total of 2,446 laboratory-confirmed cases of H1N1 flu virus have been reported in all provinces and territories in Canada except Newfoundland.

The Ottawa Public Health Unit says officials are now unable, in some cases, to trace the spread of the virus from one person to another, a development that experts say is a sign of an impending pandemic.

"We are seeing cases in Ottawa with no specific link to another person. It has been happening in Ontario. That is what we call community spread," Sherry Nigro, the manager responsible for pandemic planning at Ottawa Public Health, said last week. "We know that it is fairly transmittable and we know that we are not going to prevent it so what we have to do is minimize it."

The growth in the number of cases, and concerns about the spread of the illness in the region, has prompted CHEO to issue a set of guidelines for parents rattled by reports of H1N1 in schools and who are contemplating a trip to the emergency room.

CHEO's emergency room has been busier-than-expected with an increased number of patients with flu-like illness, said Dr. Ken Farion, associate medical director of CHEO's emergency department.

Just because a child has flu-like symptoms, however, doesn't mean they have H1N1. Usually the flu season has tapered off by now, but this year there are a number of flu strains making the rounds, said Farion.

"If you're sick, stay at home to prevent the spread of all infections -- not just this one," he said.

"Most of what we're seeing is milder disease. They have a fever and they have a cough, but they're still able to get around and eat and drink. If this is the case, they should stay at home."

Farion said parents should be concerned if their child has difficulty breathing, there are concerns that the child has become dehydrated because of lack of fluids, if the child is extremely listless or if there is an underlying health condition such as immune system weakness or a respiratory condition.

Parents should also be concerned if a child with mild to moderate symptoms appears to get better, then gets worse. The flu may have caused a weakening of the immune system, leading to a secondary infection such as bacterial pneumonia.

Many parents are bringing their child to the emergency room to seek testing, but this is usually only done if the child has an underlying condition.

"Showing up doesn't mean you're going to get tested," said Farion

"Because we're not testing the vast majority of patients, whether they have Flu 1 or Flu 2 isn't really relevant. The focus should be on symptom management."

The vast majority of CHEO patients with flu-like symptoms are being discharged to home with the instructions that they may have H1N1 or another flu. CHEO has only admitted about one or two flu patients a day, and these are often children with underlying medical conditions.

Farion said most healthy children with the flu are ill for four or five days and are significantly improved within a week. For infants and toddlers and children with underlying conditions, it may take longer to recover.

Children and families who arrive at the CHEO emergency department are screened for febrile respiratory illness. Those who have symptoms will be directed to a separate isolation area to see a nurse wearing full protective equipment. These extra measures mean it takes staff longer to see patients, and the sickest patients will be seen first.

Farion advises parents who decide to visit the emergency room to ensure healthy siblings stay at home.

"We don't need the volume and congestion," he said.

Meanwhile, the emergency room at the Queensway-Carleton Hospital has had a "record week" -- but only a tiny proportion of the patients have been ill with the flu and the hospital is still puzzling over what caused the bump in numbers, said chief of emergency Dr. John Earle.

One day last week, for example, the emergency department logged 230 visits when a normal flow would be 185 patients.

"It's usually what you would see on July 1 or Boxing Day," he said.
