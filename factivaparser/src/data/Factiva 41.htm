Although the H1N1 pandemic has proven to be a mild one so far, it would be a mistake to downplay the threat it poses, says one of Canada's top public health experts.

Kumanan Wilson, Canada Research Chair in public health policy at the University of Ottawa, said Canada must not repeat the mistakes that were made during past public-health emergencies, such as the SARS outbreak and the tainted-blood scandal, and that the fundamental ethos of public health--the precautionary principle--must be applied.

"We have to assume the worst-case scenario in public health because the worst-case scenarios in public health, they don't affect a few people, they affect large populations with potentially catastrophic results," he said in an interview with Canwest News Service and Global National. "One of the mistakes we made with the blood system and the tainted-blood scandal was downplaying the risk, waiting for better evidence, not overreacting...and the results were somewhat catastrophic," he said.

In the 1970s and 1980s, the national blood supply was contaminated and tens of thousands of Canadians were infected with hepatitis C and some with HIV.

The emergence of the new strain of the H1N1 virus in the spring, and its quick migration around the globe that prompted the World Health Organization to declare it a pandemic, has been driving fear and panic that this is the deadly flu experts have been predicting for years. Governments around the world are mounting massive responses and are spending billions of dollars to fight the virus, also called the swine flu.

Individual cases of swine flu are not being tracked, so it's impossible to know how many Canadians have caught the bug, but more than 1,400 people have been hospitalized and of those, almost 300 were admitted to an intensive care unit. The virus has been linked to 76 deaths. The regular seasonal flu is estimated to cause about 20,000 hospitalizations every year and up to 4,000 deaths, mostly in elderly people or those with underlying health conditions.

The novel H1N1 strain is acting differently than the seasonal flu because it seems to favour younger, healthy people and can cause severe illness in them.

In response to some critics who are asking whether all of the flu hype is overblown, Wilson said public health officials "had no choice" but to take the steps they have because the virus had never been seen before and its behaviour is unpredictable.

"In retrospect, I think it's going to be better to look like we overprepared than underprepared," he said.

Public health officials say the key to stopping the pandemic in its tracks is to vaccinate and they are encouraging Canadians to get the H1N1 pandemic shot when it becomes available in November. Not everyone is keen to get vaccines, however, particularly this one because the safety testing will not be as thorough as it normally would be in a non-pandemic scenario.

Wilson said a vaccine injury compensation program is needed in Canada for people who may be harmed by it, or any other vaccine. That would help encourage the public to buy into any mass immunization program, but Quebec is the only jurisdiction in Canada that has a no-fault program.

"I think the No. 1 reason to bring this program in is an issue of fairness," he said. Vaccination is not mandatory in Canada but if that were to change, or, if certain populations like health-care workers were required to get the shot, Wilson said it's only fair they be compensated if something were to go wrong with the vaccine. Going through the justice system and launching lawsuits is an uneven and ineffective method of getting compensated, he added.

The debate about whether certain people should be required to take the shot extends beyond health-care workers and has many parents wondering what would happen if they choose not to have their child vaccinated. They may be facing off with parents who think it's irresponsible to forgo the shot.

"This could cause a lot of tension in schools," said Wilson.

Parents are allowed to get exemptions for routine childhood vaccinations, such as the measles and mumps shot, but if there's an outbreak schools have the authority to send children home that don't have their shots.

"We could see a similar scenario if the H1N1 outbreak worsens," said Wilson.

Canada's chief public health officer said on Friday that 1.8 million doses of unadjuvanted swine flu vaccine have been ordered for pregnant women and children under the age of three. The move recognizes that not enough clinical data exists on the safety and effectiveness of adjuvanted vaccines in these groups, Dr. David Butler-Jones said.

Decisions about whether schools will be sites for the H1N1 immunization program are up to local public health authorities.

Wilson said public health officials have already taken many of the steps that are needed to protect the public, such as ordering enough vaccine for the whole population.

"What we have to be careful (of) now is making sure that the measures that we bring in to protect against the harms of the virus are not harmful in and of themselves," he said. "We don't want to introduce unnecessarily harmful measures if this virus does not turn out to be that bad."

Resistance to viruses, particularly cold and flu viruses, is dependent on a strong immune system. A strong immune system is regulated in the body by micronutrients (vitamins and minerals), especially vitamin A, zinc, iron and B12, the role of each of which has long been established in peer-reviewed research literature.

In recognition of this, Canada has been directly supporting delivery of micronutrients to vulnerable communities in drought or war-ravaged countries all over the world through something called the micronutrient initiative. By extrapolation, vitamin A supplementation among First Nations peoples may reduce vulnerability to H1N1 by 25 per cent or more.

The estimated cost per capsule of vitamin A is approximately two cents, with proven reduction in infection incidence and none of the adverse reactions already reported in H1N1 vaccine trials.

Rather than body bags, would widespread vitamin A supplementation among our First Nations peoples not be a better priority in H1N1 preparedness?

Let's use a small portion of our international micronutrient initiative to support First Nations peoples and, for that matter, anyone in Canada living at or below the poverty line. Two cents per capsule is a minimal cost for an estimated 25 per cent potential reduction in infection incidence among our vulnerable peoples.

Studies of absenteeism have long shown that the decision of whether or not to go to work is based as much on how people feel about their jobs as it is on their symptoms. People in managerial positions take far fewer sick days than employees who feel they have less control over their working conditions. Dysfunctional workplaces can be identified by higher rates of absenteeism.

One of the themes of our working culture is that people who are sick a lot are slackers who are more likely to fall prey to the Monday flu.

But the flip side of that is what has been called "presenteeism," the irrepressible urge some people have to show up to work no matter how sick they may be.

Presenteeism may be driven by guilt, by subtle pressure not to let down co-workers, by sheer enthusiasm for the work or fear of a loss of wages for people who don't get paid sick leave.

A survey of American workers by the U.S. based National Foundation for Infectious Diseases a couple of years ago found that about a third of employees felt pressured to go to work despite being sick.

None of this bodes well for containing the spread of the H1N1 flu, which is now well-established in British Columbia.

It may be that slackers and hypochondriacs are the heroes in the battle against this global pandemic.

Dr. Perry Kendall, B.C.'s provincial health officer, says employers who don't encourage their workers to stay home when they are sick are being short-sighted.

"If they encourage them to come to work or make them feel like they should come to work, all they are going to end up with is a lot more illness in their workplace and the potential for even greater absenteeism and disruption."

The service industry may be faced with the biggest challenge, Kendall says, since workers usually can't work from home and often don't get sick leave.

As it turned out, my wife did have a cold, (dang, right again) which, health officials say, doesn't have to be a red light for people who can control the symptoms. Just don't expect your co-workers to be all that thrilled to see you.

In 1966, a patient with leukemia would almost certainly die within a year. End of story. End of cost.

Since 1987 this patient could be offered a bone marrow transplant.

The transplant itself is, of course, expensive because it has to be preceded with no end of investigations and procedures.

In all subsequent years he or she will require office visits with a family physician, twice a year visits with a local haematologist and once a year visits with a haemopathologist in Vancouver.

The transplant is not always completely compatible, and this slight incompatibility will lead to ocular problems requiring visits with an ophthalmologist and troublesome skin problems requiring a dermatologist.

Sometimes the immune system never quite gets up to par, and every one or two months the patient might need an expensive infusion of antibodies. Bone marrow transplant also leads to an increased incidence of cataracts, diabetes, thyroid disorder and kidney problems with their attendant costs.

So I have some degree of sympathy with the provincial government when medicare is rapidly becoming the major draw on diminishing provincial revenues. Since this is the reality it is essential every penny be spent wisely.

From my point of view in family medicine, this is not the case.

Recently I noted that a local walk-in clinic had closed prematurely because the attending physician had seen the maximum number of patients for which he would be paid in the one shift. Based on the arithmetic and a four-hour shift, the number of patients seen would have been 50.

That is less than four minutes and 48 seconds for the physician to take a current and past history, make an examination, discuss the findings, decide on treatment, prepare a prescription, outline future treatment plans and make a written record.

It cannot be done.

Most of these patients will end up with a subsequent visit to their family physician or another walk-in clinic -- at the cost of another office visit.

This is not good medicine, nor is it good value for the taxpayer's money. It is a very expensive $1,500 Band-Aid.

I have been reading an official information bulletin to physicians concerning the imminent epidemic of H1N1 influenza. This pandemic has the potential of completely overwhelming the system functionally and financially.

One of the principal suggestions is "Ensuring rapid access to telephone consultation and clinical evaluation for these (high risk) patients as well as patients who report severe illness."

How much better that the patients be able to speak to their own family physicians, who have access to complete medical records and knowledge of their general health status.

This would also serve the important purpose of keeping a potentially infectious person isolated from the community.

This service can no longer be provided free by the physician, and the fee need not be excessive. (Fifty per cent of the regular office fee was suggested by an arbitrator in 2001.)

The telephone time will probably take one or two hours a day, maybe even more.

The knowledge and the responsibility involved has a value -- and the cost to the physician of rent and staff will continue during this time.

This would save the cost of a visit to a walk-in clinic or physician's office.

I suggest that the provincial government overcome their long-standing prejudice against paying for telephone consultations -- if only temporarily during this potential crisis.

With safeguards (such as restricting the service to existing patient/physician relationships) this would be a cost-effective and public-health-smart way of dealing with the impending calamity -- making the most of every health-care dollar.

If I focused on hospitals, schools, business or government, I would be joining a lively and useful public debate about how prepared we are, and how prepared we should be, for a major outbreak. But charities and non-profits are too often not part of the discussion.

My question is how prepared are we to meet this potential threat to the ability of charities and non-profit organizations to deliver critical services to Canadians.

Many of our most vulnerable Canadians depend on charities and nonprofits operating in communities, large and small, across the country. Many of these organizations rely on a small number of staff and some, on only a loyal and dedicated band of volunteers. If the H1N1 virus hits Canada hard--and we don't know at this point if the impact will be mild or major--can supports and services be maintained if these organizations lose up to a third of their staff and/or volunteers to illness?

What if food banks start closing --how will desperate families feed their kids?What if meals are no longer prepared and delivered to elderly people who can't get out and who have no friends or relatives nearby to help them? What about Canadians needing dialysis or chemotherapy but who can't get to the hospital because there are no volunteers to drive them?What about the thousands of children and their families who rely on local sports and recreation and arts and cultural organizations for their weekly activities? What if the homeless shelters shut their doors in the middle of winter?

Canada has one of the largest charitable and non-profit sectors in the world. Perhaps because it is so diverse, the sector is generally underestimated. Charities and non-profits provide critical social, family and community services, often in concert with government. Among their many other activities, they help build our quality of life through sport and recreation, arts and culture, health, educational, environmental and philanthropic pursuits. And let's not forget the role they play in supporting people in developing countries around the world. They are the third pillar of Canadian society alongside governments and business.

These organizations are part of an intricate system of societal supports that make us a country that actually works, despite our many economic and social challenges. Without them, those challenges would be much worse and the quality of life of all Canadians would be profoundly diminished. Take charities and nonprofits out of the equation and we would not have the Canada that we know and value.

They are also a significant part of our economy. The sector generates more than $87 billion annually, a contribution of almost seven per cent to Canada's GDP. It employs more than 1.5 million Canadians (full-time equivalents)and mobilizes 12.5 million volunteers in Canada.

The importance of business continuity during an outbreak is being addressed. Major corporations are putting plans in place and the Public Health Agency of Canada recently announced funding for the International Centre for Infectious Diseases (ICID) to help small and medium-sized businesses prepare for a potential outbreak. That makes sense. Everyone wants our economy to keep on working.

Charities and non-profits are part of our economy too, as well as being major contributors to our quality of life. In the event of an outbreak of H1N1, so many of them will be on the front lines supporting and caring for Canadians of all ages and all backgrounds in every community across this country.

The demand many of them face is already greater given the impact of the recession and now they must prepare for the possibility of an H1N1 outbreak. What if they are not adequately prepared?

We will need them more than ever at a time of crisis. This is no time for blank looks. As a country, we need to figure out how we can help them be prepared for whatever this influenza season brings.

Marcel Lauziere is president and CEO of Imagine Canada

WATERLOO

The Liberal Party abandoned its support for the Conservatives in the House of Commons because of the Tories' terrible record on a whole slew of issues, Liberal Leader Michael Ignatieff said Saturday.

During a visit to Wilf's campus pub at Wilfrid Laurier University, Ignatieff attacked the Tories on a broad range of issues, including the rising deficit, high unemployment, its response to the H1N1 flu epidemic and its failure to fix the problem over medical isotopes.

After detailing the Tory shortcomings, Ignatieff paused for effect and asked, "Is that good enough?" The audience packed with young Liberals responded on cue with a loud "no."

As students banged thundersticks and chanted "Michael, Michael," Ignatieff took particular issue with Tory ads questioning his years of living and working in Britain and the U.S. Almost 20 per cent of Canadians were born elsewhere, he said, and two million Canadians are currently working in other countries.

"I want a country where everyone sets their sights on going overseas at some point in their lives," he said.

On the federal deficit, the Tories started with a balanced budget, which suddenly ballooned to $32 billion, then $50 billion, and now they're saying it will go to $55 billion, said Ignatieff, who spoke for about 20 minutes.

"It could be $60 billion by Christmas," he added.

To suggestions by the Conservatives that he is trying to force an election for selfish reasons, that he is "only in it for himself," Ignatieff scoffed. "I'm in it for the same reason you're in it, for Canada."

The Tories have dropped the ball on the H1NI flu epidemic, he said, noting that vaccines won't be here for another month and that body bags were callously sent to aboriginal communities.

As for medical isotopes needed for cancer tests, the reactor at Chalk River where they are produced has been shut down twice on the government's watch, he said.

Canada's status as a world leader in this technology is in jeopardy because of the Tories' negligence on this matter, he added.

After his speech, the Liberal leader mingled and chatted with students for about 10 minutes in the packed pub. Joining him at the event were local Liberal candidates Andrew Telegdi, Karen Redman and Teresa Huegle, and Guelph Liberal MP Frank Valeriote.

During a scrum with reporters afterwards, Ignatieff assailed the Tories for ignoring the pleas of Waterloo's Research In Motion and allowing the sale of Nortel Networks' wireless assets to Ericsson of Sweden.

"They (Conservatives) are just walking by the Nortel sale. They haven't stood up for the Canadian intellectual property that keeps Kitchener-Waterloo humming."

On the issue of student debt, Ignatieff said he was "disturbed" to learn in chats with students before the event that some are having to interrupt their studies to find work to pay for their education.

"We've got to relieve the burden of student debt," he said, and one of the keys to doing that is providing incentives to employers to hire "the best and brightest coming out of our colleges and universities."

In an interview on Sunday, Cambridge MP Gary Goodyear said the Liberal leader is trying to force an election that could jeopardize Canada's economic recovery.

"It's very clear to me that Mr. Ignatieff is going to stop at nothing to plunge this country into an election of an opportunistic nature."

Goodyear said the national unemployment rate of 8.7 per cent is lower than it was in the mid-1990s and the Tories plan to balance the budget by 2015.

In urging an election now, Ignatieff is "putting himself above the nation," Goodyear said.
