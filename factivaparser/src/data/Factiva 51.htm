Tom Kelly, a syndicated columnist based in Washington state, has co-authored two books about buying real estate in Mexico and Central America.

He believes prices in those areas will go up once American baby boomers recover some of their wealth.

U. S. boomers weren't prepared for the economic downturn and once they rebuild, they are going to look for a cheaper lifestyle. And it's definitely cheaper in Mexico and Central America, Kelly said.

Canadians, on the other hand, haven't been as hard hit and were better prepared, he said. So they are in a better position to buy now.

Panama, for example, "is a bargain right now," Kelly said. "But it costs money to get there."

As Ottawa's emergency planners try to mobilize hundreds of family physicians for a possible surge in flu cases this fall, it's unclear how many of the city's doctors have followed provincial guidelines to stockpile enough protective equipment to get them through a potential pandemic.

"It's definitely an unknown, and this is why we're working so aggressively," said Thomas Hayes, who chairs a committee of emergency planners from area hospitals, nursing homes and other health-care providers.

The committee, along with the city's public-health officials, is working with representatives of Ottawa's 800 family physicians on a detailed plan to assess and treat patients should there be a flu pandemic this fall.

The plan is based on the assumption that the health-care system must be prepared to respond to all types of flu because officials are no longer doing large-scale diagnostic tests to determine whether patients have the seasonal flu strains, or the H1N1 swine flu virus.

During the 2003 SARS outbreak centred in Toronto, the Ontario government sent every physician in the province a starter kit containing samples of protective masks, gloves, gowns and hand sanitizers to reduce their risk of infection when treating patients.

Since then, the province has established a pandemic plan that urges all health-care providers to keep, at their own expense, at least a month's supply of protective gear and antiviral drugs to cope during a pandemic.

Public health officials say they are attempting to gauge the number of physicians who have built up and kept such a stockpile.

"We'll at least have some kind of qualitative assessment of what's out there and how we can support them in getting what they need," said Dr. Nadine Sicard, associate medical officer of health for the City of Ottawa.

So far, the degree of readiness among Ottawa physicians seems to vary, with some appearing more prepared than others.

The Appletree Medical Group, which runs 11 primary-care clinics across the city, estimates its 120 physicians could screen and triage up to 700 patients a day during a flu pandemic.

The group has told emergency planners that if needed, it has the capacity to set up a flu assessment and treatment clinic within 48 hours of being notified.

Appletree also has a comprehensive electronic-health system, including a website and hotline, that allows patients to find clinics in the city with the shortest wait times. When patients arrive at the clinics, they can use electronic kiosks to self-register and self-triage, giving doctors an immediate picture of which patients have flu-like symptoms and need to be isolated.

Such tools are designed to allow the clinics to run even if a pandemic leaves support staff sick and unable to work, said Dr. Thom Tyson, president of Appletree.

"Our resources have been built over the past decade that connect all our practices and allow them to continue running, even during a worst-case scenario," Tyson said.

But Dr. Merrilee Fullerton, president of the Academy of Medicine Ottawa, which represents a majority of the city's family physicians, said not all physicians are willing and able to respond during a pandemic.

"Each family doctor would need to decide for him- or herself what level of care or access they are comfortable in providing," said Fullerton, a Kanata family physician.

"Some physicians may be pregnant or have chronic conditions that could make H1N1 particularly threatening to them. Others may have young families and be reluctant to bring home the virus."

Fullerton favours having flu patients sent not to their own family doctors, but to dedicated flu assessment and treatment clinics.

"This makes good sense because it keeps family doctors seeing their other patients in their clinics and prevents the co-mingling of patients with H1N1 with other patients in the office," said Fullerton.

A 2004 study of the city's family doctors, conducted a year after the outbreak of SARS respiratory illness panicked public-health authorities, suggested a majority felt they were not ready to cope during a flu pandemic. Of the 250 family doctors who responded to the survey, half described themselves as being unprepared, while another third said they were unsure.

Even so, three-quarters of the physicians who responded to the survey indicated they were willing to pitch in during a flu emergency by immunizing patients, or assessing and treating them.

Dr. William Hogg, the study's lead author, said much has changed since the survey was conducted. In particular, public-health officials have started a registry of Ottawa-area family doctors and actively share information with them -- a relationship that did not exist during the SARS epidemic.

Hogg advocates the creation of a citywide network of physicians, nurses and other primary-care providers that would work together, especially when it comes to emergency planning.

Today, however, to judge by a letter in this paper earlier this month, the discharge of slaver on public transit, despite the awareness of what diseases -- H1N1, included -- saliva can transmit, wouldn't land you within spitting distance of a courtroom, let alone a jail cell.

While on an OC Transpo bus to his Kanata home, Jim Taggart saw a teen spit on a seat. A fellow passenger notified the driver, who spoke to the offender but allowed him to return to his spot.

When Taggart reached his stop, he asked why security had not been summoned. "Well, he's gone now," the driver replied.

"There's something very wrong with that kind of behaviour in a civilized society," said Beaudry.

Does Ottawa need to return to punitive days? The city of Kingston has drawn up a regulations to ban spitting on sidewalks or in buildings, stairways or passageways. Is spitting in public becoming as big a problem as, say, breaches of cellphone etiquette? Do Canadians' expectorations approach the level of those in China, where, in a pre-Olympic crackdown, authorities in Beijing held that city's first No Spitting Day to inform residents that "spitting kills even more than an atomic bomb?"

What's remarkable is how public spitting has, in a relatively short time, become a socially enforced inhibition. As recently as the mid-1800s, spitting was considered necessary for one's health because it disposed of bodily excrement.

Yet, as author Margaret Visser notes in her 1994 book, The Way We Are, spitting, while historically tolerated, has always been regulated. "For more than 2,000 years we have been taught to recoil from slimy substances," she wrote. "Ours is a culture that is unusually determined to distinguish between liquid and solid. We easily dislike anything that falls between the two categories, especially if the substance is bodily: tears, freely running, are fine; spit or snot, which we describe as runny, are not."

(This helps explain punk's affinity for "gobbing" in the 1970s, in which fans and practitioners of the music form that embraces rebellion regularly spat upon one another -- punk rocker Siouxsie Sioux contracted conjunctivitis after a fan spat in her eye, while Clash frontman Joe Strummer blamed hepatitis on a fan's phlegm.)

With concerns over tuberculosis and other diseases rampant, spitting's popularity nose-dived by the early 1900s. Searching for the next great killer species

I cycled past a dead crow the other day and was hit by a powerful wave of nostalgia: whatever happened to the eco-scares of yesteryear, I wondered? Shouldn't we all be dead by now?

They used to be as reliable (and as hair-raising) as summer blockbusters at the local Cineplex -- horrifying tales of invasive foreign species that were undermining native ecology and, in the worst cases, threatening human life. These alarming forecasts arrived reliably every spring and, as far as I could tell, were largely spread by newspapers and television newscasts.

This year, nothing. Or hardly anything. Another sign, perhaps, of the declining influence of print journalism. There is passing reference in a recent Globe and Mail story to a new "killer shrimp" -- the bloody red mysid, a tiny aquatic creature recently spotted in the Great Lakes, that is said to be "ravenous," and to "travel in packs." All the elements, in other words, of a great eco-scare: sinister "alien" origins, creepy name, prolific reproduction, insatiable appetite. Alas, Googling the words "killer shrimp" mostly turns up recipes from California seafood joints.

As for dead crows, they were associated with the 1999 discovery of West Nile virus in New York City -- Africa, apparently, being an inexhaustible wellspring of gruesome diseases. The virus was actually spread by mosquitoes, not crows, and it killed hundreds of people in the United States at its peak in 2003.

There were victims in Canada, too, although fatalities have been declining markedly. Indeed, four of five people bitten by infected mosquitoes experience no symptoms at all, a factoid usually relegated to the scare story's fine print. (Not that I'm suggesting these imported illness don't cause tragedy; just that their impact is invariably oversold.)

There are still occasional cases of West Nile reported and, anywhere there are mosquitoes, lingering concerns. The specific culprit -- the Culex tarsalis mosquito --prefers hot weather, which hasn't been an issue this summer. "Often on a nice warm evening, just as you're finishing the soccer game, you notice mosquitoes biting your ankles -- that's often Culex tarsalis," Saskatchewan provincial entomologist Phil Curry explained. (An obvious precaution: wear socks.)

My favourite eco-scare -- patient zero, perhaps, of the modern genre -- is killer bees. They were the product of a 1957 experiment-gone-wrong in Brazil, when 26 aggressive African Queen bees escaped a research compound, mated with milder European bees, and began a relentless expansion north. En route, they swarmed Mexican farmhands, killed innocent pets and dominated headlines in the early '90s.

Since then, they have lost their sting -- stalled at the Louisiana border by cold winters, their reputation rehabilitated, somewhat, by selective breeding and a name change. They are now called "Africanized bees" and lauded by beekeepers for their gentle nature. They are still creating a buzz, however, judging by the number of sports teams bearing the moniker Killer Bees.

The Great Lakes remains a cauldron of unwelcome visitors -- home, by some estimates, to at least 57 alien species.

The most famous is the zebra mussel, still cutting swimmers' feet, clogging pipes and temporarily disabling nuclear power plants around Lake Ontario. The ugliest is the round goby, described as an extremely aggressive bottom-feeder that even eats zebra mussels. Because this frankenfish, with its buggy eyes and mottled colour, is prone to botulism, it can infect other unlucky species, including loons, that order goby for lunch.

Since 2006, freighters entering the Great Lakes have been required to flush their ballast, which is intended to slow or halt the introduction of non-native invaders -- although how any species can survive the industrial pollution in the Great Lakes is a scientific wonder in itself. Humans, after all, have done more damage than any hapless mollusk.

On land, the invasive purple loosestrife -- that vivid popsicle of colour waving brightly from every country ditch -- remains on the watch-list, despite the introduction of two leaf-eating beetles, a root-mining weevil and a seed weevil, intended to contain its spread.

In Charlottetown, P.E.I., two women have been hired to dig up loosestrife along the city's Confederation Trail this summer. The work is supported by the federal Invasive Alien Species Partnership, a program also helping Nova Scotia scientists and university students monitor the rugged coastline of Cape Breton. They are girding for a multi-pronged assault: watching for European green crab, Asian shore crab, Chinese mitten crab, Japanese skeleton shrimp, lacy crust bryozan, sea squirts, oyster thief or dead man's fingers, spineycreek crayfish and rock snot.

Maybe the media have exhausted the annual allotment of alarming headlines on the swine flu -- now menacing summer camps, faithful to any Hollywood script -- and the recession. But you'd think dead man's fingers would at least warrant a mention.

H1N1's oink is proving to be far worse than its bite; Latest flu outbreak is shaping up as fourth pandemic dud in the past six years
Dr. Richard Schabas
Medical Officer of Health in Hastings and Prince Edward Counties

Toronto is gripped in a frenzy of worry about the dreaded "second wave" of H1N1 now scheduled for this fall. A severe "second wave" of H1N1 is possible, in the same sense that it's possible the Blue Jays will win the World Series this year. Science and public policy need to look beyond possibilities and also consider probabilities. Our appreciation of probabilities should be based on evidence, not speculation.

The evidence strongly suggests that a severe "second wave" of H1N1 is very unlikely. It will almost certainly be merely the latest instalment in a growing list of pandemic false alarms.

Let's begin by putting this warning in some context. This is the fourth pandemic alarm in the past six years. The first three have been wrong.

The first alarm was about SARS. At the time, pundits predicted that SARS would become a pandemic and that more than 100 million people would die. Wrong. SARS died out because it was not really very infectious outside of hospitals.

The second alarm was for H5N1 "bird flu." We were told that this disease would leap across the species barrier and cause a devastating human pandemic. More than a billion people were supposed to die in an imminent catastrophe of unprecedented dimensions. Wrong. H5N1 remains a disease of birds that rarely infects people who live in close contact with birds. There is no scientific reason to expect this to change.

The third alarm was for the dreaded "first wave" of H1N1. All of our pandemic planning had been directed toward this "first wave." It was supposed to hit fast and hard. Eight to 12 million Canadians were supposed to fall ill over two to three months. Between 10,000 and 50,000 Canadians were supposed to die. H1N1 may have hit quickly but in public health terms it has not hit hard. Regular seasonal influenza kills 2,000 to 4,000 Canadians every year. H1N1 has killed fewer than 50 people in Canada in its "first wave."

So now we are warned about the H1N1 "second wave." How serious is the risk? There are three general arguments supporting the "second wave" hypothesis. None of them stand up well to scrutiny.

The first argument is historical, based on the 1918 "Spanish Flu" pandemic. In the spring of 1918 there was a relatively mild "first wave" of influenza followed by a much more severe "second wave" in the fall. The problem is that the cause of influenza was a mystery in 1918. The influenza virus itself was not discovered until 1933. There is no reliable basis to conclude that the two 1918 "waves" were caused by the same virus. Based on everything we have learned about influenza in the last 75 years, it is much more likely that the mild 1918 "first wave" was caused by a different influenza virus. This argument is more folklore than science.

The second argument warns that H1N1 could "mutate" and become more virulent. Mutations are extremely common in all viruses, indeed in all living organisms. Important sustainable behavioural changes caused by mutations are, however, very, very rare. Mutations are just the raw material of evolution. Natural selection favours micro-organisms that don't kill their hosts. Evolutionary trends in infectious diseases are consistently in the direction of lower virulence. Our experience with influenza and every other infectious disease supports this. The mutation argument is science-fiction.

The third argument is that H1N1 will be more severe when it hits during our normal flu season - the cold, dry months of winter. This hypothesis is, at least, coherent. It is also testable. Our eyes should be glued on the southern hemisphere where H1N1 is now active during their normal flu season. And the news is good. Australia is now two months into its H1N1 "first wave" and it hasn't been substantially more severe than what we faced. Furthermore, the data now suggest the Australian outbreak is ending. The seasonal hypothesis may be reasonable but the evidence is against it.

Why is H1N1 so mild? I think the reason is becoming increasingly obvious. It relates to the demographics of this outbreak.

Influenza is a benign and short-lived illness in the great majority of people infected. Seasonal influenza is an important public health problem because of the secondary complications of influenza - most typically pneumonia. These complications occur almost exclusively in older people, particularly those with underlying chronic illnesses. Influenza can cause serious illness in young people but this is rare.

Most of the serious illness from H1N1 is in relatively young people. The pundits are presenting this as bad news. It is actually very good news.

The key epidemiological characteristic of H1N1 is that it is not affecting older people. By and large, people over the age of 50 appear to be protected, probably because of exposure to similar H1N1 viruses prior to 1957. This is why H1N1 is such a pandemic dud. The people who should be dying just aren't getting sick. There is no reason to think this is going to change.

The proportion of serious illnesses in young people is high by default. The actual number of these cases in young people is actually very, very small. Is H1N1 more serious in young people than seasonal influenza? Maybe, but even if it is it's not very much more severe. We won't know for sure until we know the actual infection rates in this age group.

So, we should relax and enjoy the summer. H1N1 may well return in the fall but we should expect attack rates to be modest and the incidence of serious illness to be very low. There are lots of good reasons to get Toronto municipal workers back on the job but urgent pandemic planning is not one of them.

I'll end with a challenge to the media. The media love this story and accept the pundits' gloomy predictions uncritically. If this turns out to be the fourth pandemic false alarm in six years, as I think it will, it will be time to start asking some probing questions.

Dr. Richard Schabas was Ontario's Chief Medical Officer of Health from 1987-97.

It seems as though it was only yesterday. What began as a story of isolated cases of a potentially lethal respiratory illness evolved into a full-fledged global epidemic known as severe acute respiratory syndrome (SARS). I was out West skiing when I heard of the first case of a Toronto man dying from the illness. By the time I returned to Toronto, more people were hospitalized with the disease and more deaths were occurring. Soon, there was profound confusion as no one seemed prepared for what was happening. Hospitals operated in vacuums, no coordinated community-based approach was in sight, and multiple mixed messages were being sent out from different sources to various stakeholders.

Professionally, these were trying times. Often gowned, gloved and always masked, I found it difficult to interact with my patients because I felt separated from them. For me, the dynamics of patient care in the emergency department, the delivery room and my office changed dramatically. The day-long use of masks made me feel ill and tired. Furthermore, I was politically involved in the events that transpired through my role as chair of Family Physicians Toronto -- a grass-roots group that had been established to address the needs of the community. This was the group that demanded and formulated a response to the community-based needs during SARS.

At a personal level, while I was resigned to the possibility that I might become infected, I was constantly worried about bringing SARS into my household. I was obviously concerned about the health of my family. What was particularly gnawing was the sense that there was no end in sight.

All this is just to give an appropriate context to what follows -- an attempt to provide some balance to the myriad media reports suggesting an impending fall epidemic that is bound to have a catastrophic effect. If nothing else, SARS taught me not to be complacent. In fact, for a period of time after SARS, at different venues, I expressed concern regarding the apparent apathy that seemed to prevail. This was the theme in a presentation given to the SARS Commission.

But, in spite of the dire predictions related to H1N1 influenza, I am much less worried now. This is not to say that I do not have some concerns. I still believe governments and health officials have not given enough thought to certain funding issues and to concerns related to community-based leadership.

However, I am also aware that a lot of effort is being put into developing strategies to address the needs at varying levels should a pandemic strike. A unified template for how hospitals and the multitude of community providers and agencies should respond has been established. Vaccinations are being processed. Leadership is being put in place. The basics of proper infection control are widely disseminated. These are all good things.

The message is clear. Vigilance and awareness are important. To be hygienically careful (and to teach our children) is wise. But to be paralyzed by fear is counterproductive and truly unwarranted. I have no doubt that we are better prepared than we were for SARS. And that's most reassuring.

Statistics Canada had its crack at reporting the latest inflation rate. Now it's my turn.

Let's see. The price of bus tickets here in Ottawa has just surged to $1.15 from $1, or 15 per cent (this after a three-month bus strike this past winter, but never mind). The monthly cable and Internet bill is up 4.2 per cent from earlier this year. The annual car and home insurance bills, which I paid in the past couple of months, were up 15.8 and 6.9 per cent, respectively, from a year ago. Property taxes, which I finished paying off last month, were up just under 4 per cent from 2008.

Gasoline is about the only thing I can think of that has fallen noticeably and had an impact on our family cash flow. The price for regular gas in Ottawa was about 88 cents a litre yesterday, down from $1.36 a year ago.

Depending on how you read Statscan's inflation report last Friday, the cost of living either fell a little bit in June or rose by a routine amount. Regardless of which view you choose, we're seeing the word deflation – falling prices, in very simple terms – being thrown around a fair bit these days, both here and globally.

Meanwhile, inflation is far from dead at our house. In fact, I have to wonder as a bill payer if I'm being squeezed to make up for revenue shortfalls caused by the very recession that is supposedly causing a risk of deflation.

The 0.3-per-cent decline in the headline inflation rate in June was notable because it was the first monthly year-over-year drop in 15 years. The biggest contributor to this decline: energy. In fact, if you took energy out of the calculation, inflation actually moved up 2.1 per cent last month.

While the all-in cost of living fell in June, this is not true deflation. For that, we need sustained declines in prices that cause consumers to stop buying things in the hope of being rewarded for their patience with lower costs.

A deflationary period in Japan from the late 1990s to the middle of this decade caused a long period of economic stagnation. From an investment point of view, deflation would be a disaster because it would entail ultralow interest rates coupled with poor stock markets.

With energy prices being the prime driver of lower living costs last month, many economists don't believe we face any risk of true deflation.

“The whole deflation topic has been blown out of proportion in the media,” said Krishen Rangasamy, an economist at CIBC World Markets.

But that doesn't mean the matter is settled.

Check out money manager Eric Sprott's July investment commentary (go to Sprott.com and check under Markets at a Glance). He and his colleague David Franklin never actually use the word deflation, but they provide a bleak economic outlook in the United States that could lead to falling prices. Their evidence includes things like declining retail sales and tax revenue and idle industrial production.

“We are now in the early stages of a depression,” they write. “The economic indicators we follow to track real economic activity are all signalling a slowdown of massive proportions.”

Deflation is already threatening in Japan for a second time this decade. If swine flu fares up again when the weather cools down, there's speculation that it could nudge a struggling global economy into deflation.

Still, at ground level where people work, spend and pay bills, signs of deflation are tough to find. CIBC's Mr. Rangasamy sees shelter costs falling some more because of mortgage rates and declines in the cost of natural gas, which is used in home heating. He also mentioned car prices as an example of a product where prices have been falling.

Got the picture? If you're not buying a new car, or if you haven't set up or renewed a mortgage, you're missing out on two major categories of price declines.

It all comes down to energy, then. If you drive a lot, your personal inflation rate may be down. If you don't, then you may be paying more for goods and services at a time when the term deflation is being tossed around.

Let's remember that true deflation is marked by the reluctance of consumers to buy things because they expect better deals ahead. That's the opposite of what's happening in two prominent areas. Far from waiting to buy homes, people are jumping back into the market. New car sales are still down, but the pace of decline has been easing.

Rule out nothing with an economic slump as nasty as the one we're in now. But worry about deflation? At our house, the bills coming in tell us inflation's the bigger problem.
