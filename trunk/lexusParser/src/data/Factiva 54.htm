Tourism spending fell 1.3 per cent in the first three months of this year, Statistics Canada said, marking the third straight quarterly decline. In total, spending fell 2.4 per cent over the last three quarters.

"This was the first time tourism spending has fallen for three consecutive quarters since 2001, when the tourism sector, already in a downturn, was hit by the events of Sept. 11 and their aftermath," the federal agency said.

"Subsequently, with the severe acute respiratory syndrome outbreak, tourism demand fell 5.8 per cent over the first and second quarters of 2003."

Randy Williams, president of the Tourism Industry Association of Canada, said the declining numbers can be mostly attributed to the weakened North American economy.

"(Travelling) is discretionary spending, for leisure or business, and in many cases, people are postponing trips to when they'll be more affordable," said Williams.

David Redekop, an analyst with the Canadian Tourism Research Institute, said the actual situation is "not as bad as it appears."

He said domestic travel is actually "holding up quite well," and much of the decline in this area is due to lower prices for things such as hotel rooms rather than a lack of activity.

Spending by international visitors fell 5.7 per cent in the first quarter -- the 14th drop in 17 quarters, "and the sharpest decline since the SARS outbreak during the second quarter of 2003," Statistics Canada said.

Redekop said the international numbers largely reflect the long-term trend of declining travel from the U.S., and it's expected to get worse with new requirements that American visitors to Canada have a passport or other accepted document in order to return home by land.

Foreign visitors to Canada cut their spending in all areas in the first quarter, Statistics Canada said, particularly transportation and food-and-beverage services, which were both down eight per cent for the quarter.

Canadians's domestic travel spending was down 0.1 per cent in the first quarter.

"This was the first time domestic tourism spending has declined for three consecutive quarters since 2001," it said.

Due to a lack of residents travelling abroad, spending for air travel in this country was reduced by 2.5 per cent in the quarter, Statistics Canada said.

These latest tourism figures do not take into account any effect that might come from the recent swine-flu scare. But Redekop said this is not expected to be a major factor for the tourism industry.

Pregnant women and those with underlying risk conditions, such as chronic heart or lung disease, should avoid crowds to reduce the risk of being exposed to human swine flu, federal health officials said Monday.

Canada's chief public health officer says there is no evidence to suggest pregnant women are at greater risk of contracting the H1N1 influenza A virus. But, if they do become ill, pregnant women -- especially those in the third trimester -- are at higher risk of serious complications.

As women get closer to their due dates, their immune systems change, making them more vulnerable to serious illness -- not just from influenza, but from other infections as well, Dr. David Butler-Jones said. That can put stress on the fetus, and, in rare cases, increase the risk of an early birth or fetal death.

"It is very important for pregnant women to take precautions to help prevent infection in the first place and to seek medical advice if they do develop influenza-like illness," he said Monday.

"People with underlying risk conditions and pregnant women should consider avoiding crowds to reduce the risk of exposure to the virus," he added.

"You need to be practical about getting on with life," Butler-Jones said. But he said pregnant women should avoid situations where they would be close to crowds for prolonged periods.

Butler-Jones also said Monday that serious cases are also occurring in people who were healthy "before catching this virus" and that the infection rapidly progressed into severe illness requiring ventilators.

"We're tracking these cases very closely, trying to understand from a medical standpoint why they would be affected this way by the virus, and whether this is a signal that the virus itself is changing and becoming more virulent," or if other factors are at play, he said.

Butler-Jones also warned that in addition to a fever and a cough, anyone who develops serious respiratory problems, such as shortness of breath, or difficulty breathing, should seek immediate medical care.

The risk of children picking up swine flu at daycare or day camps is generally low, he said, similar to what it would be in schools.

However, the risk might be higher in sleep-away camps, where children live in close quarters and for prolonged periods.

"The message of prevention is critical, at school, camp, daycare, home or at work," Butler-Jones said. "That means washing hands, coughing or sneezing into your arm and avoiding others when we're ill. Not just today, not just for this outbreak, but always."

Federal Health Minister Leona Aglukkaq will be meeting with her international counterparts and World Health Organization officials in Mexico this week to discuss H1N1.

Overall, the vast majority of cases in Canada appear to be mild, "but we are seeing some pockets of more severe illness," Butler-Jones said.

As heightened surveillance continues, "we can expect case numbers to continue to increase and, given the sheer numbers of people who are catching this virus, we can also expect to see more severe cases and, unfortunately, more deaths as well."

Preliminary data shows that the majority of those infected are under the age of 20, and that relatively few people over age 65 have contracted the virus. Older people normally account for about a quarter of infections during regular seasonal flu, "so this is quite different," Butler-Jones said.

He said that once a vaccine is available, the logic would be to provide the vaccine to those who are most likely to get it, or get severely ill.

TORONTO

Canadian researchers have designed a system based on air travel patterns to predict how infectious diseases will spread around the world, offering a means of halting transmission by taking preventive measures as soon as an outbreak occurs.

The system, developed by infectious disease physician Dr. Kamran Khan and colleagues, accurately predicted how the H1N1 flu virus would circulate worldwide after arising in Mexico earlier this year.

Using their BIO.DIASPORA system, the team analyzed the flight itineraries of more than 2.3 million passengers who departed Mexico on commercial flights during March and April 2008 to predict the spread of H1N1 a year later.

The findings show the international destinations of air travellers leaving Mexico -- including cities in Canada -- were strongly associated with confirmed importations of the virus around the world.

"We know that infectious diseases don't respect national boundaries, but if there is one thing they have to respect as they're spreading around the globe, it's the architecture of the airline transportation system,'' said Khan of St. Michael's Hospital in Toronto.

"This is their mechanism for spreading around the globe.''

Khan said he came up with the idea of using the air traffic grid to better understand the global transmission of infectious disease after the 2003 SARS crisis. That outbreak killed more than 770 people, including 44 Canadians.

"It just became so obvious how the airline transportation network was a major conduit of the spread of infectious diseases,'' he said, noting that SARS arose in China's Guangdong province and it "didn't take long for it to be in 26 countries.''

In what Khan calls a case of "bizarre timing,'' days before he and his team were to deliver a report on BIO.DIASPORA to the Public Health Agency of Canada (PHAC), news began trickling out about a new flu virus in Mexico. Their system allowed them to track its spread as it quickly infiltrated populations in one country after another.

"The movement of passengers out of Mexico aligned very nicely with the observed spread of H1N1,'' said Khan, principal author of a paper on the initiative published online this week by the New England Journal of Medicine.

John Brownstein, an infectious disease epidemiologist at Harvard University, said that while it's long been known that influenza is spread by air passengers, using flight information to track H1N1's movement "was a very unique and interesting aspect of the study.''

"The data that the authors use is widely available, so although it has a Canadian-centric view, which is obviously important . . . this type of modelling strategy is definitely generalizable to other countries,'' Brownstein, who was not involved in the study, said yesterday from Boston.

The obvious next step, said Khan, is to use the system for prevention. Canada is particularly vulnerable to infectious disease threats via air travel and because of our proximity to the United States, he pointed out.

"Collectively, when we look at Canada and the United States, we generate and receive about one-sixth of the entire world's international air traffic. That's a lot for two countries.''

Brownstein, who has studied the global circulation of influenza via air travel, said he doesn't believe any system can stop an infectious disease like flu, because passengers can be incubating an infection but show no symptoms.

"I think . . . these models can help prioritize and help prepare, but they can't necessarily at the end of the day prevent the introduction of this influenza.''

Rich, 55, has delivered that warning for five years as founder of the Center on Media and Child Health at Children's Hospital in Boston.

Q: What's your main theme?

A: That children's media use changes them. I'd like to see it approached with the same level of scrutiny and self-awareness that we approach their nutrition or education because it has as profound an affect. It's like the air they breathe and the water they drink.

Q: Why the new website?

A: I spend a lot of time teaching pediatricians, teachers and parents around the country what the data are. Consistently after I speak, the stage gets rushed by people with very specific questions about their kid - "My 8-year-old wants a cellphone. A lot aren't, in part because it doesn't rise to the top when you're dealing with nutrition, growth and development, immunization, swine flu or whatever the latest fear is. I think it needs to. In many ways, this is the environmental health issue of the media age.

EVERY FIVE MINUTES

Watching your portfolio like a hawk may seem like the responsible thing to do, but it's a recipe for stress. Why? It has to do with basic math, and the way the human brain processes winning and losing.

In his book, Fooled by Randomness, Nassim Taleb provides a good illustration. Suppose your portfolio generates an average return of 15 per cent and has volatility of 10 per cent annually. Given these assumptions, your portfolio has a 93-per-cent chance of rising in any given year.
