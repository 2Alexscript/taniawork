Ever since the Reserve Bank of Australia (RBA) raised its benchmark interest rate by a quarter-percentage point to 3.25 per cent on Oct. 7, investors have been gaga over the Land Down Under.

In a world of minuscule and moribund interest rates, the Aussies are the only industrialized market where rates are rising – a tasty morsel for an investing world starving for better rates of return.

The question is, how can a little investor in Canada (where our own central bank's benchmark rate is a puny 0.25 per cent and going nowhere) take a bite out of the juicy Aussie largesse? Here are a few possibilities:

Open a savings account at an Australian bank, though most personal accounts carry residency requirements. And there's a 10-per-cent non-residency tax on interest earnings that will make a serious dent in your returns. Buy Australian dollars, which will likely remain market darlings as long as the RBA is raising rates while other central banks sit on their hands. But simply holding the Aussie buck won't pay you interest.

Buy Australian government bonds (easy to do through a full-service broker). But beware steep service fees for foreign transactions.

Buy Australian-dollar futures contracts. This will give you the benefit of both the rising Aussie interest rates and the rising currency – and you can do it via the Internet.

Futures contracts have interest rates built into their prices.

A contract to take delivery on the Australian currency in March, 2010, for example, is priced about two percentage points below the current exchange rate, to compensate for the interest you forgo by agreeing to take delivery on the currency next March rather than now.

You earn the interest payment up front – you're essentially paying only 98 Australian dollars to buy 100.

This option does expose you to currency risk, but that would be a bonus if the Australian dollar goes up.

You not only make money on the rate differential, but cash in on foreign-exchange gains.

Is that a real AK-47?

The Russian maker of the Kalashnikov rifle, or AK-47, says it will move to protect the patent of the widely copied gun. The Kalashnikov has become the world's most popular weapon, with some 100 million made over 60 years, but only about half are the genuine article. The company says it loses as much as $500-million (U.S.) a year to copies.

The real Kalashnikovs

The Russian company Izhmash makes the rifle in the Ural Mountains city of Izhevsk. Venezuela, which struck a deal to buy 100,000 rifles and produce more under licence, is the only fully legitimate licence holder.

The counterfeits

About 30 foreign manufacturers make Kalashnikovs. The former Soviet Union paid little attention to copyright laws, easily handing out arms production licences to its satellites in Eastern Europe and elsewhere. The Cold War-era licences have long-since expired, but production has continued.

The Chinese market

The Soviet Union provided China with weapons technology until the two Communist giants turned into bitter rivals in the 1960s. Moscow and Beijing rebuilt their ties in the early 1990s, and China again became a top customer for Russian weapons, purchasing billions of dollars worth of fighter jets, missiles, destroyers and submarines. Only one Quebecer in three is eager to get vaccinated against the H1N1 swine flu virus, according to a recent survey. If these figures are correct, governments have serious work to do.

The vaccine is the best way, perhaps the only way, you can protect yourself - and everyone else - from this dangerous disease. Vaccine-avoidance is both unwise and dangerous.

So far, the swine flu has killed 4,735 people around the world, and infected hundreds of thousands more people in 191 countries. Most victims do recover on their own, without medical intervention, as with an ordinary flu.

But for a minority of the people who catch this bug, it's a much scarier scenario. In some patients, the virus mounts a fierce attack on the respiratory tract. These patients need hospital treatment and have to be hooked up to mechanical ventilation for several days.

Studies in the United States have found that the number of patients requiring mechanical ventilation is increasing "six times faster than the rest of hospitalizations," a University of Massachusetts scientist told Bloomberg.com. Health officials worldwide fear that most countries don't have nearly enough equipment to handle a surge in such serious H1N1 cases.

That's why those officials are all but unanimous: The vaccine against H1N1 cannot be delivered fast enough, especially to those at higher risk, including children, pregnant women, heart patients, the obese, and younger people.

As many as half of unexpected flu deaths are among patients with pre-existing heart disease, according to a recent study published in the Lancet. Those researchers urged everyone with heart disease to be sure to be vaccinated.

Despite all this, a surprising number of people around the world irrationally fear the H1N1 vaccine.

Getting the vaccine is voluntary, but as you can see there are powerful reasons to get the shot. As with any epidemic, transmission of this flu could be quick and unpredictable. If the malady gets into the whole population, our medical institutions could easily be overwhelmed. The disruptions to schools and businesses, public services, and daily life could be severe even for the healthy.

"I never get the flu" is no reason to ignore this menace. That statement means only "I haven't had the flu yet." There are no guarantees against H1N1.

Vaccination will be available, free of charge, to all in Quebec, starting as early as next week.

If you still have doubts about the vaccine, discuss the question with your family physician or someone at your neighbourhood clinic.

Ontarians in high-risk groups will get the H1N1 vaccine starting Monday as part of Canada's largest-ever immunization campaign, after the federal government approved the vaccine Wednesday.

Some 722,000 doses have been shipped to public health units around Ontario, and they will be distributed within days to family physicians as well as health-care facilities.

The identified high-risk groups include adults under the age of 65 with chronic conditions, health-care workers, caregivers for high-risk groups, people living in remote and isolated communities, pregnant women and healthy children six months to five years of age, Dr. Arlene King, Ontario's chief medical officer of health, said in an interview in Toronto.

King said the vaccine "is our most effective weapon in fighting H1N1. We are ready, we are prepared, and we are going to win this battle one shot at a time."

Health Minister Leona Aglukkaq, in announcing approval of the vaccine in Ottawa, said, "I encourage all Canadians to get vaccinated because it is the best way to protect our health and the health of our loved ones."

Two million doses of the vaccine had already been shipped to the provinces and territories, awaiting Aglukkaq's go-ahead. Other countries have already begun vaccinations.

Local health authorities will ask people at lower risk to wait until people at higher risk get the shots. But Canada's chief public health officer, Dr. David Butler-Jones, acknowledged the vaccinations are "not a rationing exercise" and no one will be turned away.

"We're not going to spend a lot of time trying to go through people's medical history. We want to get as many people immunized as possible," he said. "I think Canadians have shown, time and time again, their respect for the most vulnerable."

Ottawa has agreed to shield drug companies from lawsuits over the H1N1 pandemic vaccine. That means the federal government, not the vaccine manufacturers, would have to pay any damages awarded in court, except in cases of malpractice.

Canadian clinical trials of the vaccine are still underway, but the federal regulator was satisfied enough with the results of thousands of clinical trials in Europe to approve the drug in Canada.

"This authorization was arrived at independently and on the basis of sound health science," said Dr. Elwyn Griffiths, head of the section of Health Canada that regulates vaccines. "This vaccine is both safe and effective."

Roll-out dates will vary across the country. The federal government aims to ship about three million doses a week as the vaccine rolls off the production line.

Pharmaceutical giant GlaxoSmithKline has a contract to produce 50.4 million doses of pandemic vaccine at its facility in Ste-Foy, Que.

In Ontario, those outside the high-risk groups may have to wait until mid-November or December to get the vaccine, depending on how quickly Ottawa replenishes Ontario's stocks, King said.

Some pregnant women, who were supposed to be at the front of the line for the vaccine, may also have to wait. The H1N1 shot intended for pregnant women is not expected to be available for at least two more weeks, King said.

Expectant mothers more than 20 weeks into their pregnancy, or who have underlying medical conditions such as asthma and diabetes, should therefore talk to their doctor about getting the vaccine that's already arrived, she said.

Healthy women in the first half of their pregnancy should wait for the flu shot that doesn't contain an adjuvant - a natural additive that boosts the impact of a vaccine - because there's less risk they'll develop complications from the flu, King said.

The unadjuvanted vaccine won't be available until early November, and even then, it takes 10 to 14 days to take effect, she said. "I don't know how much more flu activity we will have at that time, but it could be significant," she said. "And I think on balance this is the best recommendation I can make."

Have you got H1N1?

Having fever, cough, sore throat, body aches, headache, chills and fatigue, particularly fever and cough, likely means you have H1N1.

How to treat it

Stay home from school or work, but it's safe to return when you're 24 hours without fever.

High-risk candidates should contact their doctors for antiviral medication. Seek immediate medical help if you have shortness of breath, difficulty breathing or chest pain.

Self-care is key: drink plenty of fluids, avoid caffeine, use basic pain or fever meds, gargle with warm water.

Some facts about the vaccine for the H1N1 flu, approved Wednesday for national distribution.

What is it:

A vaccine developed by GlaxoSmithKline in Ste-Foy, a district of Quebec City.

When available:

Provinces may begin administering their stockpiles of vaccine as early as next week. About two million doses shipped to provinces and territories, with between two million and 3.5 million more doses to be shipped each week. GlaxoSmithKline has contracted to provide 50.4 million doses.

Additives:

The current vaccine contains an adjuvant, a compound that boosts the immune response to the vaccine and stretches supplies by using smaller doses per person. There's currently no licensed seasonal flu vaccine containing adjuvant in Canada, although adjuvants have been used for years in Europe in flu vaccines targeted at seniors.

Unadjuvanted vaccines:

No data on the use of adjuvanted flu vaccines in either pregnant women or young children. The government has ordered 1.8 million doses of unadjuvanted vaccines for their use. The unadjuvanted products will be shipped separately from the ordinary vaccines; no word yet on when they will be available.

Pandemic vaccine and seasonal flu shot:

An unpublished Canadian study suggests getting a seasonal flu shot may double a person's risk of catching H1N1. Data from several other countries doesn't show the same association, and the World Health Organization isn't suggesting countries change their vaccination plans because of the study.

Who should get vaccinated:

Everyone 10 years of age or older should receive one dose of adjuvanted vaccine. Children between six months and 10 years should received the adjuvanted vaccine in two half-doses, administered at least 21 days apart. Pregnant women are advised to get one dose of unadjuvanted vaccine, though if they are 20 weeks or more into their pregnancy and the unadjuvanted vaccine isn't available, they are advised to get the adjuvanted shot. Infants less than six months old aren't vaccinated for influenza.

Next week, a new vaccine will be distributed to local hospitals, walk-in clinics and doctors.

Those doses are meant for people at greatest risk of complications from H1N1.

They include pregnant women, children under 5, those with chronic medical conditions, health-care workers, and caregivers for infants under six months old - who can't be immunized - and people with weakened immune systems.

The following week, starting Nov. 3, the public health department will begin holding general H1N1 immunization clinics throughout the region.

In the meantime, Wang said the best precautions are the same ones urged every flu season - frequent handwashing, coughing into a tissue or sleeve, and staying home from work or school if you're ill.

The new strain is making headlines because it spread so quickly around the world and hits more young people, but Wang said its symptoms are generally no worse than seasonal flu and H1N1 actually has a much lower mortality rate.

As a result, she said, people shouldn't overreact or seek medical help merely to find out if they have it.

The mildest pandemics of the 20th century killed at least a million people worldwide. After six months, swine flu has killed about as many people as the seasonal flu does every six days.

So how could WHO make such an outrageous claim?

Simple. It rewrote the definition of "pandemic."

A previous official definition required "simultaneous epidemics worldwide with enormous numbers of deaths and illness." Severity is crucial because seasonal flu always causes worldwide simultaneous epidemics. But the definition promulgated in April eliminated severity as a factor.

That's also how we can have a "pandemic" when six months of epidemiological data show swine flu to be far milder than the seasonal variety. New York City statistics show it to be perhaps a 10th as lethal.

Swine flu isn't some sort of alien from outer space as we've been led to believe, but rather "the same subtype as seasonal A/H1N1 that has been circulating since 1977," as the BMJ observes. It's "something our immune systems have seen before," echoes Peter Palese of New York's Mount Sinai School of Medicine.

Nevertheless, because WHO dubbed this a "pandemic," vaccination plans, emergency response measures and frightening predictions have been based on comparisons with true pandemics that by definition were especially severe. That includes the August report from the President's Council of Advisors on Science and Technology with its "plausible scenario" of "30,000 -- 90,000 [U. S.] deaths" peaking in "mid-October."

Check your calendar. So why did WHO do it?

In part, because it was losing credibility over the refusal of avian flu H5N1 to go pandemic and kill as many as 150 million people worldwide, as the WHO's "flu czar" had predicted in 2005. Around the world, nations stockpiled antiviral medicines and H5N1 vaccine.

So when pig flu conveniently appeared, the WHO essentially crossed out "avian," inserted "swine," and WHO Director-General Margaret Chan could boast: "The world can now reap the benefits of investments over the last five years in pandemic preparedness."

Yet this doesn't explain why the agency hyped avian flu in the first place, nor why it exaggerated HIV infections by more than 10 times, or why it spread hysteria over Severe Acute Respiratory Syndrome(SARS). That disease ultimately killed a day's worth of seasonal flu victims before vanishing.

But the SARS scare was enough, leading to a broad expansion of WHO powers, including a degree of direct authority over national health agencies. It's now using that to leverage more authority and a bigger budget. No shocker there.

Splendid! So let's put the WHO in charge of worldwide economic and social engineering.

Then let's form a new agency that sees disease as something to prevent and treat rather than something to exploit.

One emerging trend in academia might be best labelled "smorgasbord-style," although it's also sometimes called self-directed or discovery learning. Essentially, the idea is to offer the curriculum in a variety of formats -- lectures, textbooks, PowerPoint slides, video of the professor, audio podcasts--and let the students discover for themselves how best to absorb the material.

"Every student learns a different way," says Beverly Eastham, vice-president (external) for the U of A Students' Union. "Some like traditional lectures. Others never want to go, but if you put that lecture on a podcast, they'll take it in."

Examples of this are already widespread. Many professors now post lecture notes and PowerPoint presentations online, while others provide some educational extras, such as sample exams or practice problems. In fact, professors who offer none of this digital-age content tend to be unpopular with students, even if they are exceptional orators.

"Students do hope for a prof who will put at least some stuff online. If they don't, you'll hear comments like 'Oh great, one of these guys,' " Ayton says.

"It's helpful to have access to that because then you're not forced to write down everything in a 40-minute lecture," adds MacEwan biology student Leah Elder. "We are the technology generation, so it's ingrained in us to use the Internet whenever possible. Around mid-terms, I'm on there (studying) all the time."

Recently, the U of A's medical school went further by creating a comprehensive, web-based platform called Homer, which has essentially wiped out the need for paper. Students can boot up their laptops and get all of their course materials electronically, from video clips showing how an X-ray analysis is done to coloured diagrams on limb fractures.

It's possible such a system could eventually become standard for all university-level programs, however some administrators remain skeptical of such a move. What works for highly motivated medical students may not work for all students, especially those in first and second year who may still need guidance with their learning habits, says University of Calgary provost Alan Harrison.

A smorgasbord-style approach may also be somewhat incompatible with liberal arts seminars such as history and English, which are based more on interpretation and discussion than facts. Others worry that a selection of electronic aids will convince students they can skip class entirely, although some professors get around this by uploading only portions of their material online.

---

As part of its smorgasbord of features, Homer has been designed as a community-learning model because it encourages students and instructors to interact and teach others outside of class. The program allows students to create and upload their own learning aids, which are often done in fun and interesting ways, such as a rap video about antibiotic drugs. There's also a section for students to write their own practice exam questions and share answers, discussion forums, and learning games in which teams of students compete to solve puzzles about various diseases.

This kind of collaboration, including partnerships among scholars from different fields, is becoming one of the hallmarks of modern academia, Samarasekera says. The greatest discoveries are no longer made by lone researchers working away in a lab, but by interdisciplinary teams of scholars all bringing different perspectives toward solving a problem, she says.

As an example, the U of A has offered an experimental course on the oilsands, open to graduate students from any subject area, be it economics, engineering, sociology or native studies. The aim is to get young chemical engineers to see the oilsands in a way they have never considered before, through the eyes of someone more familiar with water laws or Aboriginal land claims, and vice versa.

Another course asks law students and English students to collectively examine legal issues raised in the works of Shakespeare. Another puts English and computer-science students together to design an adventure-style video game.

Should the trend continue, programs at the undergraduate level may involve less formal lecturing and more discussions and small-group activities.

The next step, Samarasekera says, is to boost collaboration not just among scholars from the same university or same province, but from around the world. Some of this has already started through international exchanges and partnerships, such as the deal the U of A just signed with the Indian Institute of Technology Bombay for joint research in energy and health.

Advanced Education and Technology Minister Doug Horner believes one more element should be brought increasingly into the learning community: the private sector. To solve big societal problems, businesses and academia must work together by sharing funding, facilities and knowledge, he says.

---

Beyond research projects, industry could contribute to students' learning experience in a variety of other ways: helping to develop portions of a program's curriculum, making facilities available for students to do lab work, arranging internships and field trips at company factories, and providing guest speakers.

All of this is an example of contextual learning, which moves knowledge beyond the theories and formulas discussed in the textbook and puts it in a real-world setting.

"The new learning experience is really about being interconnected with what's going on around the world," Horner says.

Collaboration with the industry is one way to foster contextual learning, but students could also do a joint research project with a nonprofit group in the inner city, sign up for field work in a developing nation, or spend time at a foreign university through an international exchange. Students say they like the trend, not only because it might reduce textbook time, but because it gives them first-hand experience on how the curriculum is relevant to their lives.

"It's active learning and engaged students," Samarasekera says. "So while the old model of professor-in-front-of-classroom dominated the 20th century, the new model is going to be platforms for students to engage in--whether it's in the classroom, on the web or elsewhere --but all guided by professors who design these platforms to allow students to emerge far more creative."

However, questions remain on just how far this learning model should go. In particular, the suggestion of more industry collaborations can raise fears of corporate influence in academia.

Horner notes such partnerships have already been adopted to some degree by certain programs--especially applied degrees and the trades. Less tested is the idea of businesses contributing to the learning experience of basic undergraduate courses such as anthropology, education or math.

---

Physical changes to the typical university classroom over the next 20 years are likely to be subtle, rather than revolutionary.

More technology is a given. Professors must have the ability to access content from the Internet quickly and reliably so it can be presented as part of their lectures. Satellite linkups with professors in other parts of the world will become more commonplace, so the technology must be good enough to allow students to interact with such scholars in real time.

Audio and video recordings of a professor's lecture must be of sufficient high quality so they can be understood when students later download them as podcasts or vodcasts from the course website. Students must have places to plug in their laptops so they can view course material while in class.

Classrooms will also have to become more versatile, administrators say. Future architecture must provide appropriate spaces for students to hold small group discussions, spaces to work on projects, and interdisciplinary spaces that can be used by students in the sciences, humanities or fine arts.

As an example, new lecture halls under construction at the U of C have the seats arranged in typical rows facing the front, but the seats also swivel 360 degrees, Harrison says.

"So you can immediately go from a large lecture format to forming small groups," he says. "There is a lot of value in that as a learning mechanism."

---

"Professors in front of the classroom delivering lectures, I think, is going to be replaced by professors from around the country and the world being aggregated to deliver a lecture that can be accessed repetitively, while students engage in the classroom with a kind of guide-mentor," Samarasekera says.

As an example, she questions why the U of A, U of C and other western Canadian universities have to offer different versions of Physics 100.She believes the schools should look at combining their respective strengths to create one course offered at all the campuses. Using modern video technology, students would receive their lectures from the best teachers no matter which university they attend, she says.

"Why aren't my students being exposed to Nobel laureates in physics, while professors at the U of A, who are very good, can engage them in discussions?"

Samarasekera's idea may serve as an example of a controversial move toward the "professionalization" of teaching.

The concept suggests that the traditional dual role of a professor -- teacher and researcher--will be split. Scholars whose strength is research will spend less time in the classroom while their schools hire other scholars just for teaching duties.

"The idea that undergrads are going to get (in-person) access to people who do a lot of research, I think we're beyond that in 20 years," says Alex Usher of the Educational Policy Institute. "I think this notion that everyone teaches and everyone does research is financially untenable."

Usher believes this splitting of roles could have some benefits for students because they will be guided by people who are focused on the learning environment and can come up with creative ways of offering the material.

Others, however, see problems ahead.

In many ways, the professionalization of teaching is already underway, says James Turk of the Canadian Association of University Teachers.

As a cost-saving measure, universities are increasingly hiring part-time faculty or "sessionals" whose job is just to teach, he says. Compared with tenured or tenure-track faculty, sessionals are given few benefits: No office space to meet students after class, limited access to labs, and most importantly, no time or encouragement to do research, he says.

This ultimately degrades the learning experience for students because sessionals don't have the opportunity to make new discoveries and invest in the discipline they are teaching, Turk says.

"It's breaking the fundamental link that has been a distinguishing characteristic of universities, that you are both a searcher of knowledge and the disseminator of it."

The issue raises an even bigger question: Will technology ever expand to the point where traditional in-class lectures become obsolete? Will students of the future learn only at home through online courses?

Minister Horner believes there is room to expand Internet-based education. Many programs currently delivered in a traditional format could be offered entirely or partly online, he says.

It's unclear how quickly this will happen, although planning for an outbreak of the H1N1 flu may push ahead the adoption of new technologies, including more online programming, says Lisa Given, an associate professor of library and information studies at the U of A.

However, while digital expansion proceeds, almost no one believes there will be an end to traditional teaching.

"Increasingly, we are moving to larger classes, so technology is going to have to be an enabler, but it cannot replace personal contact," Harrison says.

"We are intensely social beings," adds Usher. "Just saying 'Here's a bunch of resources online,' that won't work. People learn through discussion and from hearing from the master, and I don't think technology is close to replicating that."

The biggest challenge, Horner says, may be getting the professors to stay ahead of the technology curve. This will be increasingly important because as the world's information becomes more accessible, there will be heightened difficulties in teaching students how to critically analyze what they find, he says.

"Instructors and professors are going to have to evolve from being imparters of knowledge to being facilitators of learning," he says. They are going to have to embrace new technology because these kids know it.

"And whether we like the twittering and texting, we have to be in a position to help them understand what's real and what's not.

"Being able to determine the truth of all that information out there is going to be critical to the success of Alberta students in the future."

Public-health officials are preparing to roll out the largest vaccination campaign in Canadian history in the coming weeks, but the public remains confused about the threat posed by H1N1 influenza and the benefits and risks of the vaccine. The Globe and Mail's Public Health Reporter, André Picard, tries to clear up the “conflusion” by answering reader questions.

I keep reading about an “epidemic” and a “pandemic,” which implies there is an imminent threat. But I just don't see an epidemic or pandemic out there, just a lot of fear-mongering.

Epidemic and pandemic are technical terms that refer to the geographical spread of a disease, not its severity. H1N1 is a pandemic because it has spread to virtually every country in the world since it first surfaced in humans in March. I've had the flu before. What's the big deal?

The “flu” has become a catch-all term but it is not a cold, or a gastrointestinal bug. Influenza (H1N1 or otherwise) is unpleasant and will put you out of commission for about a week: You suffer fever, coughing, chills, muscle aches and extreme fatigue; many people have trouble getting out of bed. The vast majority of those infected will get better with rest and plenty of fluids but, in some cases, the symptoms can be so severe that they can result in hospitalization and death.

What kind of vaccine is there for H1N1?

In Canada, there are actually two versions of H1N1 vaccine: an adjuvanted vaccine that has the brand name Arepanrix and a vaccine without adjuvants. The Public Health Agency of Canada has ordered 50.4 million doses – enough for two doses for 75 per cent of the population. Inoculation campaigns will begin around the first week of November.

What's an adjuvant?

An adjuvant is a substance used to bolster the antigens in the vaccine – an adjuvant was used in the H1N1 vaccine because of fears that manufacturers would not be able to harvest sufficient stocks from eggs to make antigen. The adjuvanted vaccine, Arepanrix, contains 3.75 micrograms of antigen; the non-adjuvanted version has 15 micrograms of antigen.

H1N16

What's in the adjuvant?

Arepanrix, the product from GlaxoSmithKline, Canada's sole provider of H1N1 vaccine, uses an adjuvant called AS03, which consists of squalene (shark liver oil), DL-alpha-tocopherol and polysorbate 80. There are claims that squalene, part of the adjuvant used in the anthrax vaccine, was to blame for Gulf War syndrome, but the evidence is weak.

This is a new, experimental vaccine. Has it been properly tested?

The H1N1 vaccine is not experimental. It is the same as past flu vaccines – except with a different antigen, of course – which have a good safety record. New versions of the flu vaccine do not require new human trials each year. The controversial aspect of Arepanrix is the adjuvant. While adjuvants have been used in vaccines for some time, AS03 has undergone limited safety testing. Health Canada insists that there have been no shortcuts taken in the approval process for the vaccine.

Is H1N1 vaccine safe?

Like every drug, the H1N1

vaccine has benefits and risks. The benefit is that it can prevent infection with swine flu – though the protection is not 100 per cent. The risks, for most people, are minimal, and include some redness and maybe a slight fever. But in rare cases, there can be serious complications such as Guillain-Barré syndrome. But those risks are not unique to this vaccine. Isn't there mercury in the vaccine?

Flu vaccine is packaged in vials that contain multiple doses; to avoid contamination, a mercury-based preservative called thimerosal is added. One dose of vaccine contains about 25 micrograms of mercury, slightly less than what you will ingest in a tuna sandwich. There are persistent claims that mercury in vaccines causes autism but this has been debunked.

Should children get the H1N1 vaccine?

The vaccine is recommended for all Canadians over the age of six months, with few exceptions. However, it is recommended that children under the age of 10 get the vaccine in two doses instead of one. That is because children do not produce as strong an immune response to the antigen as adults do.

Should pregnant women get the H1N1 vaccine?

Pregnant women, whose immune system is suppressed, are at highest risk of complications from the flu. Therefore, it is recommended that they be the first in line to get the H1N1 vaccine. However, it should be noted that the H1N1 vaccine that will be administered to pregnant women, which is packaged in single doses, contains neither thimerosal nor adjuvants.

Should I get the vaccine if I've already had the flu?

If you had a laboratory-confirmed case of H1N1, there is no need to be vaccinated. However, bear in mind that only about one-third of people who say they have had the flu actually had influenza.

If I get the vaccine can I still get the flu?

Influenza vaccines are not 100-per-cent effective so, yes, it is possible. However, chances are the symptoms will be more mild. Getting the H1N1 vaccine does not mean you will not get sick this winter; there are other strains of flu and numerous other viruses and bacteria that circulate.

How long can an infected person spread this virus to others?

People infected with the flu shed virus, and they can infect others from one day before getting sick and up to seven days after.

The letter sent home with students in the Vancouver school district might have been worded more carefully, but the essential message was both crucial and on the mark.

The letter warned that H1N1, or swine flu, is circulating widely in Vancouver and has been showing up in schools.

It said that if a child, family members, friends or co-workers have flu symptoms -- a cough, fever and muscle aches -- "it is certainly due to H1N1 flu virus at this time."

At least one parent has raised a legitimate concern that such a blanket diagnosis may not be correct in every case and that such symptoms may not even be related to a flu virus.

It would have been better, as a spokesman for the BC Centre for Disease Control confirmed, to have said "almost certain" to be H1N1.

That said, the distinction should be moot for parents who pay attention to the advice that went along with the diagnosis.

What is unchallenged here is that an abnormally large number of flu cases is showing up in B.C., at a much higher rate than the other Canadian provinces and well ahead of the normal flu season. Although there are other flu strains showing up elsewhere in the world, some of which are included in the "seasonal" flu vaccine that is available now, the vast majority of those tested here are being confirmed as H1N1.

We also know that unless it changes character, for most people, the H1N1 flu, while miserable, will not be serious.

For some people, however, it can be. As the letter to parents indicates, pre-existing conditions such as diabetes, heart or lung disease and a weak immune system can turn H1N1 flu into a life-threatening encounter.

Parents of children who have such conditions are urged to see their doctors now to make a flu plan so they can get anti-viral drugs quickly when flu strikes.

But there have been and likely will be cases in which people who have no apparent pre-existing conditions fall seriously ill.

So as with any illness, parents must monitor their child's symptoms and take appropriate action. For H1N1, that will usually mean keeping the child home home until he or she is well enough to return to school.

Parents will naturally worry, however, that what their child is experiencing is one of those rare cases in which the flu -- or whatever they have been stricken with -- is more serious.

If so, no one should hesitate to seek medical advice, starting with 811, the provincial health information phone line.

It may not be H1N1, or even another seasonal flu. Even so, the advice for parents is the same -- get medical help if you think you need it.

As well, all parents should be talking to their children about the need to protect themselves and others with frequent hand-washing and coughing or sneezing into their elbows.

They should also be thinking about getting the H1N1 vaccine when it becomes available over the next few weeks.

As the letter home correctly pointed out, the vaccine will be the best protection against this flu and the sooner it is administered, the better.

Mr. Grosfield advises businesses to think about potential disruptions and include some methods for dealing with them in their business plans.

Throughout the world, high-risk groups have been placed at the front of the queue for H1N1 vaccinations. This is true, for example, in the United Kingdom, where priority groups, including pregnant women and everyone in at-risk groups over the age of six months, have been given priority. But in Canada, high-risk groups, including pregnant women and children under three, will have to wait longer to receive vaccination for H1N1, perhaps several weeks longer – nobody can say for sure – than members of the general public. This defies logic and indeed suggests a degree of bumbling by federal health authorities that is unacceptable.

The explanation offered is that, while the general public will receive a vaccine that includes chemical adjuvant to boost the vaccine's power, vulnerable groups – most notably pregnant women – are being offered vaccine without adjuvant. This is being done as a precaution, as there are no clinical data about the effects of adjuvant on expectant mothers. In July, the World Health Organization recommended pregnant women receive the non-adjuvanted vaccine where possible, and Canadian health officials agreed.

In other words, pregnant mothers and others at high risk can receive the regular vaccine, but they were effectively told the adjuvant-free vaccine is preferred, and were implicitly been advised to wait. In recent weeks, public health officials have issued contradictory statements on the matter, further complicating the issue.

The problem is that the government, though it has known at least since July of the need to arrange for a supply of non-adjuvanted vaccine, failed to acquire it ahead of the main variety.

While vaccine with the adjuvant has started to be shipped to the provinces and could be available for the general public by the end of October, David Butler-Jones, Canada's chief public health officer, could not say on Monday when non-adjuvanted vaccine would be rolled out.

Expeditiousness has not been a hallmark of Canada's H1N1 strategy. The vaccine is already available in the United States and in other countries, but not in Canada. It took Canada several months longer than the U.S. even to decide who fit the description of a “high-risk” group. The delay in non-adjuvanted vaccine, however, is a serious failure on the part of the Public Health Agency, one that could have a terrible cost if pregnant women are exposed to H1N1 in coming weeks, while waiting for their government to get its act together.

If panic begins to creep in among a good number of your workforce, it will lead to a loss of focus and a loss of concentration which will cripple overall productivity. Not only that, the high costs of absenteeism can cripple your bottom line.

Many organizations have already learned that when it comes to employee health, it is wise to focus on the overall, year-round wellness of employees. They have learned that helping employees fight health issues such as obesity, high stress or lack of exercise as well as helping those employees with chronic diseases such as diabetes or heart disease simply helps to keep people at work and productive. So, where does one start? The following guidelines will help you to set up a basic workplace wellness program.

Establish a leadership committee -- No plan, no matter how well developed, will succeed without a leadership sponsor and program champion as well as resources specifically allocated to the cause. Select a committee of employees who have an interest in the issue and can be committed to making it work.

Conduct an assessment -- Before you get going, you have to find out what the status of the current situation is. Start by asking employees to fill out a health interest survey that assesses the general health environment, eating and exercise habits, personal levels of stress, problem solving and the general support for a healthy workplace program.

Build a multi-level program -- Develop a comprehensive program that will resonate with your workforce. Focus on the individual, yet take into consideration the different cultural values and attitudes toward the various aspects of health. Make sure you also focus on your organization and how to make it as health conscious as possible through ergonomics, cafeteria choices and HR policies.

Set goals -- Target the most important health issues. Offer something for everyone including those who are already fit and healthy. Provide the tools for individuals to assess themselves, set goals and motivate themselves.

Kick off the program -- To get people to participate, you will have to communicate and motivate them to join in. Hold a kick-off event, develop a logo and name for your program and prepare a series of events over a one year timeframe.

Reward and motivate -- People like to be rewarded and recognized so plan for things like posting photos of employees who have achieved success. Include celebrations in your employee newsletter. Offer inexpensive, fun giveaways.

Evaluate and share feedback -- It is important to track your program progress against the goals you have set for yourself. This enables you to make changes to the program as necessary. It also allows you to demonstrate the return on investment for your program.

A good example of wellness success is the familiar Mall of America located in Bloomington, a suburb of Minneapolis, Minn. This large facility employs a workforce of over 900 people and after only one and one half years of wellness programming, they are already experiencing evidence of success. This initiative began when managers were forced to examine what to do about increasing health-care costs. They decided that increasing employee deductibles and co-pays on their health care was simply not the right answer. Instead, they chose to focus on supporting their employees to achieve a healthier lifestyle.

First, they determined that their program would focus on behaviour change in the areas of weight management and smoking cessation as this issue appeared to be the easiest to implement. Within one year, the program was so successful that a full-time co-ordinator was assigned and two new programs, Shape-up and Walk to Wellness Club were implemented. Next, they added a Fruity Friday and Veggie Vensday to their program.

Another group of 10 participants acted as a test case for a new device worn on the waist that measured calorie burning during the day for each individual. This tool helped individuals to assess their own metabolism and put them in control of managing their weight. The Mall of America's walk to wellness program has now been opened to the public and over 2,500 people walk more than one mile a day.

At the same time, the management team at Mall of America knows that while its return on investment is expected to top 300 per cent, additional steps must be taken to help employees be accountable for their own health. As a result, as with many organizations in today's high-cost health-care environment, mall management is assessing the potential of adding an assessment component to all new hire processes. This means that as new employees are being signed up for health-care benefits, they will be immediately assessed for health risks such as high blood pressure, body mass index, and cholesterol.

Another benefit to implementing an organization-wide wellness program is that it can help to take away the fear, the stress and the focus on just one issue such as the much anticipated flu virus. Over time, health and wellness will become part of the organization culture -- simply put, it will be "the way we do things around here".

Cover your mouth when you sneeze, keep your hands away from your face, wash your hands and please, please, please don't fall for claims from companies looking to cash in on the pandemic flu scare.

Those are the simple instructions from Canada's medical community as the country braces for a surge of cases of H1N1.

Some companies are offering more costly solutions than hand-washing, including UV lights, air filters, antibacterial tissues and natural supplements.

Health Canada issued a warning in the spring, cautioning consumers to watch out for phony products that could be ineffective, or in some cases harmful to health.

Dr. Allison McGeer, director of infection control at Mount Sinai Hospital, said antibacterial tissues or items that claim to be coated with bacteria-killing agents offer very limited protection from H1N1, because the virus is often protected within mucus, stool or other bodily secretions.

People prefer technological solutions to behavioural solutions, regardless of what proves effective, she said, noting influenza is transmitted primarily through close contact with an infected person.

"So things that clean the air might in theory have some small impact. But it is going to be small because most of it is not about the air we breathe."

Plenty of products offer protection from H1N1.

The site www.h1n1warrior.com[http://www.h1n1warrior.com] uses a comic-book-style hero and apocalyptic images to sell sanitary hand wipes and face masks.

A company called Verilux sells a cordless sweeper and "sanitizing" wands that emit UV light.

And Health Canada has warned against counterfeit or so-called generic versions of antiviral drugs.

"Health Canada has been monitoring the Internet to uncover websites with claims related to products to prevent and or treat the H1N1 flu outbreak," and has contacted companies to have unsubstantiated claims removed, spokesperson Christelle Legault wrote in an email.

The U.S. Food and Drug Administration also plans to take regulatory or criminal action against people or companies selling phony H1N1 protection products.

Some companies, however, stand by their products.

Robert LeBlanc is chief executive of LeVOCC Inc., a Brighton, Ont., company selling an air filter they say destroys the H1N1 virus.

"We make no claims other than we have been scientifically proven to do one thing that nothing else has ever done, which is absolutely destroy virus RNA," he said of the $800 device.

LeBlanc said a strain of the H1N1 virus was run through the machine for 30 minutes, after which testing detected no evidence of its RNA, or genetic building block.

He added that the device was tested by a highly reputable laboratory that he cannot name until results are released in December and said he believes it would be useful in multiple-family homes.

McGeer cautioned that even if a product could kill the virus, that would not mean it can protect against the spread of disease.
