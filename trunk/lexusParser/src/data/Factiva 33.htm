We know, according to the U.S. Centers for Disease Control, that is contains genes from pigs, humans and birds. We know from experience that such a mix gives it the potential to pass back and forth between species and to become particularly virulent.

Which is presumably one reason why the public health establishment is so fixated on swine flu. The other reason may that world governments are using this pandemic as a test run to see how they would respond to a real crisis.

But what we also know is that, so far, this flu strain has been no more serious than any other.

To put it another way: Before the H1N1 scare, the death from flu of a 13-year-old boy wouldn't have led newscasts across the nation.

Consider the facts.

According to the World Health Organization, fewer than 5,000 people have died around the globe from this variant of swine flu. In any normal year, influenza causes between 250,000 and 500,000 deaths worldwide.

In the Southern Hemisphere, the winter flu season is now over. In spite of dire predictions, only 185 people died from swine flu in Australia - considerably fewer than the roughly 3,000 who succumb to seasonal influenza in that country each year.

What does seem to be true, according to early statistics, is that this particular strain is harder on people in the prime of their lives. Common flu strains tend to hit the very young and very old.

In that sense, the real novelty of this novel H1N1 strain may be that it is killing the wrong people.

For those youngish and middle-aged people who tend to dominate both politics and the media, this is understandably worrying.

What's more unnerving, however, is the level of hype surrounding this flu virus, hype that at times comes dangerously close to hysteria.

Many parents are now wondering: Should they keep their kids home from school, should they keep them away from hockey practice and gymnastics and should they – horror of horrors – put the kibosh on Halloween? The answer to those questions is no – an equivocal no.

That doesn't mean that there is no risk, of course. The chances of contracting H1N1 are relatively high, and it is estimated that, by the time all is said and done, as many as one in three Canadians could be afflicted by the disease. Still, that is only slightly higher than seasonal flu.

While H1N1 flu is unpleasant, only a fraction of the infected will get so sick that they require hospitalization, and fewer still will die. But because the denominator is so large, a lot of severely ill people will be in intensive care in the coming weeks, and the deaths will be counted in the hundreds, perhaps thousands.

Statistics, of course, are abstract. The deaths of those two children – their stories and photos splashed across the front page of newspapers and on TV newscasts – are painfully and frighteningly real.

What is difficult is putting this risk in context so that it is meaningful.

In 21st century Canada, risk is a largely unfamiliar concept. In a country of 34 million people that has nearly 400,000 births annually, fewer than 800 children aged 1-14 die each year. (Another 1,200 or so under the age of 1 die, most of congenital abnormalities.) In Canada, the greatest danger to children is falls and motor vehicle collisions. Deaths from infectious disease are remarkably few, in large part due to vaccination.

Yet the H1N1 vaccine is hard to come by, even for high priority groups such as children. And while mass immunization campaigns are rolling out across the nation, the lines are frustratingly long.

So, what should parents do?

First and foremost, they should bite the bullet and get their children vaccinated. Pack a book, a GameBoy, and an iPod and get in line.

Stand in line and relish the thought that we live in a country where one of the greatest threats to our children is a bug with a relatively small risk. And don't forget that the risks of the vaccine itself are vanishingly small.

Paradoxically, the lineups for the vaccine are creating huge gatherings, something that should be avoided during an outbreak of disease.

But this risk too has to be kept in context.

The best and most reasoned information on the matter comes from the U.S. Centers for Disease Control, which has published a document entitled “Guidance for State and Local Public Health Officials and School Administrators for School (K-12) Responses to Influenza.”

The guidelines say, essentially, that there is no point closing schools, except in circumstances where there are huge numbers of infected students and staff. (Similar reasoning applies to other community activities such as sports and trick-or-treating.) What the CDC says is that individual children (and adults) should stay home if they are sick to avoid infecting others. But closing schools only creates alternate gathering places, such as malls and makeshift daycares.

The CDC also says that when children (and adults) are out and about, they should practise hand hygiene and etiquette to minimize the spread of germs. Only in rare instances should activities be cancelled.

In other words, life can and should go on during flu season.

Isolation and quarantine are not very effective public health measures on a large scale. They don't substantially reduce the risk of the spread of disease and they greatly increase panic and fear.

After all, what is risk?

The dictionary definition is: “The possibility of suffering harm or loss: danger.”

A more modern and timely definition comes from Peter Sandman, a professor at Rutgers University and the guru of risk management, and the creator of the formula “Risk = Hazard + Outrage.”

We know, in a mathematical way, the risk of H1N1 influenza: low but still significant. What is more difficult to measure is outrage, or fear.

When children die, fear rises in an exponential manner.

If nothing else, the tragic deaths of Evan Frustaglio, 13, of Toronto, and Vanetia Warner, 10, of Cornwall, Ont., should shake Canadians out of their complacency. But the largely-indifferent-until-now masses should not hurtle to the other extreme, panic. Between complacency and panic is a vast territory – one we should occupy actively, by working, by going to school and, yes, by trick-or-treating.

H1N1 is predominantly transferred from individual to individual through aerosol droplets in the air - which means being coughed on by someone beside you is the most likely way for the flu to be transmitted. So, the first step in staying healthy is asking exercisers to avoid the gym when they are sick.

That being said, Briedis noted that it is possible for the virus to stay viable for a short period of time on a hard surface like a piece of fitness equipment. Therefore it's prudent to take extra precautions when using equipment that is passed from one exerciser to the next, like cardio and weight training machines, exercise balls and dumbbells.

"If you come in contact with the virus and then touch your face, nose or eyes, it's possible to get the flu," said Briedis.

Wiping down equipment with a cleaning solution after use, draping a clean towel over an exercise mat or bench and washing your hands or using an alcohol-based hand sanitizer before and after a workout should be standard practice in gyms across the country.

But gym rats aren't the only exercisers who need to take special precautions when it comes to avoiding the flu. According to Briedis sport teams are also at increased risk of exposure to the H1N1 virus. The proximity of athletes to each other and to their personal belongings makes the virus easy to pass along from one teammate to the next. And once the flu hits one team member, there's no telling how quickly it will spread and the kind of impact it can have on a team's ability to field enough players to compete.

A look back in history suggests that the flu can have devastating effects on a team.

"In the winter of 1918/19, the Stanley Cup playoffs were cancelled due to an outbreak of the Spanish flu," said Briedis. "Four members of the Canadiens team got sick and one died."

Fortunately, the H1N1 virus has been linked to a small number of fatalities worldwide and, for the most part, results in a moderate fever, fatigue and aches and pains, which in a healthy population means spending a few days in bed recuperating. But that doesn't mean it can be treated lightly by teams and leagues across the country.

Simple precautions can reduce the risk. Water bottles and towels shouldn't be shared between athletes (all towels should be removed from team benches) and a protocol should be in place to keep sick athletes away from their healthy teammates.

Briedis recommends quarantining an athlete until they are symptom-free (no coughing or fever) for 24 hours. If an athlete gets sick on the road, they should be put in a private room and wear a mask during the bus ride home where they should sit as far away from their teammates as possible.

The same goes for the sick athlete's roommate, who should be kept under a watchful eye for three days before being placed back on the playing roster.

As for the ritual of shaking hands after a game, Briedis isn't in favour of suspending this age-old tradition. The ritual Briedis is in favour of forgoing, however, is the Montreal two-cheek kiss, which he claims should be suspended until after the H1N1 virus runs its course.

If the flu does strike, the decision to exercise or compete is probably moot as fever and the associated aches and pains will relegate all but the hardiest to bed until the symptoms abate. The average time to recuperate is three to seven days, after which exercisers are encouraged to get back in the game or gym slowly.

Briedis recommends waiting for 24 hours after the fever has abated before resuming light exercise. A body recuperating from flu is quick to fatigue, so respect the signals your body is sending and take it easy if you feel less than 100 per cent during your workout.

Precautions should also be taken before exercising in the cold, especially if your flu symptoms included a cough. Cold air can further irritate already inflamed bronchial pathways, causing a rebound case of bronchitis and another couple of days in bed.

Once you feel up to speed, go ahead and resume your normal workouts. Exercise is still the best medicine for a healthy mind and body.

Physician Paul Le Guerrier of the infectious diseases unit of the Montreal Health and Social Services Agency explains pregnancy and H1N1 vaccine issues:

Gazette: What are the recommendations for pregnancy?

Answer: The official recommendation from national health authorities and specialists is that women who are pregnant less than 20 weeks should preferably receive the unadjuvanted H1N1 vaccine. Those in advanced stages of pregnancy, more than 20 weeks, can get the adjuvanted one. The Society of Obstetricians and Gynecologists of Canada, however, says that if the virus is circulating in the community, which is our case, that these women could get the adjuvanted vaccine because there is no data suggesting it is dangerous.

G: Why is the unadjuvanted H1N1 vaccine preferred?

A: For the same reason we don't usually recommend medication and vaccines in pregnant women - it's touchy in respect to fetal growth. Pregnant women are not part of safety tests because we don't want to take any risks on their babies. Doctors have to weigh the risks vs. the benefits in every case, and that's what the experts are doing with the H1N1 vaccine.

G: What's in the adjuvant?

A: It's made of three substances: an emulsifier that mixes water and oil, called polysorbate 80, you find it in ice cream, it's a compound used in the food industry; vitamin E; and the third component is a fish oil called Squalene, an organic compound that is produced in the body as a form of cholesterol. And those three together have been found to induce a better immune response and more antibodies.

The reason we are using this is because the World Health Organization has asked for more vaccines to better cover the world population. We can make four times more vaccine with the adjuvant.

G: Does a woman getting the H1N1 shot also vaccinate her unborn baby?

A: It does not immunize the baby. But the mother will produce antibodies and an immune response and she will pass on her antibodies through the placenta to the baby. We call that passive immunity, and it will protect the baby during the first six months of life.
