At first, Martin Melchor didn't take Mexico's flu epidemic all that seriously.

But then, this week, a wealthy 33-year-old neighbour died of the virus at a private hospital around the corner from Mr. Melchor's Mexico City apartment. Next, he met the grieving relatives of a second flu victim: a healthy 25-year-old woman who died Tuesday at the National Institute of Respiratory Illnesses.

Suddenly, the horror became real.

“She had two young kids and her relatives were all there crying in the hospital. She was only sick for a day,” Mr. Melchor said in a phone interview from the Mexican capital.

“When I came home, I took my clothes off, put them in a plastic bag and threw them away, and showered. I got really worried. It's affecting all social classes.”

Mr. Melchor, a 37-year-old videographer who normally earns his living assisting international television crews, is now too agitated to leave his home. His biggest fear is that his three-year-old daughter will be exposed to influenza A H1N1, blamed as of yesterday afternoon for 176 deaths and 2,498 illnesses in Mexico. He only ventures out for runs to the supermarket or pharmacy.

Not that there would be much to do. The city, normally teeming with outdoor vendors, musicians, protesters, festivals and traffic jams, is as quiet as a morgue. This is life under lockdown: Parks are closed and plazas empty. Malls, museums, cinemas and shops are shuttered – as well as most restaurants and cafés, including some of the country's 259 Starbucks outlets. Concerts at the National Auditorium have been cancelled, and so have masses at churches around the capital. Soccer games at the Azteca Stadium are being played without spectators.

“The hardest thing is not knowing what's really going on,” said Mr. Melchor of the sense of foreboding permeating the city of 22 million.All across the sprawling metropolis, Mexicans are scrambling to cope with the psychological challenges of living with an invisible predator – and the practical challenges of getting around, and finding child care, food and water in a city under lockdown.

The Mexican government urged all citizens to stay home today through Tuesday, and ordered all but the most essential government services closed during the five-day Cinco de Mayo festival in the hope of containing the epidemic, which the World Health Organization warns is on the brink of becoming a global epidemic.

“There is no safer place to protect yourself against catching swine flu than in your house,” said Mexican President Felipe Calderon in a televised address Wednesday night.

But that doesn't stop the anxiety. Mr. Melchor visited his own doctor twice this week, convinced he was running a fever (he was given a clean bill of health). He has face masks and hand gel, which are now in short supply at drugstores.

“There is a sense of bewilderment,” said an expatriate consultant who lives in the wealthy Polanco neighbourhood. “Friends won't come over to your house. The people who can have left the capital.”

She continued to go to the office this week, the consultant said, but all work and social events were cancelled, including a children's birthday party. Her children, aged 3 and 6, complain about being cooped up since their school shut down. Bored with indoor diversions, they have begun colouring on face masks scattered around their living room. Yet there is underlying apprehension.

“My six-year-old is crying, saying, ‘Mama, I don't want to die,' ” said the woman, who asked not to be identified. “People don't know when it's going to end and that is very difficult.”

Those who have second homes outside the capital are decamping for them, and staying in touch with extended-family members by phone, e-mail and text message.

While most Mexicans are calmly obeying government instructions to stay inside and not kiss in greeting, or shake hands, some are starting to question the effectiveness of the official response to the epidemic.

“Every day it gets worse, there are new restrictions, and it seems like the authorities are not ready for it because they don't know exactly what it is they're fighting against,” said Paloma Jimenez Anabitarte, a 23-year-old consultant. “I heard my downstairs neighbour is in the hospital, so I'm getting more worried about it.”

Ms. Jimenez Anabitarte has stocked up on several weeks supply of water and food, and plans to get out of the city this weekend.

Public-health officers still haven't given medicine to the families of those confirmed to have died from influenza A N1H1, or determined where the outbreak began or how it spread. That means people are still spreading it. Officials confirmed that the first person to die of the flu, in Oaxaca, was a door-to-door census taker who may have had contact with scores of people before she died on April 13.

“If the government had acted more quickly, then people wouldn't have died. We don't have enough labs to test all those who are sick,” Mr. Melchor complained. “God is kicking us now. What else can happen?”

With the violence from the ongoing drug cartel wars, this week's 5.6-magnitude earthquake near Chilpancingo in the Pacific Coast state of Guerrero, and the impact of the global economic recession, some fear one more disaster could yet unfold in this beleaguered country: “What's next, a volcano?” said Ms. Jimenez Anabitarte.

Mr. Melchor is hoping to leave the capital today and fly to Arizona, where his wife is. He is worried about his daughter catching a cold on the plane. But, he says, at least that's a concern he can manage – unlike the terror of something unknown, a new flu strain even scientists don't yet understand.

CANBERRA -- A fatal blast on a boat filled to the gunwales with unauthorized arrivals has turned Australia's attention back to the vexed problem of those incoming tides of people wanting to start a new life in "the lucky country.''

Even the mounting panic sparked by swine flu hasn't deflected public attention from the sudden resurgence of boat people making their perilous way to Australia over the past few months.

Unlike Canada, Australia has a mandatory detention system for asylum seekers.

Although the nation was one of the first to sign the United Nations Convention on the Status Of Refugees in the goodwill following the Second World War, the passage of time has hardened attitudes towards those who come here seeking protection.

Australia jettisoned much of its international nice guy image in August 2001 when it appeared on the national stage openly questioning the legitimacy of those incoming hordes yearning to breathe free.

The then-prime minister John Howard refused to allow 438 Afghan asylum seekers (or unauthorized arrivals -- the terminology depends on where your sympathies lay) on board the Norwegian-based MV Tampa make landfall.

Howard at the height of the crisis was seen in the Canberra press gallery talking to journalists, furiously defending his right to maintain the integrity of the nation's borders.

"We will decide who comes into this country and the circumstances in which they come,'' he later famously declared and his domestic, if not international, popularity soared, helping him win the tough 2001 election.

Australia, which had and maintains a relatively generous refugee and general immigration program, enacted tough laws ensuring any unannounced arrivals turning up on boats were processed offshore in immigration centres.

With the arrivals receding since the high water mark of 2001, the subject of asylum seekers also receded in the public mind.

That was until last September when desperate men and women often with children in tow began arriving off the Western Australian Coast, often on isolated Ashmore Reef, fleeing countries such as Afghanistan, Iraq and Sri Lanka.

Little more than two weeks ago tragedy struck.

Defence Force personnel were boarding a boat filled with suspected illegal arrivals on Ashmore Reef when an explosion occurred, killing five of the arrivals and injuring several military employees.

Many more of the arrivals were horribly burned and are still undergoing their agonizing recoveries in burns units in hospitals around the country.

Prime Minister Kevin Rudd was faced with accusations his perceived softer approach to handling illegal arrivals had sparked the latest wave.

He immediately confronted the media declaring all people-smugglers should "rot in hell.''

Rudd then announced the nation was going into recession -- an admission which although obvious appeared to distract the headlines away from the subject of illegal arrivals, at least for a time.

But the federal Opposition saw a political opportunity, and kept the issue alive by blaming the Labor government, elected in November 2007, for encouraging the new arrivals and by extension, for being responsible for the Ashmore Reef tragedy and the loss of life.

Opposition Leader Malcolm Turnbull has called for an independent inquiry accusing the government of presiding over a "massive policy failure.''

The new Cdn $320-million detention centre on Christmas Island near Ashmore Reef is still well below its maximum capacity of 800, or the 400 it can hold comfortably.

But 234 detainees are now in various forms of detention -- creating another bureaucratic nightmare for the government.

Still, journalist Mike Steketee, writing in the national broadsheet The Australian, suggests the issues may not carry the great political weight it did eight years ago.

Australia is a giant island. Unlike European nations or even Canada it has thousands of square kilometres of ocean deterring those wanting to arrive through the back door.

Steketee cites polling which suggests Australians are more indifferent than xenophobic about the latest arrivals, "even if the debate did seem to proceed on the issue we are open to some sort of mass invasion.''

"The reality is very different: very few slip through the multiple layers of the border protection net,'' Steketee wrote.

"Our controls on people who move by air, which is the main way most would-be refugees come to Australia, are among the strictest in the world.''

It may be that the greatest effect on Canada of the worldwide swine flu outbreak will be the need for us to assist other countries.

The H1N1 virus does not seem to be having the lethal consequences outside of Mexico as in that country; while it is believed responsible for 168 deaths there (at press time), the only swine-flu death in any other country was a Mexican child who became infected in his own country before travelling to Texas where he died.

The sense of alarm that arrived with first news of the swine flu outbreak has eased slightly; the fullness of time does put things into perspective. Communications and co-operation between international health agencies has been excellent and -- by and large -- there is a sense of cautious optimism. Governments in Canada and the U.S. are better prepared than they were just a few years ago.

None of this means that Canada, or the U.S., is "safe" from swine flu. One science/medical theory is that the death toll and number of hospitalizations in Mexico represents only a small fraction of people who have been infected there. If the virus was to became as rampant in other countries, the theory concludes, they too would see climbing fatality numbers. But there may be other mitigating factors such as public-health standards and effectiveness, the general health of the population, or even just that other countries had time to start screening and public-hygiene protocols.

But following that line of reasoning leads to the worrisome realization that the H1N1 virus has not -- yet -- surfaced in Africa or Asia, where population densities are higher than in the West and where many people's health is already compromised by nutrition, hygiene or existing disease issues. Medical care is harder to come by and supplies of antiviral medications range from slim to non-existent.

That may -- and we stress may -- create a situation where Canada can offer help in ameliorating H1N1 outbreaks or treating patients.

For all that, it must be said that there are way too many people -- many of them with no real knowledge -- speculating publicly on what happens next. "We have lots of scenariologists," Dr. David Williams, Ontario's acting chief medical officer said this week. He said the public should avoid blogs and other sites where unreliable posters are speculating about the flu.

There's a good side to the almost instant availability of information in a situation such as this: medical agencies can share information and create co-ordinated responses quickly enough to get ahead of the virus. But the bad side is that anyone with a blog or a website can ramp up fear, pass along dangerous misinformation -- including denying that there is a problem. Regardless of what is yet to unfold, the World Health Organization has said it is on the verge of declaring a pandemic, the first one since 1968.

There's no room yet for sighs of relief: Things may get worse before they get better, both nationally and internationally. There are still long days ahead for public health officials. But the time may be approaching when Canada can look to see where it can help others.

But even with the most sophisticated planning, hitting the sweet spot in epidemic control -- one that protects the most people from disease and minimizes disruptions to society -- is even tougher than hitting a fast ball thrown by a major league pitcher.

The greatest tension that erupted during the SARS epidemic in Toronto related to pushback against recommended home quarantines for the sick and the heavy responsibility placed on front-line health care professionals. The historical record and recent studies of pandemic awareness suggest that the majority of people will acquiesce to, if not embrace, voluntary NPI. Compliance with home quarantine was very high during the 2003 SARS epidemic in Toronto. It is heartening that many people, due to a combination of self interest and civic duty, are willing to comply with voluntary NPI, at least for certain periods of time. Okay, it's not good news that the economy shrank even more than forecast. At IHS Global Insight in Boston, chief U.S. financial economist Brian Bethune looked through the report yesterday and saw some of those famous green sprouts of hope.

Yes, Bethune acknowledged, the first-quarter numbers were awful, but they were awful in a way that suggested some clearing of the decks for renewed production and some one-shot issues unlikely to continue hammering the numbers, he said yesterday.

Right now, in the second quarter, Bethune is betting that the slump has eased to only about half the rate of decline seen in yesterday's report.

By the third quarter, which begins in July, he predicts the beginning of modest growth again.

Bethune is not alone. Analysts from all over the U.S. and Canada saw the bad first-quarter economic slump as kind of a final blowoff of recessionary bad news. Not everyone believes growth will resume by this summer, but a survey of economists by the Bloomberg news service found that this was the median forecast.

How can such optimism come from such a serious report of economic collapse?

The really big negative surprise in the first-quarter downturn was the role of government, which actually subtracted from growth in spite of plans to spend like crazy. Unless the Swine Flu becomes a severe threat or the U.S. banking sector is sicker than markets now believe, we have a reasonable shot at seeing some economic growth soon.

A good panic doesn't just come out of nowhere. Sure, it seems effortless in the moment – the worried headlines, the nervous commentators, the lunchroom buzz – but really, it takes years of preparation to work up phobias that can be released on a moment's notice. Take the swine flu that's got everyone so jumpy this week. Pandemics loom large in the public imagination when it comes to apocalypse scenarios, right up there with having our robotic servants turn on us. We wouldn't be so worried that the flu would be the end of us if we hadn't been looking askance at every dead bird since the summer of SARS. This is one well-rehearsed freak-out.

In a way, the same can be said for the “Craigslist killer” story that bumped the swine flu from the news (and none too soon). The label refers to one Philip Markoff, a 23-year-old Boston medical student who is charged with the murder of a woman who had posted an erotic ad on Craigslist. Maybe it's the alliterative way that “the Craigslist killer” rolls off the tongue; it's certainly more assonant than “The Craigslist, then E-mail and Cellphone Killer.” But judging by the way the case sparked a moral panic about Craigslist itself, the ready association people drew between the two words seems more than just phonetic.

And then there was a bizarre piece on ABC's Nightline, in which anchor Martin Bashir perched on the edge of founder Craig Newmark's desk and demanded to know whether Newmark is a “law-abiding citizen of America,” and if so, why he's facilitating prostitution by allowing naughty ads on his website.

It didn't take long for people to start pointing out the patently obvious: There is nothing about Craigslist that contributed to this crime. The prudish can get worked up about the fact that Craigslist runs plenty of thinly veiled ads from prostitutes – but here, that's tantamount to blaming the victim: She advertised, therefore she was asking for it.

But just like any other outbreak of hysteria, this didn't come from nowhere. The Craigslist moral panic had been brewing out of sight, waiting for its moment to erupt. It's tempting to write this off as another case of semi-informed Internet phobia – remember the moral panic over MySpace, which was said to be full of child predators? – but while there's no connection between Craigslist and the crime, it does shoulder responsibility for the panic.

Craigslist has always walked a fine line between anarchy and grungy respectability. It made its name as the under-moderated, anything-goes website. The libertine act comes at a price. Craigslist is a pile of contradictions that nobody knows quite how to read. It's not bound by the rules of a corporation trying to look good or a non-profit trying to do good, and it's riddled with salaciousness.

It's a great formula, but all it took was a scandalous news story that involved some possible combination of sex, a murder and Craigslist, and everybody's worst assumptions about the site seemed to have been realized. It didn't matter that the shoe, as it were, didn't fit. Craigslist has been plaguing the Internet with diffuse anxiety for years, even as we enjoyed its services. I can't feel too bad for Craig and his list, this week. It's no fair that they got stuck with the “Craigslist killer.” But this is a panic that was waiting to happen.

Although the new H1n1 swine flu it is not yet a pandemic -- a global epidemic of a new and serious disease -- it could quickly start one. Here are a few ways the situation could develop:

Severe Pandemic

This is the worst-case scenario. every 30 to 40 years the world suffers an influenza pandemic, with a new strain of flu spreading rapidly, causing serious disease and killing hundreds of thousands of people within a few weeks. the 1918 pandemic is considered the worst-case scenario. at least 40 million people died in 18 months and the epidemics passed over communities in waves.

But that was in the pre-antibiotic era, in a time when even simple infections killed people, no mechanical ventilators were available and vaccines were primitive at best. the general population had little idea of how diseases were transmitted. even so, experts predict a 1918-like flu would keep 40 per cent of the workforce out at any time. if this kind of pandemic struck today, millions could die, world trade would slow to a crawl and many economies would crumble.

Mild Pandemic

This last happened in 1968. the H3n2 strain killed an estimated 1 million people. experts predict that a strain of such virulence today would have less severe effects, with antivirals available that were not on the market 40 years ago. even with vaccines, drugs and better public education, ordinary seasonal flu kills between 250,000 and 500,000 people every year. the difference with a pandemic strain is that there would not immediately be a vaccine against it and it would likely cause serious disease among broader age groups than the very young, very old and immune-compromised.

No Pandemic

Everyone is hoping this flu strain will just fizzle out. influenza is a promiscuous virus, readily swapping genes with other flu viruses in a person's or animal's body. it also mutates constantly. both these factors mean it can quickly grow worse, or become milder. it could at any time lose its ability to transmit easily from one person to another, or could become as mild as regular, seasonal flu. but it would take months to know whether this has happened. flu strains often disappear over the summer months, reemerging in late summer or early autumn. the 1918 flu strain did this with a vengeance.

Airlines, tour operators and travel agents are working hard to alter plans for the travelling public, steering vacationers away from Mexico, said Guelph-based Sandra Pobega, vice-president of Carleson Wagonlit International Travel Service. Cuba and the Dominican Republic are popular alternatives. Both are also excellent value for budget-minded travellers, she said.

Those going to Mexico on business or to visit family are wise to delay until the swine-flu risk in Mexican outbreak zones is resolved, she added.

"Those just have to wait until things are cleared up."

Pobega welcomed calls from vacationers for up-to-the-minute travel information.

"We don't want people to panic," she said.

While some Mexican cases have been lethal, the confirmed cases in Canada are considered mild.

People are recovering at home, rather than in hospitals, Mercer said. Health investigators haven't yet determined why the Mexican cases appear much more serious than the Canadian ones.

"It's too early to have a strong understanding of this virus's ability to cause disease," Mercer said.
