As the city and province prepare to ramp up access to the vaccine - 10 public health clinics will be operating in Toronto Monday, and more family doctors will be giving out shots - amid an unexpected reduction in supply, Canada's top public health officials are admitting they underestimated the demand.

"Three or four weeks ago, what we could not anticipate was the number of people that are interested," Dr. David Butler-Jones, the chief public health officer, told CTV's Question Period Sunday.

But that was before the recent deaths of three Ontario children who caught H1N1 - another death is still unconfirmed - put a human face on the virus and galvanized Canadians to seek vaccinations for themselves and their families.

The city's public health department has sent almost 250,000 H1N1 flu vaccine doses to local hospitals and more than 100,000 to physicians' offices to reduce lineups at public health clinics.

Westway was one of more than 200 Toronto doctors' offices that received the vaccine last week. Westway's Dr. Leah Harrington said she received a note from the public health department on Wednesday telling her the vaccines she requested would not be available. On Friday, the clinic started giving the shots to their patients, and on Sunday morning at 9 a.m., a line of 100 people was out the door, with wait times of about an hour.

"It gives us peace of mind to provide these to our patients," Harrington said between appointments Sunday, her regular day off. "My patients love it. Who wants to stand outside for hours with three little kids?"

But at the Vellore Village Community Centre in Vaughan, some lined up in the dark before 4 a.m., even though the clinic wasn't scheduled to open until noon.

"We fully respect and honour the people of Ontario" to follow the ministry's guidelines, she said.

Shots are being given to people aged six months to 65 years with chronic medical conditions, pregnant women with chronic medical conditions or more than 20 weeks pregnant, children from 6 months to under 5 years of age, persons living in remote and isolated areas, health-care workers, and those providing care to persons at high risk who cannot be immunized or may not respond to vaccines.

H1N1 is predominantly transferred from individual to individual through aerosol droplets in the air -- which means being coughed on by someone beside you is the most likely way for the flu to be transmitted.

So, the first step in staying healthy is asking exercisers to avoid the gym when they are sick.

That being said, Briedis noted that it is possible for the virus to stay viable for a short period of time on a hard surface such as a piece of fitness equipment. Therefore, it's prudent to take extra precautions when using equipment that is passed from one exerciser to the next, such as cardio and weight training machines, exercise balls and dumbbells.

"If you come in contact with the virus and then touch your face, nose or eyes, it's possible to get the flu," said Briedis.

Wiping down equipment with a cleaning solution after use, draping a clean towel over an exercise mat or bench and washing your hands or using an alcohol-based hand sanitizer before and after a workout should be standard practice in gyms across the country.

But gym rats aren't the only exercisers who need to take special precautions when it comes to avoiding the flu.

According to Briedis, sport teams are also at increased risk of exposure to the H1N1 virus. The proximity of athletes to each other and to their personal belongings makes the virus easy to pass along from one teammate to the next.

And once the flu hits one team member, there's no telling how quickly it will spread and the kind of impact it can have on a team's ability to field enough players to compete.

A look back in history suggests that the flu can have devastating effects on a team.

"In the winter of 1918-19, the Stanley Cup playoffs were cancelled due to an outbreak of the Spanish flu," said Briedis. "Four members of the Canadiens team got sick and one died."

Fortunately, the H1N1 virus has been linked to a relatively small number of fatalities worldwide and, for the most part, results in a moderate fever, fatigue and aches and pains, which in a healthy population means spending a few days in bed recuperating.

But that doesn't mean it can be treated lightly by teams and leagues across the country.

Simple precautions can reduce the risk. Water bottles and towels shouldn't be shared between athletes (all towels should be removed from team benches) and a protocol should be in place to keep sick athletes away from their healthy teammates.

Briedis recommends quarantining an athlete until they are symptom-free (no coughing or fever) for 24 hours. If an athlete gets sick on the road, they should be put in a private room and wear a mask during the bus ride home where they should sit as far away from their teammates as possible.

BACKGROUND

The H1N1 flu virus is a new strain of pandemic influenza which is different than the seasonal flu. People have no natural immunity to protect against this virus. The H1N1 flu virus emerged in April 2009 and surveillance of its spread shows that it is affecting more young and healthy people than the regular seasonal flu, which normally affects seniors and young children. People with underlying medical conditions and pregnant women may be at a greater risk for severe illness.

WHAT IS IT?

The H1N1 flu virus - also known as human swine influenza - is a respiratory illness that affects the nose, throat and lungs. This virus usually affects pigs, but has been transferred to humans.

The H1N1 flu virus has caused primarily mild illness in Canada, but Canadians need to be prepared to respond to a potentially more severe form of the virus that may emerge this fall.

How is it spread?

The H1N1 flu virus is contagious and is spread the same way as regular seasonal influenza. This happens when an infected person coughs or sneezes and their germs enter the nose, eyes, or throat of another person. The germs can also rest on hard surfaces like counters and doorknobs, and can be picked up on hands and transmitted to the respiratory system when someone touches their mouth and/or nose. It is not possible to catch it by eating pork or pork products or through blood transfusions.

CONTAGIOUSNESS

More research is being done on how long a person can be infectious (be able to spread the virus to others), but it is believed that this period is for one day before the onset of symptoms and continues for approximately seven days after symptoms have started. The time it takes between being infected and experiencing symptoms is between two and seven days.

- Sometimes

PREVENTION

- Wash hands often

- Keep common surfaces disinfected

- Cough and sneeze into your arm, instead of your hand

- If you are sick, stay home until your symptoms are gone and you feel well enough to participate in all activities

- If you get flu-like symptoms and are pregnant, have underlying health problems or if your symptoms get worse, contact your health care provider.

TREATMENT

Their concern reflects one of the most serious casualties of the whole pandemic situation -- the loss of faith in our public health officials.

That lack of confidence in previously trusted health sources got another jolt with the recent comments on cancer screening by the chief medical officer of the American Cancer Society, Dr. Otis Brawley.

"American medicine has overpromised when it comes to screening," Dr. Brawley told the New York Times. "The advantages to screening have been exaggerated."

Dr. Brawley was referring to widespread screening for breast and prostate cancer, the two most common cancers. The Canadian Cancer Society expects 22,700 women will be diagnosed with breast cancer in 2009, and 5,400 will die. Men can expect 22,500 diagnoses of prostate cancer and 9,400 deaths.

Screening for these diseases used to consist of breast self-exams and mammograms for women, and the dreaded DRE (digital rectal exam) and the PSA blood test for prostate specific antigen, which can be a marker for prostate cancer. Today, regular breast self-exam has largely been dropped as a recommendation, and most health organizations don't advise widespread PSA screening.

As a population, we've been getting our mammograms and PSA tests for almost 25 years, but the benefits are less than clear. In a recent article in the Journal of the American Medical Association (JAMA), researchers reported, "Overall cancer rates are higher, many more patients are being treated, and the absolute incidence of aggressive or later-stage disease has not been significantly decreased. Screening has had some effect, but it comes at significant cost, including overdiagnosis, overtreatment, and complications of therapy."

In the simplest terms, screening means that many more cancers are being diagnosed earlier and treated, but there has been no matching drop in deaths from advanced stages of these diseases. There are some differences between the results for breast cancer screening and prostate cancer screening -- mammograms have reduced the death rate for breast cancer by up to 20 per cent; the death rate for prostate cancer is basically unchanged. The study in JAMA pointed out the significant increase in the number of cancers detected in the United States. "Prostate-specific antigen testing has nearly doubled the chance that a man will be diagnosed with prostate cancer in his lifetime: In 1980, a white man's lifetime risk of prostate cancer was 1 in 116; today it is 1 in 6.1 (African American men have higher rates of prostate cancer). A woman's lifetime risk of breast cancer was 1 in 12 in 1980; today it is 1 in 8.1."

Most of this increase comes from small, less aggressive cancers. In prostate cancer, many men have cancers that would never cause them problems in their lifetime. Because of screening, they are identified and treated, often with consequences of incontinence and impotence.

In breast cancer, many aggressive and lethal cancers spring up quickly between mammograms and don't get caught by every-two-year screening tests, while women are treated aggressively for smaller, less threatening cancers.

Advanced prostate cancer and advanced breast cancer both have poor five-year survival rates. These are the diseases we need to treat aggressively and immediately.

The problem lies in our inability yet to identify and separate the aggressive forms of these cancers from those that require more modest treatment, or in some prostate cancer, no treatment except for watchful waiting.

I can't imagine being forced to walk around town carrying or pushing all my belongings with me because I have nowhere to lay my fevered head. That is an unimaginable torment.

Those of us with homes need to think about what happens to homeless people who are sick. Where do they go to recuperate? Who looks after them?

In days of old, when people had no one to care for them during times of illness, they could count on the acute care hospital. That is no longer the case.

A homeless person with an illness that is not life-threatening won't get a bed at the hospital in Kamloops. There is simply no bed to give him or her.

In fact, a seriously ill man was released from the Kamloops hospital recently only to die several days later. The local medical community was not to blame. His illness was chronic and he fit the criteria of a patient who should be released.

He was no longer acutely ill, only chronically ill, and physicians are pressured to get those patients out of an acute care bed even if there is no other suitable place for them.

He left the hospital for the Kamloops Christian Men's Hostel, which was his last option. Unfortunately, he threw up there and was tossed out until he could bring a note from his doctor saying he was not contagious. They have 38 other men to care for and can't risk an outbreak of stomach flu.

But it seems a heartless way to treat someone who might have an intestinal problem. That would seem to be the time a person might need to be in bed and close to a bathroom.

Most people, even those without money, have some dignity and they don't really want to return to a place where they've been embarrassed.

So this sick man didn't come back and a couple of days later, he succumbed to his chronic illness. It was an inevitable end, but also an example of what happens when the acute care medical system is underfunded.

Before Liberal MLAs Kevin Krueger and Terry Lake write letters saying how much more money is being spent on health care than ever before, let's admit it's difficult to properly fund the black financial hole of medicine.

Yet something is wrong when a sickly man who was in ICU two days earlier is deemed well enough to leave a hospital bed because someone else needs it more. That dramatically proves more beds are needed somewhere.

This story raises the question as to how many other vulnerable people have died or suffered because the medical care is not in place to look after them when they are sick.

Emergency room staff are the front line in these cases. The pressure must be tremendous on nurses and doctors who have to decide what to do with patients who are not sick enough to admit but who don't have a bed of their own to recuperate in.

Thanks to a variety of social agencies, homeless people and those who live in poverty have more of a voice. These advocates are the reason that homeless people have been put on the high-risk category for H1N1 and will be among the first to be vaccinated.

The reasoning is that homeless people, who often have compromised immune systems, congregate in shelters where contagious illnesses are a huge threat. As well, though, the rest of us should be concerned that once sick, these unfortunate people are not able to climb into bed with Tylenol.

They might be able to sit at the New Life Mission for the day, but once night comes, it could be a sleeping bag and the park for them.

Of course, if they get pneumonia, they will be considered sick enough for acute care.

Our health-care system has holes. Although most of us understand that being homeless is a miserable existence, we need to realize that being sick and homeless is life-threatening.

More of us have to join the few people who are advocating for this country's poor, regardless of whether we think they brought on their troubles themselves.

That's just an excuse not to care.

Susan Duncan is city editor of the Kamloops Daily News.
