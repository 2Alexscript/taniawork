Diagnosing and treating disease and injury is just one part of a physician's job.

Equally important is spreading the word about the prevention of illness though healthy lifestyles and the avoidance of high-risk behaviour.

Much of it is basic common-sense: don't smoke, keep alcohol consumption down, maintain a healthy weight through sensible eating and regular exercise, and so on.

It's a little trickier when the population faces an unusual health threat like the SARS virus, West Nile virus or the H1N1 influenza virus. Doctors, medical health officers and others must strike a difficult balance, issuing prudent warnings while not panicking the population with dire predictions.

That balance isn't always achieved, as we witnessed earlier this week.

Dr. Khami Chokani, medical health officer for the Prince Albert Parkland Health Region, doubtless had the best of intentions when he spoke out about Saskatchewan's undeniably serious incidence of HIV infection. But issuing a warning of thousands of aboriginal deaths and comparing the province to Sub-Saharan Africa -- where he once worked -- was regrettable and inaccurate.

"If you think decimating the African population was bad . . . HIV in this province will kill 15 to 30 per cent (of the aboriginal population). Not all at one time, but over a five- to 10-year-period," Chokani told the Prince Albert Daily Herald.

Not surprisingly, the story quickly garnered lurid headlines in other provinces, like "AIDS to decimate Saskatchewan aboriginals: MD" and stating that if Chokani's "worst-case scenario comes to pass, that would equate to roughly 42,500 deaths."

Thankfully, cooler thinking later prevailed, with the province's chief medical health officer, Dr. Moira McKinnon, and Chokani stating that the worst-case outcome was extremely unlikely for a variety of reasons, including better treatment here compared to Africa.

Chokani might have overstated his concern, but the province does have a problem; there were 174 new cases of HIV in 2008, -- up 40 per cent, or 50 cases, from 2007. Prior to 2006, only 20 new cases a year were reported.

Most HIV transmission comes though intravenous drug use, with poor and marginalized aboriginal men and women particularly vulnerable. That still leaves a lot of non-aboriginal people with HIV, too.

Regrettably, repeated warnings about intravenous drug use and promiscuous sex haven't stopped such risky behaviour or stemmed the rise in HIV cases.

At a time when people are focused on the H1N1 virus and its possible impact this winter, it would be a mistake to forget about HIV, or think it's something that only affects a small demographic. Long after H1N1 has faded, HIV will still be around -- an issue that concerns us all.

Gardam says kids, in turn, infect their parents, who take the viruses to workplaces, stores, theatres and old age homes.

Therefore, priority targeting of these two groups for flu vaccinations is the best way to keep major outbreaks at bay, the paper's lead author says.

"We were expecting more delicate results - that in this situation you should do this, and in this situation you should do that," says Jan Medlock, a mathematician at South Carolina's Clemson University.

But no matter the quality or amount of vaccine you have, or the type of flu you're dealing with, the school strategy would work best to halt the spread and save lives, Medlock says.

The study used mathematical modelling from surveys and two prior influenza pandemics, 1918 and 1957, to determine optimal vaccination ages, he says.

Looking at death and infection rates, years of life lost and economic costs, Medlock found that inoculating children aged 5 to 19 (largely students) and adults aged 30 to 39 (key parenting years) would stop the spread most effectively and with the fewest number of shots.

Verbruggen sells his hogs to Maple Leaf based on U.S. market prices, which are now below his production costs, he said.

If market prices don't rise soon, Verbruggen said he'll be unable to pay his bills within three months.

"In November, I'm going to have a problem," he said. "When I cannot pay my bills, I cannot buy feed anymore."

If it reaches that point, Verbruggen said, his only option will be to sell all his sows and close his farm.

It's a reality he witnessed only last week, when a neighbouring farmer shut down his own hog production.

Verbruggen said he's approached every major bank in Canada in hopes of securing a loan, but has been turned away each time.

He's hoping the outcome will be different with the government-backed loans, especially since he's running out of time.

"I need a loan, but I need it right away," he said. "That's what we need to survive the next three months."

"I'm trying to survive because you always have bad times, but after that, you sometimes have good times."

So many people, in fact, that Kynoch's concerned there won't be enough money for everyone who needs it.

My daughter and I are planning to visit China during August and September. Could you give us some advice to avoid swine flu (H1N1)?

When the H1N1 vaccine becomes available, we would urge everyone to be vaccinated because there will be enough vaccine for everybody, but we don't anticipate that happening until November.

At this point, clinical trials of the vaccine are ongoing, but it looks like, to get adequate immunity, people will need two doses of the vaccine about a month apart, which means you'd have to plan to have it well in advance of your trip.

Generally, after the last dose, you require at least two more weeks before your body has got enough immunity to protect you.

Because we don't have a pandemic vaccine now, the advice when travelling is to wash your hands frequently, and take along some alcohol-based hand sanitizer in case you don't have ready access to running water.

If you're coughing and sneezing, cover your mouth and nose with a tissue or with your shirt sleeve.

Try to avoid contact with people who seem to be coughing, but it's not always easy, especially in a country like China, where it's pretty crowded.

People need to understand that (swine flu) is a dynamic situation and it changes from week to week. We've been through the first wave of H1N1 and we anticipate that come fall/winter, we'll see further activity and we don't know how intense that's going to be.
