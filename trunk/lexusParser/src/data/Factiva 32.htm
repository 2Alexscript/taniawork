Symptoms and Severity

How do I know if I or my children have the H1N1 virus or seasonal flu?

It's believed that there is no seasonal flu in Ottawa now. If you or someone in your family is showing flu-like symptoms, then it's most likely you have H1N1. The only way to confirm that is to have a laboratory test, and only flu patients who are hospitalized will continue to be tested for H1N1.

What are the symptoms of the H1N1 virus?

Everybody who gets the flu is going to have a fever and cough, and likely a sore throat, runny or stuffy nose, body aches, headache, chills and fatigue. In some cases they will have diarrhea and vomiting.

What should I do if I or my child get sick?

Stay home and avoid contact with other people, except to get medical care. This means avoiding normal activities, including work, school, travel, shopping, social events and public gatherings.

When should I call my doctor?

Most children who get sick can be cared for at home. But for children over five years old, the symptoms that should trigger a call to a physician are:

- fast or troubled breathing,

- bluish or grey skin colour,

- not drinking enough fluids,

- severe or persistent vomiting,

- not waking up or not interacting,

- being so irritable that the child does not want to be held, and

- flu-like symptoms improve but then return with fever and worse cough.

In adults, you should call your doctor if you experience the following:

- difficulty breathing or shortness of breath,

- bluish or grey skin colour,

- bloody or coloured sputum,

- chest pains,

- sudden dizziness or confusion,

- severe or persistent vomiting, and/or

- high fever lasting more than three days.

At-risk patients -- children under five, especially under two; pregnant women; people with chronic conditions such as heart, liver, kidney or lung disease, asthma, neurological disorders and people who are immuno-suppressed, or have blood disorders, diabetes, or severe obesity -- should call their physicians as soon as they come down with the typical flu symptoms, because they are at high risk of developing the complications of H1N1 and need to be treated early.

At what point is a fever considered high or dangerous?

A temperature is a fever when it's over 38 C. When it gets up to 39.5 to 40 C is when you start to consider it high for adults and children. "Unless it gets well over 43 Celsius, a fever is not harmful, it's just indicative of something going on," says Hui.

When will I know it's time to go to the hospital?

It's hard to generalize, but if the ill person is unresponsive or difficult to rouse, or having great difficulty breathing, they should probably go straight to emergency. A call to your family doctor may help you decide. If you don't have one, try Telehealth Ontario at 1-866-797-0000, which is staffed 24/7.

Should I call an ambulance or should I drive?

Again, it's difficult to generalize, but if the person is turning blue or a mottled colour, or loses consciousness and can't be easily roused, then you should call 911 for an ambulance. If your child is ill and you decide to drive, says Dr. Ken Farion, acting medical director in the emergency department at CHEO, "try to avoid being a parent alone driving a child to Emergency -- you can't be watching your child and watching the road at the same time. If there isn't another parent at home, ask a neighbour to drive you."

Is Tamiflu a good idea for children under 10?

The treatment for people who are already sick with influenza "has been shown to be effective in many studies, even for kids," says Hui.

How can I get Tamiflu?

By prescription from a primary-care physician. It's important to know that it works best when it's given earlier, within 48 hours.

My child was sick, got better and now is sick again -- should we get a Tamiflu prescription?

That depends on other factors, so it's best to consult your doctor.

How soon can my child go back to school after being sick?

Children have to be without a fever for more than 24 hours and their symptoms have to be completely resolved except for a small cough before they can go back to school. It can take seven to 10 days to get over H1N1 and the cough can linger for many weeks.

If my child is healthy but I'm hearing that lots of kids at school are sick, should I keep my child home? Should we avoid sports and other group activities?

No -- Ottawa Public Health is monitoring the situation and if need be, they will ask for school closures. If your child is well, he or she can go to school. Public Health is not recommending that people avoid mass gatherings.

Will the school board identify the schools with high rates of H1N1 so parents can make their own decisions about keeping their kids at home?

No. The school board only knows which schools have high rates of absenteeism, not what's keeping children away.

How long can an infected person spread the H1N1 virus to others?

People infected with H1N1 or seasonal flu can infect others from one day before showing symptoms to seven days afterward.

What can I do to protect myself from getting sick?

Get the H1N1 vaccine when it is available at your local clinic operated by public health. For addresses and information on Ottawa, go to ottawa.ca/health; the Citizen has information on the city and outlying areas at ottawacitizen.com/flu.

Vaccine

Should my child get the H1N1 vaccine if he's already had the flu?

"If the child has had a laboratory-confirmed H1N1 infection in the spring, and they're otherwise well, then they do not need the vaccine," says Hui.

That's different from somebody who went to a doctor and was told they had the flu -- that's not laboratory-confirmed diagnosis. If you're at high risk or you are immuno-suppressed, then CHEO infectious-disease physicians say that regardless of whether you've been tested for it before, get the vaccine. Even if you've already had H1N1, the vaccine will provide further protection.

If my child is feeling sick now, should she still get the vaccine now?

If she has a mild illness, she should get it. If she has a high fever and is acutely unwell, she should not get it until her symptoms improve.

Can my four-month-old baby get the vaccine?

No. Children under six months should not get the vaccine. Children from six months to 10 years should get the vaccine in two doses, 21 days apart, though the science is still evolving on whether one shot might be enough. Stay tuned.

How soon after the shot will the vaccine take effect?

It depends whether you're getting one or two shots. Preliminary studies show that antibodies develop seven to 10 days after, and full coverage is achieved 21 days later.

For children, it's believed that full protection is achieved 21 days after the second shot.

What kind of adverse reactions should I look for after my children get the vaccine?

Some people experience pain and minor swelling at the site of the injection. Some have fatigue, headache and joint pain, and some people will get muscle pains.

"These are known adverse events, they're not reason to seek medical attention," says Hui.

If anything else develops and seems to be persistent, you should speak to a doctor.

"I would say the benefit of getting the H1N1 vaccine far outweighs any potential or theoretical risk out there," says Hui.

How should parents react to hearing stories like the one in Toronto, where the 13-year-old boy went from a sore throat to death in just days?

"It's always tragic when someone dies, for any reason," says Hui. "Influenza is known to cause the deaths of many people on a yearly basis. A very small minority of those are otherwise healthy people. So it does occur, but that's not the vast majority of people.

"What we are experiencing here and in the U.S. is that with H1N1, most of the people who have complications associated with H1N1 are people with underlying disease.

"However, there are some people who are otherwise well that do unfortunately die. We are unable to identify who those people are right now.

"It causes a lot of anxiety to parents when something like that happens, but it is not common, it's very rare," says Hui.

Saskatoon Health Region officials disagree. At a news conference Tuesday, they said Saskatoon hospitals have enough respiratory therapists and other health professionals to handle an influx of patients on ventilators during a mild to moderate pandemic flu outbreak.

Deb Gudmundson, executive director of acute care for the region, says a mass immunization campaign for health-care workers and the public, currently underway, should prevent an outbreak of the virus from reaching a worst-case scenario.

Driol says he believes an appropriate staffing level in Saskatoon should be one respiratory therapist for every four to six ventilated patients. Currently, he says, the region has one therapist working for every six to eight ventilated patients.

But Karen Levesque, director of general medicine for the health region, says neither the region nor the Canadian Society of Respiratory Therapists has any guidelines about such ratios. Matlow says first and foremost, kids who are not feeling well should stay home.

Parents shouldn't be worried that children could consume food contaminated with H1N1 because swallowing the virus doesn't cause infection, says Dr. Bonnie Henry, director of Public Health Emergency Management at the B.C. Centre for Disease Control.

But Henry suggests parents might want to be a little more cautious than normal when shopping for costumes for their children.

A mask in a costume store could potentially be contaminated for a few hours if the last person to try it on was sick and coughed while wearing it, although the risk is low.

Henry suggests a better way to buy masks is for parents to visually size them up for fit and then wipe them down with a sanitizing cloth before handing them over.

The deadly 1918 Spanish flu is making headlines again as a new pandemic sweeps the world.

So far, H1N1 has proven to be far less of a force than the feared virus that ravaged the world in 1918, killing 20 million to 40 million people including 50,000 Canadians. It was almost as deadly as the First World War.

Called the Spanish flu because Spain alerted the world to the pandemic, it has been top of mind since H1N1 burst out in Mexico in April, quickly spread around the globe, and declared a pandemic in June by the World Health Organization.

Both pandemics were caused by an influenza A H1N1 virus never before circulated among humans.

Both attacked young healthy people, unlike normal seasonal flu that primarily targets seniors and young children.

And both came in waves -- first appearing in the spring and coming back stronger in the fall.

The key difference is that H1N1 has been a lamb compared to the Spanish flu's lion.

Go to the ER if you develop:
