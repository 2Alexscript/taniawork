To vaccinate or not to vaccinate.

That is one of a host of difficult questions that Canadian public-health officials will face if the swine flu outbreak escalates.

In 1976, then U.S. president Gerald Ford ordered a national vaccination campaign in response to an outbreak of swine flu at a military base in New Jersey. In the end, only one person died from swine flu, while roughly 25 people died from a rare neurological syndrome believed to have been a side-effect of the vaccine. The program, now considered a case study in how not to handle a flu outbreak, cost roughly $500 million US in today's dollars.

Working with the World Health Organization, an international network of scientists is racing to develop a vaccine for the new swine flu strain. Canada is one of the few countries in the world that has a drug manufacturer on contract to supply the vaccine once it is developed.

But the Public Health Agency of Canada estimates it will take as long as six months to produce a vaccine, and some experts believe it could take longer.

"When everybody talks about six months, that's with no glitches. You start to get glitches, and all of a sudden you're fighting to solve problems," said Earl Brown, a flu specialist at the University of Ottawa's faculty of medicine.

Until a vaccine is ready, the primary line of defence will be the government's national stockpile of antiviral drugs. Unlike a vaccine, antivirals do not immunize patients from a flu strain, but they can relieve the symptoms, and some experts believe they can prevent infection and slow its spread.

Canada has stockpiled two antiviral drugs, Tamiflu and Relenza. The stockpile contains 55 million doses, which officials say is enough to treat the Canadians who will require medical treatment during a pandemic. But Canadian officials and medical experts warn the current swine flu strain could become resistant to such antivirals if the drugs are used on a wide scale too early.

"The good news so far is that the antivirals are working," said Dr. Robert Ouellet, president of the Canadian Medical Association. He cautioned that Canadians who haven't been exposed to the outbreak shouldn't look to antivirals as a preventive measure. "Some people will be afraid and will ask to have antivirals right now, even if they don't have the disease.... This is not something we should do."

Even with the stockpile, federal and provincial officials and health-care workers will have to decide on the fly how to coordinate testing for the flu, as well as how to distribute and administer the antivirals.

The logistical challenges would be even more daunting if a mass vaccination program becomes necessary. The Public Health Agency admits there would be many unknowns in the event of a pandemic, including the nature and severity of the flu strain, the availability and safety of a vaccine and the tone of the public's reaction.

For one thing, the government will have to decide who gets the vaccine first, a potentially explosive decision. The agency has identified several categories of people who would likely be first in line, including health-care workers, emergency responders and "key societal decision makers," but it says the decision would depend on the nature of the pandemic.

Despite such challenges, health officials and pandemic experts insist Canada is better prepared than during the SARS outbreak of 2003, which killed 44 Canadians and cost the economy an estimated $1.5 billion.

The outbreak prompted the creation of the Public Health Agency, which now coordinates Canada's pandemic plan.

Don't count on those disposable masks to completely protect you against the swine flu percolating around the globe.

Many people in Mexico City, as well as travellers to and from there, have been seen wearing mouth and nose protection of one type or another in recent days.

Most were wearing face masks, which are loose-fitting and designed to help stop droplets from spreading from the person wearing the mask. They also protect the wearer's mouth and nose from splashes. They are not created to protect the wearer from breathing in very small particles.

Respirators, on the other hand, are made for just that. They are similar in appearance to the relatively inexpensive face masks but are designed to protect the wearer from breathing in particles. These masks, known as N95 for their filtering ability, fit more snugly on the face than face masks so that air is breathed through the filter. They work best if they are fitted to the person wearing the mask.

A respirator that fits snugly can filter out small particles that can be inhaled around the edges of a face mask. But it's easier to breathe through a face mask than a respirator over a long period of time, U.S. Centers for Disease Control and Prevention experts said.

Federal health officials said Monday that they don't know how helpful either device is in preventing swine flu infection.

If used correctly, masks and -- more likely -- respirators might reduce the risk of getting influenza, according to the CDC.

But for people who live in areas with confirmed cases, no single action will provide complete protection. It simply isn't practical to wear a mask all the time, and they are not 100-per-cent effective.

Face masks and respirators are most useful for people in crowded settings, such as classrooms or airplanes, to protect the mouth and nose from inhaling germs and to reduce the likelihood of coughing or sneezing on other people.

Face masks and respirators are often recommended for people in close contact with an infected person. "Close contact" is defined as a metre or less by the World Health Organization and two metres or less by the U.S. Occupational Safety and Health Administration.

Face masks and respirators should be worn once, according to the World Health Organization. Look for masks labeled for surgical, dental, medical procedure, isolation or laser use.

Health experts say that masks and respirators should be used along with other precautions, such as frequent hand-washing, covering coughs, staying at home if ill and avoiding crowds.

This epidemic is caused by a novel strain of influenza A/H1N1 virus. Predicting the progress of flu pandemics is fraught with problems, given the ease with which influenza spreads and its inherent biological unpredictability. It is too early to tell if the swine flu outbreak that began in central Mexico and has spread to more than 10 countries across the globe will reach the severity of the deadly 1918-1919 pandemic that killed approximately 55,000 in Canada, 675,000 in the United States, and 50 million people worldwide.

Like then, we potentially are confronting a virus which appears to sicken and sometimes kill not only the very old and the very young, but those in the primes of their lives. However, due to developments in science and public health, we are in a much better position to weather this crisis. Scientists already have identified the strain of this new A/H1N1 virus, epidemiological surveillance is moving at a nearly instantaneous pace, and there exists at least a limited stockpile of anti-virals that can help treat those infected. Seed virus already has been collected to manufacture a swine flu vaccine in the months to come.

It is imperative that government authorities -- at the local, state, national and international levels -- monitor the situation, communicate with one another, and assure that they are ready to intervene immediately if evidence of a deadly pandemic emerges.

And this is precisely what's been happening. For example, officials at the U.S. Centers for Disease Control and Prevention have been in constant contact with Mexican health authorities, Health Canada, the health agencies of other affected nations, the World Health Organization, as well as local authorities in communities affected by the flu.

No matter what any expert tells you, it is unclear how this global health event will unfold in the weeks and months to come. But even if this is not the Hurricane Katrina of flu crises, it would be foolish not to use this situation to enhance pandemic preparedness. In reality, the alternative to over-reacting is under-reacting. And that is a prescription for disaster.

On April 28, Premier Dalton McGuinty declared that Ontario was well prepared to handle a swine flu outbreak having "learned the painful lessons of SARS six years ago." In case you forgot, it was then that Toronto became our hemisphere's epicentre for an outbreak which took the lives of 44 Canadians, infected over 400, and involved the home quarantine of thousands more. In the years since, Ontario and Toronto have worked diligently to develop pandemic preparedness plans and applied many lessons from that episode. The real test of the protocols they have developed will depend largely on the spread and progress of swine flu.

But even with the most sophisticated planning, hitting the sweet spot in epidemic control -- one that protects the most people from disease and minimizes disruptions to society -- is even tougher than hitting a fast ball thrown by a major league pitcher.

The greatest tension that erupted during the SARS epidemic in Toronto related to pushback against recommended home quarantines for the sick and the heavy responsibility placed on front-line health care professionals. Nearly nine decades earlier, during the 1918-1919 influenza pandemic, North American communities faced similar tensions when it came to restrictions on individual liberties during an extended health emergency.

Confronting escalating cases and deaths in the fall of 1918, communities had to decide whether and when the public health measures that today we term non-pharmaceutical interventions (NPI) -- such as quarantine and public gathering bans -- should be voluntary or mandatory and, if decreed by law, how they could be enforced fairly and effectively. Communities in Canada and the United States navigated these tricky decisions as they determined when and if to shutter schools, how to close theatres and business establishments without inciting panic, and how to encourage volunteers, above all women who were seen as natural caretakers, to nurse the ill.

The historical record and recent studies of pandemic awareness suggest that the majority of people will acquiesce to if not embrace voluntary NPI. Compliance with home quarantine was very high during the 2003 SARS epidemic in Toronto. Furthermore, a 2006 health survey conducted by Harvard University indicates that most Americans voluntarily would sequester themselves at home during a severe pandemic for at least one month. Today, one is impressed by the willingness of most Mexico City citizens to wear the face masks that the police have been distributing.

It is heartening that many people, due to a combination of self interest and civic duty, are willing to comply with voluntary NPI, at least for certain periods of time. Such goodwill, however, can evaporate quickly when government officials institute mandatory NPI, particularly if such orders are not communicated well.

In 1918, a mandatory face mask ordinance in San Francisco was met with protests, scoff-laws, and time-consuming arrests. One of the lessons from 1918 is that officials should be careful not to squander the first instance of implementation when community receptivity is likely to be the highest. The 1918 experience reveals numerous instances when NPI were activated, de-activated, and then re-activated, ultimately leading to skepticism or fatigue among city residents.

In the end, the effective management of the swine flu of 2009 will require a solid foundation of public health science that is acted upon with high degrees of inter-agency co-ordination, transparent communication, and civic co-operation.

Regardless of the outcome of this crisis, we all need to do our part to co-exist in a world stalked by newly emerging and deadly infectious diseases.

A 2008 paper prepared for a Queen's University conference on pandemic preparedness declared: "An interdependent, interconnected and highly mobile world appears to be concurrent with potential and extant threats to global public health security on scales heretofore unimagined."
