A number -- among them several affluent countries with antiviral stockpiles and pandemic vaccine contracts -- insisted the lack of severity associated with this new strain of flu means it doesn't merit the name.

The job of defining what constitutes a pandemic belongs to scientists, not politicians. But even scientists have divergent views about what is unfolding with the new H1N1 virus.

"I think it is a pandemic already,'' says Dr. Allison McGeer, an influenza expert at Toronto's Mount Sinai Hospital, which this week admitted two H1N1 patients. Yesterday, Ontario's confirmed case count jumped 45 per cent, to 272 from 187.

Dear Lonely,

You'll have to overcome management's design blunders by changing your habits. Take the long way around, use different elevator banks, bathrooms and coffee machines, and seek out colleagues where they sit to get around the fact that the transition team didn't create areas where people naturally converge.

This meandering will not only boost your morale but your efficiency. All recent evidence points to spontaneous meetings as the germs of creativity – a formula that any good sales team knows well. When salespeople fly thousands of miles to meet and greet clients, they know that a handshake transmits much more than swine flu. It's all about building trust through brief, even chance encounters.

Barry Wellman, a sociologist at University of Toronto and the director of NetLab, a research group devoted to computer networks, is one of the world's experts on connecting through technology. In the early nineties, he and his colleagues used webcams and other forms of electronic media to exchange ideas and keep tabs on each other.

But these days he prefers rubber soles to fibre optics, he told me when I met him over breakfast last month. When he wants to chat with U of T colleagues, Prof. Wellman walks down the halls, wryly calling this type of communication “sneaker net.”

His immediate impression of your employer? “I think they're in trouble. You want to have places people go to casually. And this is the tip of the iceberg for remote workers, because informal interaction drives a firm's tacit knowledge.”

In fact, the free-wheeling give-and-take of our conversation, and our subsequent e-mail contact, reflect one of his research findings: Face-to-face communication is the catalyst for most electronic communication.

In 2004, Prof. Wellman and Anabel Quan-Haase, now a sociology professor at University of Western Ontario, published a study that showed how people who work right near each other communicate more often with each other than they do with people whose offices are on different floors.

That shouldn't be surprising, but here's the kicker: They communicate more using the electronic gadgets that are supposed to cancel out the effects of physical distance.

Being close by meant that they called, e-mailed and texted each other more often than they did with more far-flung colleagues – whether these colleagues were one floor up or in Hong Kong. It turns out that proximity matters.

Keeping “sneaker net” in mind, one guideline to decreasing your isolation is to alter your patterns of movement within the building. Make that more likely to happen by changing your destination. Walking toward a coffee machine located at the end of a long corridor prompts people who don't usually work together to bump into each other on their way to a cup of joe and then congregate in front of the machine, creating what University of Waterloo research psychologist Colin Ellard calls a “thirdspace” – a water-cooler-like area where information is exchanged.

In his engaging new book, You Are Here: Why We Can Find Our Way to the Moon But Get Lost in the Mall, Prof. Ellard shows how basic features of our environment – from where we place our desks and coffee makers to the vantage point from a favourite armchair – reflect our mental architecture: the maps in our heads that allow us to move toward what is important to us, while keeping tabs on possible threats.

Prof. Ellard also cited research showing that people whose offices are separated by more than 30 metres almost never meet spontaneously. But bumping into someone by accident, “that's where creativity is sparked,” he told me.

Of course, it all depends on what you're after. In professional offices, fostering collegiality lines up nicely with other corporate goals, but that's not always true in other kinds of workplaces.

When I took a visitor to the Montreal Casino, I discovered a warren of slot machines and gaming tables so tightly packed that it was all but impossible not to cross paths with other players.

No one really wants to bump into their supervisor or bank manager in that environment, but bump we did, into my son's third-grade teacher. When I encountered her by the blackjack tables, she deftly averted her eyes.

The space was expertly designed to part people with their cash, but the architect forgot to consider another human factor: the desire to be in your own bubble when an activity is taboo. In my view, we'd have been better off in your new office space, designed as it is for lone actors in isolated lairs.

It is incumbent on you to get around this secret society with the help of “sneaker net.” Don't wait for next spring: do it now.

It takes a village to kill a fever.

That, at least, is the simple idea behind a research project in Queensland, Australia. Dengue fever has ravaged the population there and scientist Paul Young is trying to develop a rather counterintuitive vaccine that does nothing to help the person who actually gets the shot.

In fact, he says, the best way to stop the fever is to not treat people when they get sick, other than alleviating their symptoms, but to give them a vaccine that stops the disease from spreading to other people.

Dubbed an "altruistic vaccine," it poisons the blood of people with dengue fever, killing the mosquitoes that spread the disease before they can fly away and infect another person.

It's a relatively new idea, and one that has attracted the attention of the Bill and Melinda Gates Foundation (of Microsoft Corp. fame), which has donated $100,000 (U.S.) to Young's research.

"I am thrilled to have received this grant, as it will allow us to kick-start a new an untested strategy for disease control," Young said in a news release from Queensland University, where he teaches at the School of Chemistry and Molecular Biosciences.

Dengue is a flu-like disease spread by mosquitoes. It usually starts as a sudden high fever, rash, severe headache, and pain behind the eyes and in muscles and joints. Nausea, vomiting and loss of appetite are common. Dengue hemorrhagic fever is a severe, often fatal, complication.

There is no specific treatment or vaccine. The fever's spread is fought by getting rid of mosquitoes either with pesticides or by draining standing water wherever possible.

"Mosquito-transmitted pathogens such as dengue and malaria are a significant disease burden on society, particularly in the developing world," Young points out.

To get the money, he wrote a two-page description of how his idea falls outside current scientific paradigms and might lead to significant advances in global health, a requirement for the Gates funding.

There is an altruistic element to most vaccination programs, says Angus Dawson, a visiting senior research fellow at University of Toronto's Joint Centre for Bioethics.

Traditional vaccines, he says, offer more than just protection for those getting the shot. They also help stop the spread of a disease to other people and that's why governments back vaccination programs.

"The primary objective is to protect the population," Dawson says.

Altruistic vaccines take that principle one step farther by offering no personal protection to the person getting the medication, only a channel to help stop the spread of the disease.

"You have to switch from thinking purely about personal benefit," says Dawson, founding director of the Centre for Professional Ethics at Keele University in Britain.

Young's project is one of 81 chosen by the Gates Foundation from more that 3,000 international proposals.

Among the chosen 81 ideas are a British idea to use magnets to diagnose malaria, a New Jersey project to develop a tomato that delivers antiviral drugs, and a Tanzanian plan to design a better mosquito trap.

Dengue afflicts between 50 million and 100 million people a year, mostly in tropical areas. The disease has been spreading in recent years in the Queensland area of northeast Australia.

A recent study, also involving Queensland University researchers, suggests it will soon be a bigger problem, because of global warming. The study, published in the journal PLoS Neglected Tropical Diseases, predicted dengue fever could be in Australia's major cities by 2050.

One of my favourite books belongs to a time-honoured, yet oh-so-unfortunately-zeitgeistful literary micro-genre, the plague novel. Among the great inventions of infection are Albert Camus's The Plague, Gabriel García Márquez's wondrous Love in the Time of Cholera and Jose Saramago's Blindness. As well, Camus, Boccaccio and Petrarch, Pushkin and Chaucer were all inspired by the ravages of the Black Death, and most writers in the genre owe a debt to Daniel Defoe's 1722 A Journal of the Plague Year. Meanwhile, the germ that will become the definitive SARS novel or the great H1N1 romance remains latent on somebody's hard drive somewhere.

Published in 1951, The Horseman on the Roof (Le Hussard sur le toit) is a delicately written, rollicking and profound masterwork by Jean Giono, a French writer born in Provence. Giono wrote dozens of other novels, collections of poetry and plays, and died in 1970. This novel describes the experiences of Angelo, a young Italian noble who had been banished from Italy to France after a duel, and who passes through cholera-stricken Provence on his way back home.

The great, giddy delight of this book lies in its shameless buckling of swashes, its joyous derring-do. Add to that an unexpected but pervasive humour, conveyed mostly through the dissonance between the hero's idealistic way of thinking and his rather foolish aspect (but as we come to see, his commitment to his ideals is deeper than one supposes at the outset). Angelo, you see, is a young fop, an aristocrat with a purchased army rank who nevertheless believes in the great virtues of courage and loyalty. The humour comes out through his awkward, wordy, contradictory, overweening but endearing internal monologues:

It's OK. We know you've been busy, caught up in playoff hockey, Star Trek on the big screen and that season finale of Lost. You've been distracted by the buzz around swine flu and liquor prices going up, and how dreamy that Adam Lambert is on American Idol--oh, it's just been a crazy spring.

So amid all this, it was tough for you to know that our professional lacrosse team, the Calgary Roughnecks, was destroying the competition in the National Lacrosse League playoff run. And tonight, these heavy hitting lads are on the cusp of winning their second National Lacrosse League championship as they take on the New York Titans at 7:30 p. m. at the Saddledome.

You've been there from the start, though, right? Of course you have.

Don't worry, we're here to help you jump on that bandwagon as gracefully as you can, without calling attention to yourself. We want to help you so you can help our Calgary Roughnecks bring home that cup (something we saw in a Kevin Spacey movie about paying it forward).

So unpack that red garb you put away when both the Flames and the Hitmen got knocked out of their playoffs. Rush the box office for some last-minute tickets. Your Roughnecks need you.

To help you fully appreciate tonight's thriller, here are 10 things you should know about our Roughnecks.

1 Kaleb Toth is the Jarome Iginla of the Calgary Roughnecks, both in spirit and on the scoresheet. The assistant captain scored 33 goals and 80 points this season, both team highs. He is playing in his third NLL championship tonight after winning with the Toronto Rock in 2000, and then the Roughnecks in 2004. His last-second goal in 2000, to give the Rock a 14-13 win over the Rochester Knighthawks, is considered one of the all-time greatest in the short history of the league. It was also the last goal to ever be scored at Maple Leaf Gardens.

2 The Roughnecks are also called the Riggers, much like the Montreal Canadiens are also called the Habs. The Roughnecks are also the last professional Canadian sports team left in any playoffs this spring.

3 Spoiled rich athletes? Hardly. Most NLL players have day jobs outside their commitments with their teams, as the average pay is $14,500 a season, and top pay is $25,000. Riggers defender Bruce Codd is a supermarket manager in Orangeville, while goalie Matt King is an elementary school teacher in Duncan, B. C. Ironically, his rival across the rink, Titans goalie Matt Vinc, is also an elementary school teacher in St. Catharines, Ont.

4 The top-seeded Roughnecks secured home floor advantage throughout these playoffs after a franchise-best 12-4 regular season record, including five straight wins to open the season. To get to the championship game, they rocked the No. 4 seed Colorado Mammoth 15-8 in the West Division Semifinal, then eliminated the No. 3 seed San Jose Stealth 17-5 in the division final.

5 Despite a stellar year in the record books and some fantastic action on the floor, the Roughnecks are averaging under 10,000 fans per game during these playoffs. It's a sharp drop-off from 2004, when a sellout crowd of 19,289 fans watched them win the cup.

6 In the spirit of competition, Calgary Mayor Dave Bronconnier and New York Mayor Michael Bloomberg have a friendly wager on tonight's final. If the Roughnecks win, Bloomberg will send Bronconnier Irish stew and shepherd's pie from Mary's Celtic Kitchen in Woodlawn, stuffed grape leaves, moussaka and spinach pie from Pinocchio Palace in Astoria, and an assortment of meats and cheeses from Enzo's of Arthur Avenue in the Bronx. If the Titans win, Bronconnier will send Bloomberg a supply of world famous Alberta beef, Italian sausage from Spolumbo's Fine Foods and Deli in Inglewood and beer from the local Big Rock Brewery.

"My hat is off to our friend Mayor Bloomberg," Bronconnier said. "He's pulling out all the stops to support his team, offering a taste of some of New York's most interesting neighbourhoods. I do feel a bit bad, though. I mean, it's going to be a lot of work for him to pick up all that stuff for us on Saturday morning!"

7 Here's some basic rules about the game itself. Each team dresses 18 players, consisting of two goalies and 16 "runners." A team consists of five runners and a goalie. Games consist of four 15-minute quarters, and tied games at the end of regulation are decided with a sudden-death overtime. Like hockey, there are both two-minute penalties and five-minute major penalties, and like basketball, there is a 30-second shot clock.

8 Between players on the Roughnecks and the Titans, there are a combined 15 pro cup rings on the floor tonight. Kaleb Toth (Riggers) is going for his third, while Titans' Dave Stilley will be vying for his fourth.

9 If you'd like to attend tonight's final, you can still purchase tickets at Ticketmaster. Tickets start as low as $15.75, with the top price being $86.62. Stampeders QB Henry Burris will be doing the ceremonial ball drop at the game. If you'd rather stay at home, you can listen to the game on radio on the FAN 960.

10 All 23 active players on the Roughnecks roster are Canadian. If you'd like to send them a word of encouragement for tonight's game, write them an e-mail at NoiseForTheBoys@CalgaryRoughnecks.com. Each message will be directly passed onto the players on game day to help pump them up for the big matchup.

An autopsy on a northern Alberta woman who died after catching H1N1 influenza was unable to determine whether the virus played a role in her death.

The 39-year-old woman from Gift Lake Métis Settlement died in hospital in April after catching the virus from unknown sources. She also had asthma and diabetes.

"The report does not clarify what role the H1N1 virus may or may not have played in her death," Dr. Andre Corriveau, Alberta's chief medical officer of health, said in a news release on Thursday. "These results were not unexpected. In all cases with chronic pre-existing medical conditions, those patients are always at greater risk," he said, adding the woman had a mild strain of the virus.

Provincial health officials said six new cases of H1N1 were confirmed on Thursday, bringing the number to 67 cases in the province. Twenty-four of the cases are from the Calgary area, 18 from the Edmonton region, one from southern Alberta, 14 from central Alberta and 10 from the north.

Mild symptoms have been reported in all but five of the cases. A woman from southern Alberta and a boy from Calgary have been hospitalized. A girl is still recovering in an Edmonton-area hospital.

Of the confirmed cases, 30 had travelled to areas affected by the virus; nine had contact with travellers to affected areas; 16 cases had no travel connection and 12 cases have travel or exposure histories yet to be determined.

The provincial government has also released a guide for workplaces in the event of a pandemic, although the risk on most sites is low.

Corriveau said the its expected the H1N1 virus will re-appear in the fall or winter.

"Our advice remains the same: stay home if you are sick, wash your hands often, and sneeze or cough into your sleeve, not your hand."

Federal Health Minister Leona Aglukkaq will travel to Switzerland this weekend to attend the World Health Organization's annual assembly, where the outbreak of H1N1 swine flu -- which has spread to 33 nations -- is expected to dominate discussions.

Aglukkaq is heading a delegation of Canadian health officials and while in Geneva for the World Health Assembly she plans to meet with WHO director-general Margaret Chan, as well as her U.S. and Mexican counterparts. She will also participate in a panel discussion on the virus that originated in Mexico and has hit the U.S. the hardest with more than 4,000 confirmed infections.

Among the topics that will be reviewed during the meetings is whether to direct manufacturers to begin production of a vaccine against the virus.

"It's an international joint decision," Aglukkaq said Thursday after appearing before the House of Commons health committee.

Expert scientists convened by WHO had a discussion Thursday about whether mass production of a vaccine should be undertaken. Acting assistant director-general Keiji Fukuda said in a briefing for reporters that "no big decisions" had been made and that more meetings are needed.

If the UN agency does recommend that manufacturers proceed with a vaccine for the virus, it would disrupt the production of seasonal influenza vaccines which is why, according to Fukuda, the decision requires careful consideration.

The seasonal flu causes three to five million cases of severe illness a year and according to WHO, kills between 250,000 and 500,000. In Canada, it is estimated that between 4,000 and 8,000 people can die of influenza and its related complications yearly.

The recent outbreak of H1N1 in humans has so far killed 65 people worldwide and infected 6,497 people, WHO reported Thursday.

However, that figure did not include the jump in cases in the U.S. that was reported Thursday by the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention. The number of confirmed cases in the U.S. rose by 946 and now stands at 4,298.

Only West Virginia, Mississippi, Wyoming and Alaska have escaped the spread of the virus.
