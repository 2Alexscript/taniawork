The common thread among the half-dozen speakers at the recent Economics Society of Northern Alberta conference on recovery was that change is at hand in almost every aspect of our lives.

It may range from farmers raising bison and sheep as significant revenue sources, to cuts in government spending and increases in consumption taxes, to new technologies causing us to embrace solar and nuclear energy plus natural gas as primary power sources.

"We are in a period of extreme change, and it's not going away," said Peter Tertzakian, chief economist of ARC Financial, and author of books A Thousand Barrels a Second and The End of Energy Obesity. "There's always opportunity in change."

Therein lies the warning, and challenge, for investors. Those who seek and recognize future changes in how we live and work can prosper from them.

Tertzakian showed how energy conservation has evolved from an automatic candle snuffer that put out the flame after a candle burned down a certain length, to a box by Cisco Systems that continually adjusts energy needs for lighting, heating and powering machines throughout a building, cutting electricity consumption by 10 to 15 per cent.

During that stretch of history, we used ice blocks for refrigeration until machines were developed to do the job. We burned whale oil for lighting, and when whale stocks depleted we moved the processing "factories" from land onto the whaling ships to reduce oil deterioration. We evolved from heating with coal gas to the use of stranded "nature's gas" when pipeline welding allowed it to be transported great distances.

"The technologies that change the game are going to come from outside the energy complex, much as welding affected the fortunes of coal," said Tertzakian. " Google, Cisco and IBM are largely in the business of saving energy."

Tertzakian said the problem is that two-thirds of all barrels of oil produced go to transportation, and it isn't until "around $85 a barrel when you start to get substitution, you start to get behavioural change, when people seek alternatives."

"And the free market is the most powerful thing to sort things out, within boundaries. The role of government is to set the boundaries. The most powerful changes have nothing to do with government incentives."

That dovetails with the message from Stewart Hall, an economist with HSBC Securities Canada.

"We need to hand off the public sector spending to the private sector at some point in time," said Hall.

He said there are huge profits to be made from other Canadian resources.

"India is set to import rice for the first time in a long period of time (20 years)," said Hall. "There are areas that are not water-rich, and I'm not talking lakes and rivers, I'm talking water in the ground, aquifers.

"Canada is very well-positioned to take advantage of this, because you can set up a virtual trade of water by exporting grain and rice to areas that are water-deficient. Some argue that the next Microsoft is going to be agricultural engineering."

Don Brown, the director of economics and competitiveness with Alberta Agriculture, noted the percentage of Canadian household spending on food and non-alcoholic beverages has dropped from 26 per cent in 1945 to 8.8 per cent currently.

He said Canada's share of world wheat trade is declining, beef is facing "one calamity after another," pork is suffering from high feed costs and the H1N1 flu, while

Canadian consumption of poultry isn't rising as fast as in the United States.

The good news is that there's a growing demand for bison, as well as sheep breeding stock and market lambs.

"Never waste a crisis, never waste a disaster," Brown said. "That is a key requirement for attitude change."

The case, which pitted the Co-operators Life Insurance Company against the former high-pressure water blaster, centred on the meaning of the word "accident" in deciding insurance claims.

The ruling overturned two earlier victories for Gibbens in British Columbia courts, which had ruled that he lost the use of his legs by accident because he could not have anticipated it.

Gibbens, who is in his late 40s and lives in the Vancouver suburb of Port Coquitlam, contracted a rare form of genital herpes after having unprotected sex with three women in early 2003. The virus attacked his spine and paralyzed him from his abdomen down.

"I agree with the courts in British Columbia that Mr. Gibbens's paralysis was tragic and unexpected but I do not agree with them that it was caused by 'external, violent and accidental means' within the meaning of the insurance policy," Justice Ian Binnie wrote in the 9-0 decision.

"In the case of Mr. Gibbens we are dealing with a disease transmitted in the ordinary course of having sex."

The B.C. Supreme Court, in siding with Gibbens two years ago, said his paraplegia was an unexpected outcome of having unprotected sex and that his behaviour, while foolish, was a far cry from engaging in "inordinate risk," such as laying down on the centre line of a highway during traffic.

The ruling was upheld in the B.C. Court of Appeal.

"In ordinary speech, 'accident' does not include ailments proceeding from natural causes," Binnie wrote.

The insurance industry had warned the court against siding with Gibbens, arguing that including diseases in accident coverage would have "potentially profound" effects.

"It maintains the status quo by reaffirming the distinction between an accident and a naturally occurring disease."

Planning a winter vacation in the sun? Now is the time to visit a travel medical clinic to help you stay healthy, both on your holiday and after your return.

"Travel agents are now advising clients to visit travel clinics before they leave home," says Coralie Belman, national certification manager with the Canadian Institute of Travel Counsellors in Toronto. "The staff will have up-to-date, detailed information about international travel destinations, and the health risks travellers may encounter. With the popularity of Internet bookings, some travellers aren't hearing about health hazards outside Canada."

And don't put off your visit until the day before you leave. Dr. Carmen Poirier at Foothills Health Consultants, a medical travel clinic in Calgary, says travellers should visit a travel clinic as soon as they've decided on their destination. They may need to start vaccinations as early as three months before departure because some require several shots that have to be spaced out.

"If you show up the week before your trip, we may not be able to do much for you," she says.

Travellers can also check out Foreign Affairs and International Trade Canada's website for an overview of what immunization is required to enter individual countries.

A list of travel clinics across Canada can be found on the Public Health Agency of Canada's website. Clinics usually charge a consultation fee, but the vaccines may be covered by provincial health plans or private medical plans.

You'll need to tell the health-care professional about the activities you'll be doing on your vacation, Poirier says. If you spend your holiday on the beach at your resort, your health risks may be considerably lower than if you're exploring tropical rainforests and caves.

Poirier says parts of the Dominican Republic are currently at risk for malaria, a mosquito-borne infectious disease widespread in tropical regions. Malaria can be prevented by oral prescription medicine, starting a week or two before departure.

Travellers to Mexican resorts and major cities are not currently at risk from malaria (it is found in parts of the country less often visited by travellers, however) but they should be immunized for hepatitis A, which is contracted from contaminated food and water, and for hepatitis B, contracted from contaminated blood and body fluids.

"Hepatitis A requires two vaccinations, and hepatitis B requires three. You'll need at least two weeks after the last shot for them to be effective," Poirier says. "They're good for 15 to 20 years, possibly longer."

And, depending where you're heading in the Caribbean, Central America or Mexico, you may also require immunization against:

- Rabies. "But we only recommend shots if you'll be caving or with animals," Poirier says.

- Tetanus and diphtheria. "Both require boosters every 10 years," she says.

- Red measles. "If you were born in 1970 or later, and never had red measles, you'll need a booster," she adds.

- Pneumonia. "People age 65 and older should be vaccinated against pneumonia."

Immunization against swine flu (H1N1) is not currently required at any international travel destination, "but I wouldn't advise travelling without it," Poirier says.

Some countries, however, have implemented airport screening for swine flu. Mary Lendway, co-ordinator of the tourism management program at Humber Institute of Technology and Advanced Learning in Toronto, advises being vaccinated for H1N1 at least two weeks before departure, and carrying a doctor's letter saying that you've been inoculated.

"That way, if you have some symptoms when you arrive, you'll have proof of vaccination," she says.

Travellers' diarrhea, a common tropical vacation malady, can be greatly minimized by the oral vaccine Dukoral, Poirier says. The first dose is taken two weeks before departure, followed by a second a week later.

But the biggest threats are on the roads in the Caribbean, Central America and Mexico. "Traffic accidents are the No. 1 killers of tourists in these countries," Poirier says. "In many areas, vehicles are not equipped with seatbelts, and the roads are crazy."

In some places, an injured person who requires a blood transfusion may be at risk from contaminated blood, Poirier adds.

Travellers who are injured abroad should immediately call the emergency number provided by their travel health insurer, Belman says. "You'll be directed to the appropriate hospital and a reputable doctor. Don't try to find a clinic on your own. Not all health facilities in developing countries are up to Canadian standards."

That means keeping your insurer's emergency number with you at all times, and alerting your travel companion of the need to call it if you're unable to do so.

Travel insurance, Belman stresses, is one of the most important things to pack on any trip outside Canada. Treatment in foreign hospitals can run to thousands of dollars a day. Travel health insurance will cover these bills, as well as air evacuation back to Canada.

But, when purchasing travel insurance, it's critical to disclose existing medical conditions. If, for example, you suffer a heart attack or a stroke abroad, the insurer will check with your family doctor. If it learns you had a heart condition you didn't disclose, it can refuse to cover your medical expenses.

"And make sure your insurance is tailored to the activities you'll be doing on your trip," Poirier adds. "Some policies won't cover injuries sustained in extreme sports."

Common sense can reduce your health risks abroad. "Standards of cleanliness may not be what they are in Canada," Belman says. "Look carefully at an establishment before you have a manicure, pedicure, facial or hair treatment. The spa at a five-star hotel, however, will probably be fine."

Watch where you're walking in bare feet. "There may be sting rays or glass on the beach," she adds.

And be careful about what you eat and drink, Belman says. "You may not be drinking the local water, but are you brushing your teeth with it? Is it being used to wash fruit and vegetables on the menu? Peel your fruit and opt for cooked vegetables.

"And avoid eating and drinking alcohol to excess, especially at all-inclusive resorts."

Belman also advises travelling with your own bug repellent: "Levels of DEET may be a lot higher in other countries.

"And bring along your own non-prescription medications," she adds. "Strengths may differ abroad. And, if they're not in English, you may not be able to read the instructions."

Pack a small container of antibiotic hand gel, she suggests, and use it on your hands before eating or touching your face.
