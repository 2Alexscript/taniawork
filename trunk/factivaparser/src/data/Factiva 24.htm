Some important lessons have been learned in this H1N1 vaccination fiasco. My deepest fear is that those who need to learn from it are not.

Flu may claim bigger political victims; Credibility gap widens between Albertans and Conservatives

It should come as no surprise the swine flu has finally claimed its first political victim.

And it should also come as no surprise that the victim is not Health Minister Ron Liepert nor Chief Medical Officer Andre Corriveau nor Alberta Health Services CEO Stephen Duckett.

In a political crisis, the most vulnerable is usually the faceless, expendable bureaucrat.The higher ups are inoculated by their proximity to power.

In this case, the victim was the health-care official who's being blamed for releasing the swine flu vaccine to members of the Calgary Flames and their families.

As I write this, the official's name is being withheld, like something you'd do until the next of kin are notified. The government would prefer the bureaucrat be wheeled out the back door with as little fuss as possible. Details are sketchy about what happened. But then a lot of things about the government's flu pandemic response are sketchy.

I have to admit to having some sympathy for the unnamed health official if he or she was simply trying to follow the mixed messages about who should be immunized: "I urge all Albertans to go out and get immunized"( Liepert on Monday of last week); "We would encourage those who are not at high risk to be patient and wait" (Liepert on Friday).

You would have thought at least by now the health minister's messages would be crystal clear. They're not.

On Monday this week, Liepert tried to counter opposition accusations of incompetence by listing off those high-risk groups who have been immunized. "The homeless have been vaccinated," said Liepert. "I can tell you that our aboriginal population has been vaccinated." And then, in an attempt to shut down NDP Leader Brian Mason, Liepert added, "Every health-care worker in this province who wanted to get vaccinated has been vaccinated."

Within moments, reporters asked health department officials if indeed "our aboriginal population" has been vaccinated--and were told that, uh, no, that's not really the case. Some reserves have seen 90-per-cent vaccination rates but other reserves are much lower. No details were available.

On Tuesday, Liepert had to do some backtracking. The assertion that "every" willing health-care worker in Alberta has been vaccinated was watered down to "every health-care worker will have the opportunity to be vaccinated." The assertion that "the homeless have been vaccinated" was modified to "we are also continuing our vaccinations wherever possible with our homeless community."

Liberal Leader David Swann accused Liepert of misleading the house and demanded he be fired.

Stelmach, predictably, refused.

But you have to wonder if Liepert will eventually be a political victim of the swine flu. Perhaps not today, but a few months from now when the swine flu is an unpleasant memory and Liepert suddenly decides he'd be much happier as minister of Sustainable Resource Development.

If not Liepert, then perhaps it will be Corriveau who suddenly develops a case of resignitis.

Somebody has to take the blame for having Albertans wait in line for hours for a flu shot that the government urged them to get. And somebody should take the blame for having the vaccination clinics shut down for four days in the middle of a pandemic.

Liepert obviously doesn't feel like he's responsible for the confusion, repeatedly saying he was just following the advice of health-care officials. But in the health-care system, Liepert is the ultimate authority, not Corriveau. And it is Liepert's credibility that is key to the government's still ill-defined plans to reform the health-care system.

It was Liepert who shut down the old regional health boards and opened up the new, superboard which had--as its first big test-- the swine flu pandemic.

Right now, Liepert's credibility gap is the Grand Canyon of Alberta politics. They could give helicopter tours.

That's not good news for Stelmach as he heads into a mandatory leadership review at the Conservative's annual convention in Red Deer on Saturday. Nobody is predicting Stelmach will lose outright. Some government MLAs are worried, though, he might get a poor enough rating that he'll be fighting off internal attacks until the next election in two years or so.

However, Stelmach could face problems even if his party rallies behind him in a big way and gives him, say, 85-per-cent support. That would be completely out of step with the views of average Albertans. According to a new opinion poll, support for the Stelmach's government has fallen to 34 per cent of decided voters (with the results considered accurate within 3.1 percentage points).

If Stelmach was to win an overwhelming thumbs-up from his party while the public is giving him a thumbs-down, that would demonstrate a massive credibility gap between Alberta Conservatives and Alberta voters. That might drive more disgruntled conservative supporters to the Wildrose Alliance which, according to the poll, jumped in support to 28 per cent of decided voters.

If this trend continues, the ultimate political victim of the swine flu and other government missteps could yet be Ed Stelmach.

Tuesday, in defending the provincial government's latest about-face in H1N1 vaccine distribution, Alberta's chief medical officer Andre Corriveau described finally targeting children and pregnant women as a "no-brainer."

You'd think those responsible for the confusing mess that the flu shot program has become would be wise to avoid such dangerous talk.

For brainpower has been in little evidence with the conflicting signals that have been sent from the provincial leadership pledged to protect Albertans.

It is easy--and predictable for governments of all stripes everywhere --to wax rhapsodic about readiness and noble intentions. But in these parts, we pride ourselves on performance, not jumped-up rhetoric. We were told that Alberta was prepared to smoothly handle an H1N1 vaccination program, and most of us assumed that would be the case. The cynics have been proven right to some degree--and there is no joy in that for anyone.

It is true enough that there have been problems in other jurisdictions, beginning with Ottawa.

Most Canadians also trusted that the federal government would carry out its part of the bargain, in securing safe supply and sending out solid information. Clearly, there have been serious flaws in its execution and messaging as well. Placing all our vaccine orders with a single Quebec company without a backup hedge seems to have been a mistake. News reports that manufacturer GlaxoSmithKline is actually exporting antigen abroad while Canadians have been stuck in long lineups is little short of infuriating. GSK is sending off some 436,000 doses across the country this week, which is considerably short of the roughly two million doses expected.

And although federal Health Minister Leona Aglukkaq has generally been a refreshing presence in the Harper cabinet, her department's sundry missives over the months haven't always inspired confidence, either. And that is interesting, since for most Canadians, the questions at hand have been rather straightforward.

How serious is the H1N1 virus and what is its projected timetable?Who is most at risk?Who should be inoculated first?Why can't we go to our physicians or at least a wider variety of venues for a shot? Why isn't there proper supply when everyone knew the virus would come back? Why can't governments co-ordinate their acts together? Isn't there an alternative to interminably long queues? What about allergic reactions to the vaccine?

There are those who say the virus will peak by the end of next week. Since the vaccine takes days to kick in, need we panic about getting a dose when and if a supply becomes available for everyone? Is all of this a massive hype? We're still asking the same questions, even though the usually sensible Canadian consensus has been better safe than sorry.

While again affirming that there have been glitches elsewhere, it's only natural that we look for trouble and assign blame first at home. Alas, there has been no shortage of material to work with courtesy of the Stelmach Conservatives.

For the past few days, while other Canadians have been getting their shots, Albertans alone have been shut out.

Speaking of outrages, Tuesday's revelation that Calgary Flames players, staff and family members received special treatment only adds to the cynicism. We await other skates to drop around the country.

In fact, the problems began long ago. It borders on the incredible that Health Minister Ron Liepert would claim that there have been no planning gaffes. How could he possibly say that, given the patent contradictions with himself and Corriveau maintaining that the focus would (properly)be on high-risk Albertans first, at the same time the premier was declaring loudly that no one would be turned away?Are we meant to blame the worried seniors and families who lined up first?

Now that small children and pregnant women will be the only ones able to receive vaccine, what of the other Albertans in high-risk categories who will be denied protection until supplies for the rest of the population arrive?

Rumours are circulating that the government is quietly blaming the shemozzle on the health bureaucracy. Given the dramas that have surrounded public health ranks in the province, there could well be some truth in this. But then, who is ultimately responsible for that? Pathological buck-passing has been a leitmotif of the Stelmach regime since its inception, and so it continues on.

And that brings us to the broader, and far more serious fears associated with the government's program of health-care reform. Let us remind again that this huge, potentially admirable initiative was never mentioned during the last provincial election. No one knew that regional health boards would be scrapped in favour of a single authority, for example.

When it comes to sending out mixed messages, the flu vaccine fiasco might be seen as simply more of the same. In an overall sense, we haven't a clue what sort of planning is underway, and if there is an understanding of the consequences involved. Time and time again in the key health file, the messages have been murky instead of the straight talk and buck-stops-here accountability we were promised. At the sign of serious friction, decisions are reversed. The fault always seems to lie elsewhere.

Few of the legitimate questions Albertans are asking can be seen as political or ideological. Voters massively selected the Stelmach government in a recent election and have shown little faith in the alternatives to date. The rubs haven't been matters of philosophy, however opposition politicians might muse aloud, but of sheer managerial competence and public transparency.

You wonder how delegates to this weekend's Progressive Conservative Party of Alberta convention are feeling this week. Many are hearing loud complaints from their constituents, and the latest H1N1 miscues can't help. Ed Stelmach must be wondering what sort of vaccine his government needs to prevent a further slide.

MPs voted by a clear margin Wednesday to repeal the federal long-gun registry, signalling for the first time since the program was adopted 14 years ago that it is headed for the scrap heap, despite police assertions that it saves lives.

A private member's bill, sponsored by Conservative backbencher Candice Hoeppner, had the backing of all the Tories, from Prime Minister Stephen Harper down, and enough opposition MPs to clear its first major hurdle of winning support in principle.

The bill passed by a surprising 164-137, winning more supporters than expected as 12 New Democrats, eight Liberals and one Independent cast their votes with the government.

No Bloc Quebecois MPs voted in favour of the bill.

The proposed legislation now moves to an all-party committee for public hearings before it returns to the House of Commons for a final vote and then heads to the Senate.

"It's step one but there is still a lot of work to do," Hoeppner said outside the Commons after her bill passed second reading.

The bill would only end the long-gun portion of the registry, eliminating the need to register rifles and shotguns. The requirement to register handguns would remain in place.

If the bill makes it through Parliament, it would mean the dismantling of eight million firearms records, say police.

The registry has been the source of many court battles, bitter party infighting between rural and urban MPs, political rhetoric--and a scathing 2002 report from the federal auditor general that found that final tab to implement the program would ring in at more than $1 billion, dramatically more than the $2-million original estimate.

The Harper government, which has strong rural support, has introduced legislation to kill the registry, but the bill has languished due to a lack of opposition support and government will to propel it through Parliament. MPs are normally permitted to break ranks from the party line for private member's bills, giving Hoeppner's initiative enough support from dissident MPs to succeed, even though the three opposition parties officially support the gun registry.

Pressure was on MPs to show up and the Commons was packed, with 301 of 304 casting votes--including a Bloc Quebecois MP who was wearing a mask because he suspects that he has the H1N1 virus.

Supporters and opponents have campaigned nationwide in recent weeks in an attempt to sway the outcome of the vote, lobbying in newspapers, on radio and in the hallways on Parliament Hill.

The Conservatives backed radio ads urging Canadians in swing ridings to call their MPs to voice their opposition to the registry.

The registry was adopted 14 years ago by the former Liberal government as part of a wider gun-control package, largely due to lobbying from the families who lost daughters in the 1989 Montreal massacre, when Marc Lepine used a semi-automatic rifle to kill 14 women at Ecole Polytechnique. Mothers of victims have campaigned relentlessly in the last week to preserve the registry as a vital component of gun control.

Alberta's health superboard has fired a mid-level manager involved in releasing H1N1 vaccine to the Calgary Flames and their families instead of saving the shots for those at risk of becoming dangerously ill.

The Edmonton Oilers requested no such clinic.

"The special treatment for the Flames and their families is unacceptable to us and contrary to all of our existing protocols and processes," Duckett said in the statement. "I apologize for this breach of our duty to Albertans."

Dr. Gerry Predy, senior medical officer of health for Alberta Health Services, said he thinks the queuejumping incident was isolated.

"We're reasonably certain but again, nothing is 100 per cent, so we have taken every precaution we can to control the supply of vaccine and to just ensure it goes to our clinics," Predy said.

He said the provincial health department receives the vaccination from Health Canada, then distributes it to Alberta Health Services, which is in charge of the vaccination campaign. So far, Predy said, Health Services has supplied vaccine only to the mass clinics, to hospitals where health-care workers are being inoculated and to some primary care networks, in which teams of doctors, nurses, pharmacists and other health professionals work. Those vaccinations are meant for the team members, not the public.

No special clinics were set up to inoculate politicians, Predy said, so those who received their shots stood in line like everyone else. None of the 17 cabinet ministers or three party leaders contacted Wednesday by The Journal had received vaccinations.

Nurses, doctors and others must supply identification to get their in-hospital shots. And now that Alberta is experiencing a vaccine shortage, only those who work face-to-face with patients will be eligible, said Predy, who has not received the vaccine. Supplies are now being saved for children older than six months and under five years, as well as pregnant women.

Dr. Andre Corriveau, Alberta's chief medical officer of health, received his vaccine in the Northwest Territories last week, where his family lives.

"I waited in line like everybody else," Corriveau said.

"Maybe that's part of an old marketing plan, but the plans have changed since last week," Corriveau said. "This vaccine is in short supply around the world, and so in Canada, it's distributed exclusively to the provinces who then redistribute it to the health authorities."

Corriveau said all requests to set up separate clinics--other than the one for the Calgary Flames, which he knew nothing about--were turned down.

The Edmonton Police Commission sent a letter to Alberta's health minister and the solicitor general requesting priority access to the vaccine for officers, arguing they are first responders and their work is essential to minimize social disruption during a pandemic. The government never responded.

"We feel police are every bit as much front-line workers as health-care workers are," acting commission chairman Bob Dunster said. "They have hands-on dealings with the public and put themselves and their families at risk."

To protect ourselves from getting a cold or the flu, physical barriers usually rule the day: Handwashing, not sharing cups or water bottles and avoiding public places are strategies that we hear about over and over again.

But what about your diet? Is there anything you can take that will lower the chances of getting a cold? And once you're in the clutches of one, is there anything that can shorten a cold's duration or make its symptoms less severe?

VITAMIN C

After decades of recommendations based largely around the prevention of scurvy, vitamin C made its way into our collective consciousness with two-time Nobel laureate Linus Pauling's assertions that mega-dosing was a pathway to good health. Now, many of us don't think twice about popping several thousand milligrams at a time, and there is a widely held belief that taking vitamin C at the onset of a cold will hold it at bay.

But what does the research say? In 2007, the Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews -- basically, the granddaddy of evidence-based reviews on medical research -- published an updated review of vitamin C and the common cold, and found that, in a nutshell, yes, taking vitamin C regularly can reduce the length one suffers from a cold -- by 8% in adults and 14% in children. But when taken after the onset of symptoms, there was no effect on the length or severity. In other words, mega-dosing on vitamin C after you feel your first sniffle doesn't seem to do much.

Although the general population may not benefit from taking vitamin C for a cold, there is some evidence that athletes training at a high level (marathon runners, for example), and those exposed to extreme cold temperatures can benefit from popping a few pills.

VITAMIN D

The sunshine vitamin plays a role in immune system function, and at least according to early evidence, it might help prevent respiratory tract infections. In fact, a study published in the February edition of the journal Archives of Internal Medicine found that individuals with a lower circulating level of vitamin D had a higher likelihood of developing a respiratory tract infection.

On the other hand, a more recent study, published in the October issue of Epidemiology and Infection, found that subjects given 2000 IU of vitamin D over a 12 week period were no less likely to develop a respiratory tract infection. Since there is very limited research on the role vitamin D plays in the development of the common cold, the bottom line is that we simply don't yet know enough to be able to do anything but speculate. But since so many Canadians are vitamin D deficient by late fall or early winter -- when cold and flu season hits full stride-- taking a vitamin D supplement probably isn't a bad idea, even if it is for other health benefits than cold prevention.

GARLIC

Long used by numerous cultures for taste and health, garlic's antimicrobial and antiviral properties have earned it a reputation as a classic cold-fighter. But does the evidence agree? According to a Cochrane review published earlier this year, researchers found that there is insufficient evidence to suggest that garlic should be used to either prevent or treat the common cold. Again, however, there haven't been enough well-controlled trials to allow a clear conclusion to be drawn, one way or the other.

In fact, in one double-blinded trial, subjects given garlic pills containing 180 mg of allicin (the active compound in garlic) for 12 weeks developed only a third as many colds as those given a placebo (dummy pill) for the same amount of time. Since double-blinded, placebo-controlled trials are considered the gold standard for proof, the results would certainly suggest that garlic has a role to play, at least in cold prevention, but more good trials are needed.

Yes, there are well-controlled trials, and some have demonstrated zinc lozenges can reduce the duration or the severity of the common cold, but others have shown no effect.
