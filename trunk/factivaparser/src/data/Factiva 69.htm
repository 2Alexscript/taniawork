Based on what we know right now, the swine flu scare doesn't pose a massive danger to your economic health, although it is likely to cause some pain.

Of course, we don't know nearly as much as we need to, so this reassurance must be taken with a few grains of salt.

At best, this new variant of flu will prove to be mild, not even requiring hospitalization for most patients. That's the case where it has already spread in small numbers, including Canada, the United States and Europe.

But in Mexico, where the outbreak started, there's been a rising death toll. As of yesterday, there was no good explanation for this difference, and it suggests some risk of an uglier outcome.

Still, based on the best information they have, analysts don't see a disastrous event.

Brian Bethune, chief financial economist at IHS Global Insight, a big Boston consulting firm, suggests a reasonable guess is that swine flu has the potential to slice U.S. economic output by less than half of one percentage point.

That's "kind of a mid-case scenario, where you do get some spreading, but there is a pro-active response" from authorities, Bethune said yesterday.

Of course, even if half a percentage point of lost growth isn't huge, it's significant when we're in the grip of a severe recession that only now appears to be easing its grip slightly.

For example, the IHS Global Insight forecast for the U.S. already predicts a 3.5-per-cent decline in economic activity this year, so another half-point lost would imply a slower or later recovery - maybe both.

The good news is that this added pain is nothing compared with a worst-case scenario the World Bank outlined last year for a truly deadly flu pandemic. Such an event could bring 70 million deaths around the world and a recession several times worse than the current one.

But there's little sign today of such a catastrophe.

First, despite the worrisome death toll in Mexico, experience with this new strain of flu suggests it's less virulent than avian flu.

It also seems to respond well to existing flu drugs, like Tamiflu, that diminish the severity of the illness, say analysts at Capital Economics, a British firm.

Better still, thanks to earlier avian-flu scares, most industrial nations, including Canada, already have stockpiles of such drugs.

Given this, maybe one hint of what Canada could experience this year would be the outbreak in 2003 of Severe Acute Respiratory Syndrome. The sometimes deadly viral disease afflicted southern China, neighbouring Asian countries and spread to the Toronto area, but was subdued within months.

The SARS outbreak briefly devastated travel to Canada, slicing 0.6 per cent from the country's annual growth rate in the second quarter of 2003. Two-thirds of the lost growth was recovered by the end of the year, however, a Bank of Canada analysis estimated.

A similar pattern could be seen this time unless swine flu turns out to be exceptionally deadly, said Philip Cross, head of economic analysis at Statistics Canada.

Otherwise, he said, it is "absolutely not" likely to cause deep, long-run damage to economic growth.

SARS did hit stock markets hard, but they bounced back smartly, notes Stéfane Marion, chief economist at the National Bank. After losing about 14 per cent from mid-January to mid-March 2003, the S&P 500 index of major U.S. stocks rebounded even more strongly, rising nearly 23 per cent by the outbreak's official end in July.

After the Severe Acute Respiratory Syndrome crisis of 2002-03, Cooper wrote a report titled Don't Fear Fear or Panic Panic: An Economist's View of Pandemic. "I think Canada is very well prepared. At this stage, there's no macroeconomic response required," she said in an interview yesterday.

Do not be panicked by swine flu. Be alert. Be informed. And be sure your public health officials -- federal, provincial and regional -- are up to the job. It's a big one.

As of late yesterday, the illness had killed 150 people in Mexico, the epicentre of the outbreak, and spread to enough other countries, including Canada and the United States, for the World Health Organization's emergency committee to raise the pandemic threat level for swine flu from phase three to four. This means there is now a sustained transmission of the flu from human to human.

While six cases of the influenza had been reported in Canada as of Monday, there were no new reports of people coming down with the disease during the day. Even so, Canada's chief public health officer David Butler-Jones said more cases -- and some deaths -- are expected in this country.

In an era of rapid global travel, where airports can become incubators for epidemics, the news of this virus is cause for concern. But people should know that, to a huge degree, they control their own fate. If they keep educating themselves and take sensible precautions, they can greatly limit their chances of contracting this new, infectious strain of swine flu.

Swine flu is a respiratory illness caused by an influenza virus that often triggers outbreaks in pigs. In this case, a strain of the virus is being transmitted from human to human.

The virus produces typical influenza symptoms -- high fever, muscle aches, vomiting, a cough and sore throat. Fortunately, the virus can be effectively treated by anti-viral drugs, including those Canadian health authorities have been stockpiling in case of a flu pandemic. But the treatment is most successful if it comes quickly, within 48 hours of exposure.

Of course, a gram of prevention beats a kilogram of cure. Individuals can and should take precautionary measures such as frequently washing their hands, covering their mouths and noses with tissue if they cough or sneeze, and staying home from work or school if they fall ill. It's not necessary to wear a surgical mask.

In addition to all this, people should avoid travelling to Mexico, the country hardest-hit by this swine flu outbreak. Late yesterday, the federal government signalled it would warn Canadians against non-essential trips to Mexico. The United States, the European Union, India and Malaysia had already issued travel advisories against non-vital travel to Mexico earlier in the day. Health officials in Waterloo Region are urging anyone who was in Mexico in the past week and feels sick to contact a doctor.

While individuals can do much to protect themselves, governments have a huge responsibility in confronting this public health emergency. Ontarians will hope that their provincial government has learned the lessons of the 2003 outbreak of SARS (severe acute respiratory syndrome) that killed 44 people in the province.

A commission into the SARS outbreak concluded that the first wave of infections starting in March 2003 was unavoidable but likely could have been controlled more quickly if systems of public health and disease surveillance had been in better shape. The commission also found that the devastating second phase of the SARS infections could have been caught sooner. So how much better is the province prepared for an epidemic?

Yesterday, Elizabeth Witmer, MPP for Kitchener-Waterloo and Conservative health critic, criticized the provincial government because 13 of 36 local health units still lack pandemic plans. While highlighted in the auditor general's 2007 report, this shortcoming has not been addressed. (Waterloo Region does have a plan in place.) Meanwhile, the New Democrats accused the province of being unable to communicate properly with the public, citing the government's performance in the Listeria outbreak last summer that killed 21 people nationwide, most in Ontario.

To be sure, the opposition politicians are trying to score political points. In the coming days, the federal, provincial and municipal governments can reassure the public and show they have the matter well under control by effectively communicating their strategies for containing and controlling swine flu in Canada.

What is swine flu?

Swine influenza is a respiratory disease of pigs caused by type A influenza viruses. Outbreaks of influenza are common in mammals and birds but usually remain contained within their host species. The swine flu that originated in Mexico – swine influenza A (H1N1) – is a genetic mixture of viruses seen in pigs, birds and people. It is being called swine flu because it is structured like a virus that usually infects pigs.

How did the flu go from pigs to humans?

The most likely explanation for the emergence of this new type of flu is humans living in close proximity with pigs. There are occasional reports of humans contracting swine flu but, in this case, the virus appears to have mutated to make it easier to spread in humans.

Can you get swine flu from eating pork?

No. This swine flu is not making pigs sick, it's making humans sick. And, by the way, you won't get the disease from eating fruits and vegetables from Mexico either. Influenza viruses are not food-borne.

I keep kosher – no pork or pork products – so I don't have to worry, right?

Again, this is a human flu; it really has nothing to do with pigs any more. It may be worth noting that Israel's deputy health minister said yesterday that the country would use the term Mexico flu rather than swine flu.

How is the swine flu spread?

Swine flu spreads in the same way as the seasonal flu – through the coughing and sneezing of people with influenza. Infected people can infect others one day before their own symptoms begin and up to a week after becoming sick.

What are the symptoms of swine flu?

Like the seasonal flu, swine flu symptoms can include sudden fever above 38C, dry cough, sore throat, headache and stuffy nose. Swine flu also appears to cause vomiting and diarrhea. In the fatal cases, the virus attacked the lungs, causing severe pneumonia.

Swine flu appears to be deadly in Mexicans yet relatively benign in the Canadians who have been infected. Why?

There is not yet a clear answer to that question but several possibilities. It could be that the virus is mutating and becoming weaker as it spreads. Multiple infections may be a factor: The virus may do more damage in people already fighting off other bacterial or viral invaders. It is also possible that the Mexican population does not have good resistance to this particular virus.

Should I get the flu vaccine?

The seasonal flu shot is unlikely to confer any protection directly against the swine flu. However, public health officials are recommending that, if you haven't had a flu shot, get one. Despite all the media attention on swine flu, the run-of-the-mill seasonal flu is still a much greater threat to your health: Annually, influenza kills about 4,000 Canadians and leads to the hospitalization of another 20,000. So far, six Canadians have been confirmed to have swine flu, and all of the cases have been mild.

Is there going to be vaccine to protect against swine flu?

Scientists in the United States and Canada have already isolated the swine flu virus. They are growing this strain in eggs, which is the first stage of vaccine production. But, using current manufacturing methods, it would take three to six months to produce a vaccine in large quantities.
