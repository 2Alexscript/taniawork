Step Closer

By Tessa McWatt

HarperCollins, $32.99

One of the main characters in Tessa McWatt's latest novel is a young scientist doing research on swine viruses. Given that Step Closer would have been completed long before the global outbreak of swine flu, one wonders if McWatt has a crystal ball.

In any case, there is a lot more than prescient scientific research going on in this sprawling and complex work, whose themes include identity, belonging, self-knowledge, guilt, atonement, borders, language, history and migration.

Indeed, with such a variety of weighty themes, some may wonder if the writer's reach exceeds her grasp, but I'm happy to report that, a few rough patches aside, McWatt tames this turbulent tale with an efficiency that the World Health Organization could only envy.

The heroine of Step Closer is Emily, a 30-something Canadian who lives in Spain with her boyfriend, Sam, the aforementioned virus researcher. The 2004 tsunami has just caused mass destruction in Southeast Asia; it has also galvanized Emily's desire to excavate her past and come to deeper self-knowledge. To do this, she begins to write a story about two men who played a major role in her life.

The first is Marcus, an enigmatic and beautiful young black man, who was Emily's platonic roommate and fellow waiter at a bar in Pamplona. The second is Gavin Lake, an Englishman who arrives in Pamplona in a disoriented state -- looking, we learn, to atone to Marcus for some wicked event.

Soon after they meet, Gavin and Marcus embark on a pilgrimage on the Santiago de Compostela trail, or the Way of St. James, as pilgrims have done for more than 1,000 years. For reasons of her own, Emily intercepts them en route, and lives are changed as a result.

In the end, Emily decides that writing about life is not the same as living it, leading to a glorious declaration of personal progress that involves bulls, Vaseline and public nudity. But it's not what you might think.

McWatt, whose 2001 novel Dragon's Cry was nominated for a Governor General's Award, is a talented writer who has set herself a monumental task in this wide-ranging novel, with its subplots, shifts between past and present, and complicated cast of characters. For the temporal shifts, McWatt has Emily relate the past as a diary entry in which she herself plays a role, a device that works for the most part, although occasionally it comes off as contrived.

As for the subplots, we learn that Sam, too, has a secret and terrible past, which accounts for his growing increasingly distracted and distant from Emily.

In addition to all this, there is the running of the bulls, animal-rights activists, an unusual American woman who plays a role in the pilgrimage, and detailed discussions of virology -- through Emily, who researches the field while translating Sam's work into English.

Despite McWatt's steady hand, though, there are some annoying aspects to this novel. For example, we discover that Marcus and Gavin both attended a British reform school years before, and that something monumental and horrible happened there. We know it was monumental and horrible because the author keeps telling us so, a heavy-handed narrative device that is so overused that the revelation of the actual event falls a little flat.

McWatt also is given to occasional fits of writerly preciousness -- such as ending sentences with portentous single words. Like. This. And though the plot is complicated, the novel could have been shorter.

Quibbles aside, McWatt writes with sensitivity and passion, and as a Guyanese who moved to Canada and then to Britain, she writes convincingly of the experience of the exotic other. This alone would make Step Closer a satisfying read.

Like many Canadians, I begin each morning by taking pills. The biggest is a multivitamin the size of my thumb. A Clydesdale would have trouble choking it down. The smallest pill, no bigger than a Tic-Tac, may be the most powerful. The tiny vitamin D tablet seems to be a secret weapon against everything from cancer to swine flu.

When I was a kid, we got our vitamin D by lining up in our classroom for daily spoonfuls of cod liver oil. Today, milk and other food products like milk are fortified with enough vitamin D to prevent rickets, a childhood bone ailment.

Our body naturally produces 90 per cent of vitamin D through sun exposure, but our climate and our extensive use of sunscreens mean we don't get enough sun to naturally produce enough vitamin D. It's almost impossible to get enough from diet alone. Almost all Canadians will be vitamin D deficient at some point in the year.

At Toronto's Mount Sinai Hospital, Dr. Reinhold Vieth is the Canadian authority on vitamin D. He points out that vitamin D deficiency is linked to everything from colds and flu, heart disease and diabetes to over 30 varieties of cancer.

The most interesting research links vitamin D deficiencies to the incidence of cancer. One reputable study showed a 60 per cent reduction in the incidence of cancer among people who had higher levels of vitamin D, as compared to those who were vitamin D deficient. There's similar evidence linking vitamin D deficiencies with colorectal cancer, prostate cancer and breast cancer.

The evidence on cancer prevention has satisfied even the watchful eyes of the Canadian Cancer Society. In 2007, they recommended that all Canadians take supplements of 1,000 International Units (IU) of vitamin D daily throughout the fall and winter. Those who are older, have dark skin or who remain indoors or covered during the summer months should take the vitamin year round.

Recent studies in the United States have shown that vitamin D may also be part of the treatment for certain cancers. In both prostate cancer and colorectal cancer, doctors are using differing doses of vitamin D as part of treatment protocol and getting some encouraging results.

Recurrent prostate cancer can be a difficult disease to treat. In one study, men with recurrent disease were given 2,000 IU of vitamin D daily. Their blood PSA levels were monitored. Increasing PSA levels can indicate progressing disease. Sixty per cent saw their PSA blood level decrease or remain unchanged. There are also developments with colorectal cancer and breast cancer, but there's much more work to be done on using vitamin D for active cancers.

As Canadians line up in the cold for our H1N1 shots, the Public Health Agency of Canada is taking a hard look at the connection between severe influenza and low vitamin D levels. They're also interested in the protective effects of vitamin D: "New research suggests that vitamin D induces the production of antimicrobial substances in the body that possess neutralizing activity against a variety of infectious agents including the influenza virus."

Vitamin D is fat-soluble and is not excreted, so there is always a concern about toxicity if too much is taken. However most vitamin D researchers believe the Canadian Cancer Society recommendations are very conservative. Dr. Vieth at Mount Sinai Hospital has seen little chance of problems at 2,000-4,000 IU a day, and even doses as high of 10,000 IU a day "pose no known risk of adverse effects in virtually all adults."

The take-away message for individual Canadians is to buy a bottle of vitamin D. What you're looking for are 1,000 IU tablets of vitamin D3 or cholecalciferol -- most manufacturers just label the jar as vitamin D. I paid about $7 for 90 tablets. That's less than eight cents a day to meet the Canadian Cancer Society recommendation -- a very inexpensive pill with some powerful prevention benefits.

If you're interested in learning more about your own vitamin D level and taking part in some science on the vitamin, check out D*action at www.grassrootshealth.net[http://www.grassrootshealth.net]. It's an international study to determine D levels and the impact of supplements. For a fee of $40, you can join the "open arm" of the study and find out your own vitamin D level.

With swine flu on the rise and winter on its way, workplaces all over the country are crossing their fingers that employees get the vaccine and updating their corporate plans in anticipation of greater employee absenteeism.

But while phone trees and emergency protocols are important, there's a lot that can be done on a daily basis to ward off bugs at work and maintain business as usual throughout the coming months.

Jan Chappell, a senior technical specialist with the Canadian Centre for Occupational Health and Safety, explains that regular employee hand-washing is crucial.

"People get wound up but really, it's just the normal, everyday stuff that you have to do," she says.

While Chappell isn't aware of any data suggesting a certain frequency of hand washing, doing it with some regularity -- and always after sneezing, eating or using the toilet -- should suffice. Regular soap and warm water, or gel sanitizers, will get the job done. Like many workplaces, her office has placed a number of dispensers on the walls for easy access.

Because the H1N1 virus travels about one to two metres every time an infected person sneezes or coughs, it's ideal to place workstations at least two metres apart. Ensuring the air in the office is properly ventilated is also important, says Chappell.

Just as absenteeism can cause serious productivity losses, so can presenteeism. Sick employees really do need to stay home -- especially those who are just starting to have symptoms. People are most contagious about eight hours before they come down with the flu, when they're just beginning to feel under the weather, she says. "There's toughing it out and then there's sharing the joy."

Peter Sheldon, a vice-president at Coverall Health-Based Cleaning Systems, believes that not all cleaning methods are equal. He says his company specializes in busting germs in commercial spaces. In his view, certain cleaning strategies can greatly reduce the spread of H1N1 at work.

"In today's world, things have changed. Twenty years ago, we didn't think about organisms coming into the workplace that could potentially kill us," says Sheldon. He says he's not interested in fearmongering but hopes to "educate people to take a defensive posture about potential threats."

Sheldon recommends companies talk to their janitorial service to ensure they're taking certain precautions, including using hospital-grade antibacterial/disinfectant cleaning products, microfibre cleaning cloths, and flat mops rather than conventional string mops.

Health-based cleaning is generally more efficient, says Sheldon, and shouldn't cost more than traditional cleaning strategies.

BACKGROUND

The H1N1 flu virus is a new strain of pandemic influenza that is different from the seasonal flu. People have no natural immunity to protect against this virus. The H1N1 flu virus emerged in April 2009 and surveillance of its spread shows that it is affecting more young and healthy people than the regular seasonal flu, which normally affects seniors and young children. People with underlying medical conditions, and pregnant women, may be at a greater risk for severe illness.

What is it?

The H1N1 flu virus -- also known as human swine influenza -- is a respiratory illness that affects the nose, throat and lungs. This virus usually affects pigs, but has been transferred to humans.

The H1N1 flu virus has caused primarily mild illness in Canada, but Canadians need to be prepared to respond to a potentially more severe form of the virus that may emerge this fall.

How is it spread?

The H1N1 flu virus is contagious and is spread the same way as regular seasonal influenza. This happens when an infected person coughs or sneezes and their germs enter the nose, eyes, or throat of another person. The germs can also rest on hard surfaces like counters and doorknobs, and can be picked up on hands and transmitted to the respiratory system when someone touches his mouth and/or nose. It is not possible to catch it by eating pork or pork products or through blood transfusions.

More research is being done on how long a person can be infectious (be able to spread the virus to others), but it is believed that this period is for one day before the onset of symptoms and continues for approximately seven days after symptoms have started. Influenza vaccines (also called flu shots) help you to prevent getting sick by introducing your body to a weakened or dead version of the virus to teach your body to build immunity to it.

There have indeed been real ethical lapses. The queue-jumpers themselves are in many cases embarrassed, but they didn't do it on their own. It's even more insulting when queue-jumpers and their doctors try to rationalize their choices. For example, the boarding schools say their students' close quarters make infections likely to spread.
