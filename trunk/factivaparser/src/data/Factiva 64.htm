How exactly did Ed Stelmach cause the swine flu outbreak? He didn't, of course, but to many in the oilpatch, Alberta's premier is the primary cause of everything wrong in the world. This is unfair because the collapse in energy prices and capital markets is global.

But it's not unfair to observe the huge void between the captains of Alberta's most important industry and the Tory party since Stelmach became leader. Who speaks for the oilpatch in the legislature? What MLA is standing up and demanding help for the tens of thousands of unemployed oil workers and the economic carnage many smaller companies are experiencing? No MLA is even asking, let alone answering. This conspicuous silence is of enormous benefit to the Wildrose Alliance Party.

The Conservatives started this mess with the 2007 royalty review followed by the New Royalty Framework. Lease agreements upon which hundreds of billions of investment dollars were based were abrogated without compensation, destroying Alberta's credibility and reputation. Damage done, the royalty reduction and drilling credit Band-Aids that followed made the "simplified" royalty system excruciatingly complex. There is an undeniable anti-Calgary bias among senior Tories from northern Alberta.

The Liberals under new leader David Swann have been busy making friendly with the oil industry. But forgotten is the pressure predecessor Kevin Taft put on Ralph Klein's government to collect more royalties when oil prices were rising. The party is clever enough to detect and hopefully exploit oilpatch alienation. But Swann's mission is to make Alberta greener. How he will make this politically attractive to the industry is unclear.

When NDP Leader Brian Mason does say anything about the oil industry, it's usually about how Albertans are getting screwed compared to Norway or Alaska. Mason and the NDP inhabit a socialist utopia where everyone deserves a government-funded education and free health. But the party remains a fringe movement because it's unable to explain how to have wealth to redistribute without industrial activity.

And Wildrose Alliance? Party Leader Paul Hinman lost the party's only seat in last year's election, so it has no voice in the legislature. Regardless, it's Wildrose Alliance that is getting the oil industry's attention. An event at Calgary's Petroleum Club last month attracted more than 200 executives, and managers then raised thousands when hard hats were passed around the room. Combined with Hinman's announcement that he will step down at the June convention to allow the best possible leader to step forward, pundits have writ-ten more about Wildrose Alliance in the past month than the past year.

Supporting a political party with no elected members, some strange policies and an organization still under construction is hardly instant gratification. But for 18 months industry leaders have been trying to persuade the Tories to change direction on energy policy. Traditional lobbying has been futile. Meetings with ministers and backbenchers about how the government is mismanaging the oil and gas business all end the same way. Tories listen politely. Some even acknowledge there's a problem, but nobody publicly dares challenge big Ed.

So out of necessity, Alberta's oil industry is taking a hard look at a different approach--a new political party. To his credit, Hinman was the only MLA who declared Stelmach's royalty review a mistake from the day it was announced.

Alberta's budget deficit is so enormous that future royalty reductions could be impossible. Therefore, more oil people are concluding that none of the three parties in the legislature offer solutions. Wildrose Alliance is the only answer.

Across Canada every major industry--automotive, agriculture, forestry, fishery, manufacturing--has at least one vocal advocate in government. During tough times somebody says something. Absolute indifference is forcing the battered oil industry to create a completely new voice.

David yager is a Calgary oil service executive. His column appears the first Sunday of each month

Here are some questions and answers about the science of swine flu -- the H1N1 virus that's sweeping the world:

Q: What exactly is a virus?

A: It's a tiny packet of only eight genes wrapped in a cloak of proteins, much smaller than a bacterium. Unlike bacteria, a virus is only half alive. It can't eat or reproduce on its own, but must take over the genetic machinery of a living cell. Most viruses are harmless; some are useful, but others, such as the flu virus, can be deadly.

Q: What makes this swine flu virus special?

A: It's a novel combination of bird, pig and human viral genes never before found in North America or elsewhere, so people have no immunity to it. It's a descendant of the H1N1 virus that killed tens of millions of people worldwide in the pandemic of 1918-1919, mixed in with recent strains of swine and bird flu viruses. The 1918 virus originated in birds and then jumped to humans. This year's virus apparently jumped from a pig to a five-year-old boy in Veracruz, Mexico, who passed it on to other humans.

Q: What does H1N1 stand for?

A: It's the initials of two sugar proteins (their scientific names are hemagglutinin and neuramidinase) that sit on the surface of the virus and do its dirty work. There are 16 types of the Rating 1protein, numbered H1 through H16, and 9 types of the- protein, numbered N1 through N9. That makes 144 possible combinations of the virus, a constantly changing challenge for prevention or treatment. A new combination, H2N2, caused a brief swine flu epidemic in 1957. An H3N2 strain was the source of another epidemic in 1968. The bird flu virus that began in Southeast Asia a decade ago and has spread throughout the Old World is an H5N1 combination.

Q: How does the Rating 1protein work?

A: The Rating 1protein looks like a little spike that fits into a notch, called a receptor, on the outside of an animal or human cell and lets the virus enter. Once inside, the virus hijacks the DNA in the cell's nucleus and uses it to make copies of itself.

Q: What does the- protein do?

A: After infection, it opens a passage in the cell wall and releases the new baby viruses, which can now invade other cells. Without the- protein, infection would be limited to the first cell, rarely enough to cause disease.

Q: How do medicines such as Tamiflu and Relenza work?

A: They block the action of the- protein so the virus can't spread. They're not vaccines to prevent an infection, but drugs to limit its impact. They should be taken as soon as possible since the virus reproduces most rapidly between 24 and 72 hours after illness begins.

Q: How does a new virus develop?

A: When the genes that govern the Rating 1and- proteins reproduce, random changes -- mutations -- can occur in their DNA. The changes gradually accumulate, ultimately producing a virus that may be more lethal or may penetrate a target cell more easily. Another possibility is gene-swapping. This can happen when a cell is infected by viruses from different creatures, say a chicken and a pig. The cell becomes a "mixing bowl," whipping up a new virus containing some chicken genes and some pig genes. In the new strain of H1N1 virus, pieces of human, bird and pig genes are all scrambled up.

Q: How does this H1N1 virus differ from the H1N1 that caused the Spanish flu pandemic in 1918-1919?

A: That virus developed various changes over the years, so it's similar but not identical to its ancestor -- like a grandson who resembles but also differs from his grandfather. So far, H1N1 is not as virulent as the previous strain, but that could change. The earlier pandemic began mildly in 1918, but returned in a devastating second wave six months later. Experts fear that could happen again. Hence they are rushing to develop a vaccine by this fall.

Q: How does H1N1 virus jump from animals to humans?

A: Usually the Rating 1protein on a pig or bird virus doesn't fit easily into the receptor of a human cell. So a person exposed to such a virus is unlikely to get infected. However, random changes may occur in the genes that control the shape of the Rating 1protein and allow the virus to pass through the cell wall. This gives rise to a new strain of H1N1 that's adapted to humans. Now the virus can pass from person to person, as is happening now. Contact with infected pigs or birds no longer is necessary.

Q: How does one person catch H1N1 from others?

A: The virus can be transmitted through the air -- by a cough or a sneeze -- or by a handshake or by touching an infected surface, such as a doorknob. The virus can live for up to two hours outside a cell. It can't be passed by eating pork.

Q: Why does this disease seem to be more deadly in Mexico than in the U.S. or other countries?

A: That may be an illusion. The first U.S. death has now occurred in Texas and more fatalities are expected. It's possible that many cases of mild disease in Mexico went undetected, making the mortality rate appear to be higher there than it does here. It's also possible that the virus strain in the U.S. differs slightly from the one in Mexico, making it less virulent.

Q: Why does H1N1 seem to attack healthy young adults more than sick and elderly people, who are most affected by the ordinary seasonal flu?

A: Young adults have a healthy immune system that launches a massive counterattack of antibodies against the flu virus. Unfortunately, the counter-attack can cause an overwhelming inflammation that damages other organs, such as the lungs. Elderly people with weaker immune systems are less likely to suffer from such harmful inflammation. Older people who were exposed to earlier flu epidemics might also have some residual immunity in their systems.

To do this they must dedicate themselves to scrupulously transparent reporting that is properly weighted with honest perspective and context. This is no easy task considering today's financial pressures on the media, smaller news holes and slots, and the traditional demands to report news on the fly.

Editors will need to raise standards of accuracy, build better fact checking systems and find ways to convince the public they do not sensationalize to improve revenues. In fact, there isn't much blatant sensationalism in the best of the mainstream media, but the public believes there is. There is, however, unintentional sensationalism created by haste and thoughtless mental slips.

An example: One morning this week the prestigious ABC-TV news operation advertised that it would have an evening report on how the Swine Flu is "criss-crossing" the United States. That morning there were fewer than 60 cases in the U.S., all in small clusters, so the thoughtless reference to "criss-crossing" only added to the potential for hysteria among millions of viewers.

News promos, shouting headlines, newscast voices raised a notch to convey urgency, photos and video selected more for impact than for information: All are drops that can become a bucketful of hysteria.

No one is immune to the high anxiety created by the reporting of climbing Swine Flu numbers. During the past week, many of us have felt our chests tighten at the thought of a killer flu running amok among our families and friends. When I feel like that, here's the advice I give myself, advice based on my experience researching and writing about flu:

* Prepare yourself with factual information collected from sources you trust.

* Put what you learn in context: for instance, hundreds of thousands die globally each year of everyday flu.

* Follow the simple hygiene advice of health officials: Thoroughly washing hands, avoiding unnecessary contact with others, following sneezing and coughing protocols, and keeping respiratory mucous tracts moist to protect them. Dry air compromises the body's defence systems.

* Don't put social graces ahead of infection control. Don't hug, kiss, shake hands or mingle close when you suspect flu or colds are about.

* Be thoughtful when you communicate with others about the flu crisis. This is serious stuff and many people are hypersensitive to it. Toss-off talk done without thought creates anxiety.

* Remember our strengths in any modern flu crisis: Powerful antiviral drugs and much better disease surveillance. Also, remember the tens of thousands of dedicated health-care workers out there working hard for you.

Some years back I was treated for a life-threatening condition by a brilliant, well grounded doctor who worked hard to have me understand the reality of my situation while keeping it in perspective.

"Worry a little," he used to tell me. "But when you have all the best information, there is no need to worry a lot."

The World Health Organization has said a flu pandemic is overdue and is the most serious known health threat facing our world. That should be enough to make us all worry a little.

But if we prepare well and stay well informed, we won't let a little constructive worry become destructive panic.

Is this a new strain?

Yes, and therefore, people have not built up a natural immunity to it. It's highly unusual in its composition: one part North American swine flu virus, one part North American avian influenza, one part human influenza and one part swine flu virus commonly found in Asia and Europe.

What are the symptoms of swine flu?

Generally, the symptoms are similar to regular human flu: a fever, cough, sore throat, body aches, headaches, chills and fatigue. But swine flu can also cause diarrhea and vomiting. In extreme cases, the U.S. Centres for Disease Control warn, swine flu can lead to pneumonia, respiratory failure and death.

How deadly is this strain?

The short answer is: Nobody really knows.

"But the fact that there are very large clusters of unusual respiratory deaths in Mexico in young people is cause for concern," said Brian Ward, a microbiologist and infectious-diseases physician at the McGill University Health Centre.

In a typical flu season, one in 1,000 infected people will die from the disease. That's a kill rate of one-tenth of one per cent. In Canada, 10 to 15 per cent of the population falls sick each year with the flu, or at least 3.3 million people. Of that number, 2,000 to 4,000 mostly elderly and frail Canadians die from it.

In a pandemic, the flu mortality rate is at least a half per cent. In Canada, that would translate into about 16,500 deaths.

During the 1918-19 Spanish flu epidemic - considered the worst of all time - about 40 million people worldwide died. No one knows for certain what the mortality rate was, but many experts place it at between one and two per cent.

In Canada, a flu mortality rate of two per cent would translate into at least 66,000 deaths - about 30 times worse than the average flu season.

In Mexico, where the swine flu originated, the World Health Organization has definitively linked the virus to seven deaths. But there are also reports that more than 150 Mexicans have died from flu-like respiratory infections.

Although the data on deaths are accurate, no one knows how many Mexicans have been infected with swine flu. Initial news reports of more than 2,400 people infected are probably a huge under-estimate, experts say.

Thus, it would be a big mistake to divide 150 deaths by 2,400 infections and arrive at a mortality rate of six per cent.

What have troubled WHO officials, however, are two dramatic developments: the newness of the virus and the fact that many young, otherwise healthy individuals have died.

However, Miller cautioned that there might be complicating factors in the Mexican deaths: pre-existing lung illness in some individuals, malnourishment, slum-like conditions and a lack of health resources.

Yes, they would develop antibodies against the disease.

The 1918-19 pandemic occurred in three waves, and the second wave, five months after the first, was by far the deadliest.

Should pregnant women be vaccinated?

Pregnant women are particularly susceptible to the flu, and would be given priority for vaccination in the event of a pandemic, Ward said.

There are periods when the world of travel seems to be tossed into turmoil as worldwide events beyond our control spin into a vortex of media facts and fears.

These past couple of weeks have seen concerns about protests, health and bankruptcy taking over the travel agenda. Hopefully we can create some balance and perspective with today's column. The first question came from the travel-agency community.

QUESTION: Can you please explain that it was not a travel agency but a tour operator that went bankrupt with the closure of Conquest Vacations in Toronto a couple of weeks ago?

ANSWER: The travel-agency community was deservedly upset when a headline, as the company was going bankrupt, suggested that Conquest was a travel agency. The story was accurate, but the headline did create a perception that travel agencies could play a part in the demise of this, or any other tour operator. Phil Houde, Manitoba chair for the Association of Canadian Travel Agents (ACTA) wrote a letter to the editor of the Free Press clarifying the misconception, but many travellers still don't recognize the distinction.

"ACTA travel agencies are a big part of the solution, and not any part of the problem, when situations like this occur," Houde says. In past travel-industry bankruptcies, such as Canada 3000 and Jetsgo, it was the travellers who booked through travel agents who got the best results. Their agents worked to help those affected, despite knowing they would never see compensation for their extra efforts.

By and large, tour operators use travel agencies as their primary distribution system for the packaged vacations they offer. With a suffering economy, particularly in Eastern Canada, there was suddenly an overcapacity in the market. Prices were so low that it seemed obvious no one could be making a profit. But there was no sense that travel agencies could predict which, if any, tour operator would go down.

There is not a travel agent in Manitoba, knowing how much extra work that would come back to them later, who would ever book vacation packages for their clients with a supplier they thought might not survive.

Still, when the worst does occur, Manitobans are placed in a dilemma. Ontario has an excellent passenger-protection plan. Manitoba does not. Instead, the province counts on the fact that most travellers will be reimbursed by their credit-card companies on the basis of non-delivery of service. But corporate and school groups, who usually pay by cheque are completely out of luck under this system. And those Conquest clients who ended up having to pay a second time to get out of their hotels in Mexico, will go through significant frustration before their situation is finally resolved.

While the Ontario body, through ACTA, has offered to help Manitoba set up a similar protection program, this offer so far has not been taken up by the government. Perhaps now is the time.

QUESTION: I was considering booking my wedding in Mexico for next winter. What is your advice?

ANSWER: Details of this swine flu health scare are evolving so quickly that by the time you read this it may have already changed dramatically.

A number of people have already started to switch their weddings from Mexico to the Dominican Republic, Jamaica, and Cuba. I believe it is too soon for such a reaction to the current situation. Once the world health bodies started to issue travel advisories against non-essential travel to that exceptional sun destination, there was an immediate impact on short-term travel to Mexico. And even though we don't know when the advisory will be lifted, tour operators were right to cancel flights until June 1 at least.

In many ways, it already is an international problem and no longer just a Mexican problem, even though that is where the first reported cases of swine flu originated. Many are comparing the current situation to Toronto's SARS epidemic, which, while solved in a reasonably quick time from a health point of view, crippled Toronto's tourism economy for years.

My advice is to wait a few weeks. There is still plenty of time to make a destination decision. This situation could be resolved sooner than we expect. Mexico is by far the top non-stop destination for tour companies who offer travel from Manitoba during the winter season. The reality is that if Mexico is not be in the equation for next season, pricing for all other destinations will go through the roof.
