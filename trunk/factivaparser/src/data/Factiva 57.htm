"Globally we believe that we are at Phase 5 but are getting closer to Phase 6," said Keiji Fukuda, World Health Organization assistant director-general, referring to the agency's six-level pandemic alert system.

Phase 5 signals that a pandemic is imminent, while the world would be in a full-fledged pandemic - marking global spread - at Phase 6.

"It is clear that the virus continues to spread internationally," he said.

"We know there are a number of countries that appear to be in transition moving from travel-related cases to established, more established, community-type spread."

A criteria for the WHO to move the alert to Phase 6 would be established community spread in a country outside the first region in which the disease was initially reported - in this case, outside the Americas.

"We still are waiting for really widespread community activity in these countries, so I think it's fair to say they are in transition and are not quite there yet," Fukuda said.

But, he stressed, countries like Britain, Spain, Japan, Chile and Australia are showing larger numbers of H1N1 influenza infections, "with some early spread into communities."

Other than geographical spread, member states have asked WHO to integrate an assessment of the disease's severity into its criteria for moving up the alert scale and declaring a pandemic.

Almost 19,000 cases of infections including 117 deaths have been reported to the WHO by 64 countries around the world since the virus emerged in the United States and Mexico in April.

On Monday, Canadian health authorities reported 194 new cases of the H1N1 virus, including four in the far north, bringing the total number of people with the virus in this country to 1,530, including 57 who are hospitalized.

TORONTO

Chalk up another species for the wily flu virus.

Humans, horses, dogs, whales, seals, birds, cats, ferrets and even raccoons are known to be susceptible to the tiny eight-gene viruses. Researchers in Tennessee have now added giant anteaters to that list.

"Who would have thunk it?'' senior author Dr. Melissa Kennedy said with a laugh. "We were pretty amazed.''

The February 2007 outbreak, involving 11 adult giant anteaters at Tennessee's Nashville zoo, is reported in an article that will be published in the July issue of the journal Emerging Infectious Diseases. All the anteaters survived.

"Mainly it's an indication of how much we don't know, and the promiscuity of flu in terms of being able to change hosts and infect and adapt to new situations,'' says Dr. Jeffrey Hall, who last November added raccoons to the lengthening list of flu's hosts.

According to the article, the animals suffered a familiar litany of symptoms -- "severe'' nasal discharge, congestion, loss of appetite and lethargy.

Kennedy's team applied bits of the nasal secretions to a variety of different cell cultures, hoping something would grow.

To their surprise, two of three samples produced influenza A viruses of the H1N1 subtype -- the human type, not the new swine flu virus.

Sequencing of the genetic blueprints of four of the eight genes of the viruses showed they were virtually identical to the human H1N1 viruses circulating in Tennessee at the time.

Because it had been an unusually cold winter, the animals had been housed indoors when the outbreak happened.

They had no contact with other animals, and limited contact with people.

Though the theory hasn't been proven, the investigation into the source of the sickness points to their main caregiver, who was working with an undiagnosed respiratory illness.

It's not clear whether all the animals caught the flu from the zoo worker, or if some of the spread was anteater to anteater.

On the surface of it, it seems unlikely the anteater findings would have any implications for human health.

But Hall and others wonder if undiscovered pools of influenza viruses among species less studied than birds and pigs -- assumed to be the major players in the evolution of new flu viruses -- might contribute to the rise of new genetic variations that could work their way into humans.

"Is there an anteater flu out there that we don't know about?''

When news of a possible swine flu pandemic began to trickle out of Mexico in April, landlords and property managers across Canada congratulated themselves on their foresight.

Canada's major office buildings and retail and industrial malls are likely the best-prepared commercial structures in the world to face a global pandemic, says the Building Owners and Managers Association of Canada. It is all thanks to steps taken three years ago when BOMA Toronto kick-started a process that resulted in a comprehensive “pandemic guide for building owners and managers.”

Published in April, 2007, it sets out what building owners and property managers should do to keep their buildings operating and avoid legal liability if a pandemic strikes. Included was a 50-page workbook that is, in essence, a template for steps to be followed.

The workbook covers everything from communicating preparedness plans to tenants, to ensuring staff do not spread disease, to how to maintain key staff roles and systems as more and more people fall ill.

“To the best of our knowledge, Canada has the only pandemic preparedness plan for commercial properties in the world,” says Randall Rothbart, a BOMA Toronto director and a partner in law firm Solomon Rothbart Goodman LLP, whose contribution to the plan was writing the legal implications.

“Our rationale was that, if a pandemic strikes, then we must be ready to contain its spread while maintaining business operations within our members' buildings,” he says. “When it looked like H1N1 might turn into a pandemic, we were ready.”

Whether landlords could face lawsuits if they failed to do their best to help contain a pandemic or if tenants faced business losses because entire buildings had to be shut down is a grey area, he adds.

“However, now that there are guidelines in place, there may be an argument that landlords who fail to follow them may be liable for damages,” he says.

This enlightened approach to the dangers posed by pandemics was the brainchild of Cheryl Gray, senior vice-president of real estate services at Bentall LP in Toronto. Bentall manages 77 million square feet of commercial space across Canada and in the state of Washington, including 200 King St. W. in Toronto and the Bentall Centre in Vancouver.

“The idea came to me in 2003, shortly after the SARS epidemic struck Toronto,” she says. “I could easily see the impact other diseases could have on building operations right across Canada if a real pandemic struck.”

In early 2006, Ms. Gray began talking with other property managers such as Redcliff Realty Group, GWL Realty Advisors, Brookfield Properties Corp. and Oxford Properties Group Inc. Together, they agreed to strike a committee under the umbrella of BOMA to create a comprehensive pandemic preparedness plan.

“We took as a template work done by the Canadian Manufacturers & Exporters association,” she says. “It was not to be a business continuity plan but one to ensure availability of labour and service at our buildings. There was lots of angst about pandemics then and an almost impending sense of doom.”

The final plan was not a document destined to sit on a bookshelf and be dusted off should the need arise. Almost immediately after its release, major landlords such as Bentall and Redcliff began taking steps to prepare for what they saw as the eventuality of a pandemic.

“We started about 18 months ago by doing three key things,” says Jonathan Fleischer, executive vice-president at Redcliff, which manages 26 million square feet of commercial, retail and industrial space, including major office towers such as 320 Bay St., 1 Yonge St., 65 Queen St. W. and 40 University Ave. in Toronto.

“The first thing was to send a letter to all tenants to inform them what Redcliff was doing to prepare for a pandemic and suggesting things they might do as well,” Mr. Fleischer says.

The second was to install hand-sanitizing stations in all lobbies, outside all washrooms and in service areas used by subcontractors, such as cleaners, security staff and delivery people. The third was to install posters in key areas advising of the threat of communicable diseases and the need to wash hands regularly as an effective preventive measure.

Redcliff, like other major property managers, also issued key staff with emergency kits that included rubber gloves, face masks and hand sanitizers. It even went so far as to stock buildings with emergency food supplies with a shelf life of up to five years.

Property managers then turned to ensuring there would be enough trained staff to maintain buildings. “That includes cross-training our own staff to ensure there are enough people available to maintain building systems, and reaching out to our service providers to ensure they have preparedness plans,” Mr. Fleischer says.

“We made use of all the lessons learned from events like 9/11, Y2K and the great power blackout earlier in the decade.”

When news of a possible swine flu pandemic began to appear, most major landlords activated the plan's first steps toward meeting the challenge, Ms. Gray says. At Bentall, that meant forming a five-person crisis management committee, which met daily by conference call.

“We identified staff who had recently been to Mexico and reminded them of the dangers and warning signs, and then regularly informed tenants and our building owners of both the progress of the disease and the measures we were taking should it begin to affect our operations,” she adds.

The wisdom of being prepared aside, most property managers understand a severe pandemic may still wreak havoc on operations.

“We have told tenants that, in case of a pandemic, we will try to maintain operations but there may indeed be some disruptions,” Mr. Fleischer says. “They should also make their own plans. We will not, for example, provide screening of visitors to our buildings. We will restrict deliveries to tenants and, in case of a severe event, we may have to close a building down.”

BOMA has tried to think of everything that landlords and owners can do to contain a pandemic and maintain operations, Mr. Rothbart says, but acknowledges that a pandemic could still throw a curve ball.

“A pandemic would be a moving target,” Mr. Fleischer says. “We can't say for sure how we will respond until an event occurs.”

It was Nov. 11, 1918, Armistice Day, and Windsorites were dancing in the streets to celebrate the official end of The Great War.

Four years of bloodshed and global gloom were lifting, but inside Hotel-Dieu Hospital were dozens of people suffering from a terrible affliction that was bumped from the front pages by the conflict in Europe that would later become known as the First World War.

The Spanish flu had its grip on Windsor.

Many of those suffering should have by rights been celebrating in the streets with their friends. Many with influenza were young, vibrant and healthy, not the stereotypical very young or very old who typically suffer worst from the condition.

"The noise of the celebration was somewhat hard on the patients in the city," read the account in The Border Cities Star.

"Nurses and physicians resorted to put batting in the ears of the sick to prevent as much as possible the spirit of excitement affecting them."

The paper that day trumpeted the fact that only seven new cases had been reported that morning.

Over the previous two days, 35 new cases had been confirmed.

On perhaps the worst day of the outbreak, Oct. 26, a total of 93 new cases were reported.

Windsor had been waiting for the flu to make its way to extreme Southwestern Ontario, and leading up to its arrival The Border Cities Star carried stories about cases in Quebec, then eastern Ontario, and on Oct. 6 the paper reported that an ear, nose and throat doctor had quickly succumbed to the disease in Brantford.

On Oct. 12 the paper identified the city's first victim of Spanish influenza, Miss Irene Graham, 19, a store clerk who had moved to the city from neighbouring Sandwich two days prior.

"The Dreaded Spanish influenza, and his equally hated brother, pneumonia, claimed a Windsor victim Friday afternoon," the paper announced.

Over the coming weeks hundreds of people would come down with Spanish flu in Windsor, and more than 100 would die.

The victims were named, and ranged from the average shop worker to a 40-year-old priest and head of the philosophy department at Assumption College. Even the mayor of the day, Charles R. Tuson, came down with the flu and had to take several days off during the height of the crisis.

Widely believed to have been transported to North America by soldiers returning home from the war, The Star tried to prepare people for the arrival of the "sneezing disease."

"Spanish influenza, that dreaded sneezing disease that sneaked into this continent from Europe and, as the censor would have us say, "landed at an Atlantic port", is getting nearer every day to the Border Cities, and has now broken out at the United States naval station at adjoining the Ford Eagle plant, which has been quarantined," The Star reported on Sept. 23, 1918.

The flu is generally believed to have begun in China in early 1918 and made its way to western Europe by late spring, where it raced through the battlefields where soldiers spent their days and nights in damp, rat-infested trenches.

The flu came to North America on ships bringing back wounded soldiers, and it spread across the continent as the men travelled home on the railways of the U.S. and Canada.

There was even speculation in some quarters that the flu was in fact the result of a biological weapon developed by the German kaiser.

Windsor's medical officer of health, Dr. G.R. Cruickshank, did his best to prepare the public for the coming onset of the flu.

"Any one who goes into a crowded street car and coughs is worse than the German kaiser; any child that goes to school with a cold is worse than any German atrocity," Cruickshank told the Border Cities Chamber of Commerce days before the flu arrived.

"Every time you have a new influenza virus there is always the fear of a mutation into something more scary," said El-Masri.

"We're not out of the woods yet. The public need not panic but to do the right thing. Be careful, be vigilant, but don't overreact."

There's trouble brewing in the Tory heartland as an obscure law governing the funding of agricultural commissions has put rural Conservative MLAs in a quandary and prompted traditional party supporters to question the government.

Affected agriculture groups fear the Marketing of Agricultural Products Amendment Act threatens their survival. They say it will divide farmers and ranchers and hinder the work they do to market and research beef, hogs, lamb and potatoes.

The bill, which will enable farmers to demand the return of the mandatory fees that fund the commissions that work on their behalf, would take effect in the 2010-11 fiscal year.

Critics say it transfers power from democratic agriculture organizations to big producers.

"It amazes me that they want to do this," said Alberta Beef Producers chairman Rick Burton. "I am disappointed as a cattle producer, as a taxpayer, and as a long-time supporter of Conservative politics in Alberta."

Burton said the government is punishing his organization for opposing aspects of its Alberta Livestock and Meat Strategy which require farmers to verify the age of their cattle.

"If the government can do this to this association, where is the freedom of speech? And what does this say about the way government fulfils its mandate?

"That's why it should concern the taxpayer in the city. It's me today, but it's him next."

Conservative MLA Doug Griffiths, who introduced the bill, said it doesn't have anything to do with payback. The government is simply stepping in to resolve a dispute the beef producers have not been able to resolve on their own, he said.

A previous plebiscite 15 years ago ended up with a 51-49 split in favour of the status quo, he said.

Griffiths, parliamentary secretary to Agriculture Minister George Groeneveld, said larger producers have questioned why they have the same voting power as smaller producers when they have much more at stake.

"Maybe the focus should be on the people who are actually driving the industry, not just on people with one cow."

Griffiths said nine other Alberta commissions have refundable fees and they manage to keep revenues to do the job.

But Rod Scarlett, executive director of Wild Rose Agricultural Producers, said that in the cattle and hog industry, large producers who contribute a lot to the commissions could dramatically affect commission budgets by exercising their new right to recoup their money.

"They could basically hold these commissions to ransom," he said.

Farmer Ernie Israelson, 63, said feedlots that sell 100,000 head of cattle annually stand to recoup $300,000 or channel it to other organizations that will promote their causes.

"Suddenly, the dollars are voting instead of producers," Israelson said.

He fears the Alberta beef producers and the Canadian Cattle Commission could lose 30 to 40 per cent of their revenue if some of the big feedlots ask for the return of the service fees.

That's money that goes to promote the product and help the industry get through crises, he said.

Groeneveld told the legislature it's not up to government to tell business owners what business association they must support with their own money. He rejected the idea of letting the organizations decide the matter themselves.

"No matter the outcome, everyone loses in a plebiscite because it will only further divide a group that's already faced with the difficulty of trying to work together," he said.

Driving a wedge

The bill is driving a wedge through the heart of the agricultural community that has staunchly supported the Conservatives for decades. Many farmers can't understand why the government is pushing a bill they believe can only hurt their industries.

"If our commission goes under, there's nobody to help the farmers out," said lamb producer Larry Fesyk. "Everybody is on their own. It's a free-for-all."

Some rural Conservative MLAs have serious concerns about the effect of the bill on the organizations in their ridings.

Little Bow MLA Barry McFarland, one of several government MLAs to express concerns about the bill in the legislature, said he doesn't know what to tell his constituents when they ask him how he will vote. He worries the bill could hurt his riding's potato industry.

"I know that it won't curry a lot of favour, but as a representative for a constituency that probably has, along with my colleague south of us, in excess of 80 per cent of the potatoes grown in Alberta, I find it very difficult to support the bill as it is," he told the legislature.

Edzo Kok, executive director of the Potato Growers of Alberta, said his 120 members are pushing the government to reconsider.

"We tell them it's a beef issue. We don't want to be collateral damage here."

Alberta Pork executive director Paul Hodgman, whose industry has been hit by concerns over swine flu, said the bill shocked his 450 members.

"It's going to cause some big pressures on this organization on how it wrestles with cash flow. It will be difficult to plan and do things for the long term."

The NDP said the bill overrides the rights of the producer organizations.

"The government will, by regulation, give itself the authority in cabinet to overrule the democratic decisions of these producer associations," NDP MLA Rachel Notley said.

Liberal Kevin Taft said the issue pits a handful of powerful beef producers against the majority of small producers. He has suggested in the legislature there's a link between the bill and the big feedlot operators who supported Premier Ed Stelmach's leadership bid.

"They are big players and they stand to gain significantly from this," he said.

But Stelmach has denied a connection.

"This has got nothing to do with who contributed either to my campaign or to individual campaigns of any member of this house," he said.

Beef Industry Alliance chairman Russ Pickett, whose coalition of beef producers lobbied for the bill, said its members are a cross-section of farmers, ranchers and feedlot operators who haven't been happy with their industry representation.

"It should be run more like a business where shares are involved," he said. "Really, for us, it comes down to choice and accountability."

While there may be animosity over the bill for a while, in the long-term it will strengthen the industry, he said.

First and foremost, employers need to play a key role in protecting their employee's health and safety as well as their business interests by having a business continuity strategy. To get started on their planning, employers need to begin by classifying the risk of exposure for employees and minimizing risk as much as possible. Next, be sure that you are aware of all federal and provincial health department guidelines and plans. Review your sick leave policies to ensure they do not penalize sick employees.
