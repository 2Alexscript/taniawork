If, as some researchers suspect, the H1N1 virus is poised for a new wave of infection this fall, it's imperative that governments get the lines of communication as open as they can be.

People are going to have questions. If those questions go unanswered, there will be many unnecessary visits to clinics and doctors' offices, and a lot of unnecessary stress. Rumours and misinformation will rush to fill even the smallest gap in public education.

The fact that information now knows no borders might, in a way, add to the confusion, since public-health recommendations in the United States and Europe can differ from the recommendations in Canada. If, for example, Canadians aren't clear on when it's appropriate to use antivirals, there could be overuse or misuse, which might lead the virus to become drug-resistant.

Each country is also formulating priority lists for who gets the vaccine, and those lists are not the same. Canadian officials will, therefore, have to work all the harder to make this country's strategy and guidelines clear.

On a local level, people need detailed, up-to-date information about such things as where and when they can get the vaccine if they are in an at-risk group. Parents need to know what's happening in schools.

This summer, the City of Ottawa decided it would only make spokespeople available to talk about H1N1 at weekly press conferences, and not in between. The thinking was that since the outbreak was relatively stable over the summer, restricting media interaction to press conferences would make for the most efficient use of staff. If something significant arose, the city communications staff said they would inform the media.

That plan might have been well-intentioned, but public information on a vital public health matter should be available for the asking at any time, rather than when government deigns it convenient. The city is revisiting its H1N1 communications policy for the fall; it would do well to have knowledgeable spokespeople available when journalists call.

Timing matters when it comes to getting information out during a pandemic. And if it turns out that H1N1 is having a lower impact in Ottawa than expected, the public needs to know that too. There's no point in causing economic harm -- especially during a recession -- by leading people to avoid crowds, parties, shopping malls and workplaces if there's no need to do so.

Of course, communication works in both directions. We're all in this together, and all Canadians have a responsibility to inform themselves and protect themselves. If you're feeling sick with flu-like symptoms, stay home from work and don't socialize. Wash your hands. Cough and sneeze into your sleeve. Eat your fruits and veggies to keep your immune system strong. Keep an eye out for friends and neighbours, especially people who might be at particular risk of developing complications if they get the flu.

Simple advice, maybe, but sometimes the simple things make a difference.

Symptoms

What it is: The new H1N1 flu virus is a respiratory illness that causes flu-like symptoms.

Who it affects: Youth more than other age groups. Half the people in Ottawa with swine flu have been children and teens.

Severity: Most cases have been mild in Ottawa.

How it spreads: In the same way seasonal flu does -- person to person, through coughing, sneezing, or touching contaminated objects. People can infect others from the day before their symptoms develop to seven days after they become sick.

Symptoms: Fever, cough, shortness of breath, muscle aches, fatigue, severe headache, sore throat, lack of appetite, possible nausea, vomiting, diarrhea.

Severe symptoms: High fever, difficulty breathing.

If you have ...

1) mild flu-like symptoms: Stay home from work or school. Sleep, drink plenty of fluids, stay away from others. Avoid tobacco smoke, use cold compresses for comfort.

2) severe flu-like symptoms: go to your doctor or a hospital emergency room.

3) flu-like symptoms and a chronic medical condition, such as asthma, diabetes or cancer: speak to your doctor. Most people can battle the virus on their own, but people with a chronic illness might need antiviral medication early.

4) flu-like symptoms and are pregnant: Speak to your doctor about antiviral treatment. Pregnant women, especially those in the third trimester, are at greater risk for complications.

If your symptoms are moderate and you can't decide what to do: Call Telehealth Ontario at 1-866-797-0000, or the city's Public Health Call Centre at 613-580-6744.

Am I Better?

Your bout with H1N1 is over when you feel well and no longer experience symptoms, usually 24 hours after the break of a fever. If a cough lingers, it's fairly normal, and not a reason to stay home.

Prevention

Wash hands frequently, with soap and water for at least 20 seconds.

Stay at least two metres away from people who have flu-like symptoms.

Carry hand sanitizer for use in crowded places.

Do not share things that go in the mouth.

Clean surfaces in your home on a daily basis.

If you have to cough or sneeze, cover your mouth with the inside of your elbow.

If the virus spreads, you might want to avoid crowds to avoid exposure.

Masks are not a good form of prevention because most people use them incorrectly, and contaminate themselves when they put the mask on and take it off. If you're sick, you can wear a surgical face mask to avoid exposing others, especially when you leave home. Also, caretakers should consider wearing a surgical face mask. After you take the mask off, throw it in the garbage and wash your hands.

Surgical face masks can be purchased at your local pharmacy, and are usually labelled "N-95," meaning that the mask has a 95-per-cent rate of filtration efficiency.

Treatment

Antivirals: Tamiflu and Relenza effectively treat the virus, but most patients recover without medication. Antivirals are best for moderate to severe cases, and people who have complications. Antivirals have been distributed to each province on a per capita basis. Prescriptions come from your doctor.

Vaccines: Immunization is likely to begin in November. The federal government is purchasing 50.4 million doses of H1N1 vaccine from GlaxoSmithKline. Provinces and territories will be responsible for delivering the vaccines. As of yet, no decisions have been made on who will get priority for vaccines.

When the second wave of the swine-flu outbreak lands this fall, Canadian public health officials will be armed with a national pandemic plan that's been years in the making.

Insiders say Canada is better prepared than it was during the 2003 SARS outbreak, which triggered a major overhaul of how the country responds to health emergencies.

The Public Health Agency of Canada, created in the wake of SARS, has drafted a 550-page national pandemic influenza plan that makes recommendations on everything from how doctors should diagnose the virus and how researchers should collect and test lab samples to what temperature should be maintained at temporary morgues.

But critics say crucial parts of the plan remain vague. In a recent editorial, the Canadian Medical Association Journal (CMAJ) called for a greater "sense of urgency" about the second wave of the outbreak, and made a number of proposals to improve Canada's response, including the appointment of an independent national public-health "czar."

The easiest way to create such a position, according to the CMAJ, would be to expand the powers of Canada's chief public health officer. Currently, the chief public health officer largely plays an advisory role to the federal health minister, as well as to provincial health authorities and the public. But in a pandemic, Canada needs someone in charge who has the independence to rise above politics, and the authority to compel the provinces to take action, said CMAJ editor-in-chief Paul Hébert.

"First and foremost, you need an authority figure to manage the crisis, like in any crisis. You need a general," Hébert said in an interview.

PHAC insists it has the situation under control.

Chief Public Health Officer Dr. David Butler-Jones told the House of Common health committee this month that PHAC's work could be compared to a duck moving smoothly across a pond with its legs furiously paddling beneath the water. "There's an incredible amount of work going on."

More details about the pandemic plan should become clear next month. On Sept. 2, Butler-Jones will start two days of meetings in Winnipeg with provincial public health officials, intensive-care specialists and medical experts. Allison Stuart, the senior public servant at the centre of Ontario's pandemic preparations, sighs heavily when asked what she was doing when SARS hobbled the provincial health system six years ago.

"You're gonna make me do this?" she says.

It's hard to tell if she's joking. Ontario officials have been heavily criticized for their handling of SARS, the mysterious respiratory disease that made its way into Canada in March 2003, killing 44 people.

"The wonder is not that the health system worked so badly during SARS," Justice Archie Campbell wrote in his commission's 1,204-page report released in early 2007, "but that it worked at all."

Campbell blasted the province for failing to protect doctors, nurses and other hospital employees in their workplaces. Seventy per cent of the 330 people who contracted SARS in the province were infected in a health-care setting. Two nurses and one doctor died.

From there, the commission's criticisms expanded.

"The lack of preparations against infectious disease, the decline of public health, the failure of systems that should protect nurses and paramedics and others from infection at work -- all these declines and failures went on through three successive governments of different political stripes," he wrote.

Stuart, who was the Ministry of Health's lead executive during SARS, remembers those troubled times well. It was her job to manage the ministry's response, then implement that plan -- or, in bureaucrat-speak, to "push that response out to" -- the health sector.

She said the system was utterly unprepared for the task.

"For many people in health, when we think of emergencies we think on an individual basis, somebody coming into an emergency room who's really, really sick, bleeding, or having a heart attack or something like that. We tended not to think of emergencies as something the health-care system would be the lead on ... as a system challenge."

Among the province's failings, the lack of effective communications was high on the list.

"We didn't have really good ways of making sure the right information was getting to the right people at the right time," Stuart says. "I remember that first night, I had a staff member who had to individually phone each hospital."

Stuart, who, as an acting assistant deputy minister charged with public health, is at the forefront of today's pandemic preparations, says things have changed since SARS, beginning with Ontario's first pandemic plan tabled in 2004.

The province has implemented a 24-hour communications cycle, which includes fixed points for daily happenings. Updates to the media, for example, are to occur daily at 3 p.m. All 36 local medical officers of health are to teleconference each day at 1 p.m. And a nightly bulletin is to be issued at midnight to ensure that in the morning, the entire health-care community has the same information.

"We want to provide a rhythm to communications in an emergency," Stuart explains. "We use a 24-hour clock and there are certain points on that clock that will not move. For example, if we were doing daily media updates, that happens at 3 o'clock. We work with that as a reality."

Dr. Arlene King, who was recently named the province's chief medical officer of health, has a different perspective. As the technical lead for the federal government when SARS hit, King was responsible for gathering information from provinces and territories and passing that on to the World Health Organization.

It was a "challenging" task, she says.

In the wake of SARS, a new structure was put in place to organize all the top brass of provincial and territorial public health organizations, says King. That new public-health network has alleviated some of the confusion that occurred during SARS.

King now leads the Ministry of Health's executive emergency management committee, which has been given new digs in an undisclosed office building in downtown Toronto.

From there, a group of about 15 staff monitors the course and behaviour of a potential pandemic, gathering information and preparing the provincial response on a 24-hour basis. An executive committee with representation from various affected ministries meets there often and holds daily conference calls with a range of health agencies, including hospitals, long-term care homes, public health units, provincial labs, federal and provincial governments and other ministries.

"It's not the best strategy to have to introduce yourself in the middle of an emergency," says Allison Stuart, referring to the chaotic communications that occurred during SARS. "You want to have a relationship there, where you know the players. And I believe that's well-implemented now."

The health ministry's emergency-management committee has honed its skills during several smaller incidents over the past number of years. They include an emergency evacuation of a remote native community, a power outage in a Sudbury hospital and a suspected infectious disease outbreak on a VIA Rail train in northern Ontario.

"There's never going to be a point where I would say we're done," says Stuart. "But do I think we're in reasonable shape? Yes."

Despite the government's claims, however, there are critics who say they have not gone far enough.

Linda Haslam-Stroud, president of the Ontario Nurses' Association, says the initial response to the first wave of the illness in the spring exposed a range of weaknesses in the system. While acknowledging that the lines of communications have improved since SARS, she says nurses were still unclear about how H1N1 was transmitted, in much the same way as they were during the outbreak six years ago.

She says hospitals also failed to screen new patients upon entry.

"Patients shouldn't be coming into (emergency rooms) and sitting in a waiting room exposing other patients to potential H1N1," she says. "They should be screened, given the appropriate protective equipment and then isolated."

Haslam-Stroud says H1N1 didn't become the full-blown outbreak that was feared, but if it did, hospitals would be unable to deal with any surge of admissions.

Moreover, nurses feel the government is slipping when it comes to applying the precautionary principle -- or, put simply, erring on the side of caution -- trumpeted in the SARS commission report.

That discussion crystallized on the use of N95 masks. Rather than ensuring all front-line health care workers received the masks immediately, Ontario public health officials held a prolonged debate over their effectiveness.

British Columbia, on the other hand, adopted measures to reduce the risk of infection, even in the absence of clear scientific evidence.

Haslam-Stroud credits the province for stockpiling the masks, but says nurses at some long-term care homes didn't have access to the masks during the recent H1N1 flu outbreak, a fact she attributes to a lack of proper planning.

"It's been six-plus years. You would think that with all the money put into debates, discussions and consultations that we would have this totally ready to go. And I really think we're not there," she says.

"Nurses want to be there for their patients. However, they're not willing to put their lives and their families' lives at risk."

What a difference a few years make. Despite recent American remakes of Godzilla, and despite director J.J. Abrams' attempts to create a homegrown, made-in-America monster with Cloverfield, the giant monsters have recently been buried by tiny ones.

Specifically, by microbes. The last few decades have witnessed a veritable pandemic of pandemic flicks, in which little monsters do what Godzilla never could: destroy not just Tokyo, but the world.

We've seen, for example, a lonely Vincent Price in the The Last Man on Earth (1964), which was remade as The Omega Man starring Charlton Heston in 1971, which then became a vehicle for Will Smith in 2007 as I am Legend. There was also a fourth version, I am Omega, starring straight-to-video martial artist Mark Dacascos, but forget I mentioned it.

Although each of these movies differs in details, the plots are similar enough -- humanity has been wiped out, except for a lone human and a not-so-merry band of photophobic deformities, as a result of those damnable scientists creating biological weapons or a cure for cancer that goes awry.

Then there was George Romero's 1968 classic Night of the Living Dead -- the granddaddy of zombie flicks -- in which our metabolically challenged anti-heroes first rise from a cemetery. We later learn, while they're chewing their way through a shopping mall in the sequel Dawn of the Dead, they were the product of a plague.

Next came Michael Chrichton's The Andromeda Strain, first filmed in 1971 in a faithful adaptation of the book, and "re-imagined" in a 2008 TV miniseries.

Here a satellite containing an alien microbe falls to Earth, and earthlings soon start falling themselves, and falling over themselves in search of an antidote.

In 1995, we saw Outbreak, probably the least fantastic of the pandemic movies in that not a single zombie or alien made the cut. Then again, having Dustin Hoffman scurrying around trying to save the world is probably fantastic enough.

The Brits got in on the action with 28 Days Later (2002) and its sequel 28 Weeks Later (2007) -- is there a prize for guessing what the third instalment will be called? Oh, wait, I just read that 28 Months Later will be released in 2011. Anyway, here we had a virus, released when animal rights activists vandalized a lab, turning people into, you guessed it, flesh-eating zombies.

Not to be outdone, the Spanish produced REC (2007) -- remade in English as the vastly inferior Quarantine (2008) -- in which a rabies-like virus, stolen by a doomsday cult from a military research lab, magically transforms people in an apartment building into -- do I have to tell you?

That's just a small sample of the worldwide end-of-the-world-in-a-microbe mythos. But how did the little monsters come to eclipse the big ones? And how did we come to be awash in viruses and bacteria? Blame Richard Matheson. The celebrated sci-fi author, who penned many of the original Twilight Zone episodes and a series of acclaimed novels and short stories, is also almost single-handedly responsible for the disease-apocalypse scenario, and for the rise of the zombie.

The year was 1954, the very year that Godzilla first strutted his stuff for Japanese audiences. In that year, Matheson also strutted his stuff, publishing the novel that would become his most famous: I am Legend.

Not that I am Legend. Not the one with Will Smith, or Charlton Heston, or Vincent Price. Matheson rejected all three treatments since they were insufficiently faithful to his vision.

Matheson's vision had Robert Neville, an apparently lone human at war with people who were infected by a bacterium and now resemble vampires.

Neville meets a woman, Ruth, who also appears uninfected, but is sympathetic to the vampires.

Eventually he discovers that Ruth is infected, that the vampires have begun to rebuild society and that they hate Neville for killing some of their members.

Neville is ultimately captured by the vampires, and comes to realize that he has become the biological deviant, that he is the sole survivor of an obsolete race, that he -- not the vampire -- is now legend. Upon realizing this, and with Ruth's help, he commits suicide.

One can readily see here the genesis of the pandemic-apocalypse theme as well as the zombie motif. George Romero in fact admitted that Night of the Living Dead was "ripped off" from I am Legend, and Stephen King, whose novel The Stand concerns a human-made superflu, has admitted a debt to Matheson.

King's recent novel Cell, which involves zombies, is dedicated to Matheson and Romero.

This explains the inspiration for pandemic films, but not their appeal. There's an easy answer for this, though: Unlike Godzilla, who, let's face it, is pretty hard to miss, microbes are, by definition, invisible. We never know when we're coming into contact with a virus or a bacterium and hence they produce the greatest fear of all: fear of the unknown.

And that fear is exacerbated by the breakdown in communications that inevitably follows an outbreak.

The appeal of movies also depends on the cultural milieu in which they arise. Many Japanese movie monsters were in some way associated with atomic weapons -- Godzilla, for instance, was the product of atomic radiation -- which is entirely understandable given that they were spawned in the only country that has experienced the horrors of nuclear war.

Similarly, and despite the fact that pandemic movies are virtually never scientifically accurate, these films rose to popularity in the age of AIDS, a time when some feared the disease would render I am Legend more science than fiction.

Reports of other exotic viruses, such as Ebola -- Outbreak concerned an Ebola-like virus -- and Hantavirus solidified this fear, and with new viruses such as H1N1 making appearances, dangerous microbes continue to occupy our imagination more than our bodies.

The invisibility of microbes also allows them to act as metaphors. For example, since I am Legend was published during the height of the "red scare," when people feared that undercover communists were threatening America, many commentators interpreted the novel as a narrative about communism.

Matheson has explicitly denied that this was his intention, but one can see Ruth, the apparent human who is actually working for and from the other side, as a metaphor for the undercover commies that Senator Joseph McCarthy sought to expose.

Microbes work equally well as a metaphor in the age of terrorism, since just as we never know who might be infected, people were oblivious to the fact that those quiet young men down the hall were preparing a terrorist attack on 9/11. Much like in pandemic movies, you can never tell for sure whether or when your neighbour might morph into a flesh-eater.

Given this state of affairs, the prognosis for pandemic flicks is good: They are likely to have a long and healthy life, even though they're more fiction than fact, more tale than truth.

They are, one might say, their own curious form of legend.

Don't wait until H1N1 influenza finds your family, says Vancouver Island Health Authority's chief medical health officer, Richard Stanwick. Start preparing now to reduce any impact on your family.

What's not known is when - and to what degree - the H1N1 pandemic will hit. The return to school could see a spike in flu cases ahead of the usual mid-winter flu season, according to Stanwick.

"Kids are the prime reason for the spread of a virus through the community," says Stanwick, both a pediatrician and epidemiologist.

Coughing kids spread viruses among themselves and then take them home to potentially infect their parents. This is one argument for first immunizing school kids and their parents to stall the spread of H1N1. Usually, immunization programs first target the very old and very young, chronically ill, pregnant women and health care workers, he says.

"People who are sick need to stay home. You don't have to be there. Cemeteries are filled with irreplaceable people," Stanwick says. This not only applies to work or school, but family visits, playdates and community events.

Your family's best defence against H1N1 is to stay healthy, he says. This includes the obvious - eat right, get enough sleep and exercise. "If people need a reason to stop smoking, this is it," he adds.

The key to combating any flu is frequent hand-washing, Stanwick adds.

Viruses can last on an unwashed hand for minutes and hard surfaces for hours. Inadvertently touching your eyes or nose introduces the virus to your body.

Use soap and water or a hand sanitizer for 20 seconds - a good gauge is as long as it takes to sing Happy Birthday, he says. Parents using, for example, public transit can carry alcohol gel packs or wipes when wall-sanitizers or soap and water are not handy.

Avoid coughing into the bare hand. Use a handkerchief. A better alternative, according to Stanwick, is coughing into the crook of your arm - an area less likely to be in contact with others or your own eyes or nose.

Now is the time to stockpile household cleaning supplies and a seven-day supply of non-perishable food, Stanwick says. Renew any prescriptions early.

There could be shortages during a flu crisis. Parents - sick themselves or home with a sick child - might be unable to leave the house to shop.

Further, he says, now is also the time to set up online banking to avoid unnecessary outings.

Plan for alternatives for child care, Stanwick says. Decide which working parent's medical plan is the most amenable to staying home with a sick family member. Also, if possible, arrange now with your employer to work from home to avoid exposure or to care for a family member.

Think twice before booking the family's spring break holiday, Stanwick says.

"It's a bit of a risk. We don't know how other countries will respond," he says.

In a virulent outbreak, other countries might close their borders or confine visitors to their hotels, Stanwick says. Buy cancellation insurance if booking a trip now for what could well be the height of the flu pandemic.

Ultimately, surviving the flu pandemic requires a "collective effort," he says. This means neighbours should plan ahead to assist each other as needed during a flu pandemic, as with any disaster such as an earthquake.

"It's your neighbour who's going to dig you out, not the fire and police," Stanwick says.

- Wash hands often with soap and warm water or use a hand sanitizer.

- Cover your nose and mouth with a tissue when coughing. Cough or sneeze into your sleeve.

- Refrain from touching eyes and nose

- Eat plenty of fruits and vegetables. Get lots of sleep and regular exercise.

- Get an annual flu shot and an H1N1 one when available.

- Stay home if you are sick or feel unwell.

- Avoid crowds.

Twenty-two people died. Some might well have been saved if a stronger federal health authority had produced a faster, more coherent response.

Failure to adequately monitor and disclose the deadly spread of SARS prompted the World Health Organization to warn against travelling to Canada in 2003. The Public Health Agency of Canada was established in the wake of that blunder to speak with an independent voice on health matters and take the lead in times of crisis.

It has fallen short of that ideal. Rather than being free of the political process, Butler-Jones, the country's first chief public health officer, is classed as a deputy minister serving the Minister of Health.

Ostensibly, he has freedom to speak publicly on health matters and report on issues of concern. But it would be naive to expect Butler-Jones, or any future public health chief, to be oblivious to political pressure.

Rather than rushing ahead in a panic over H1N1, and turning to a czar, Ottawa would better protect Canadians by taking measured and careful steps toward boosting the power and autonomy of its chief public health officer.

And improving the mechanisms for federal-provincial co-operation - and decision-making - in times of crisis.

The real games driving the next federal election may have less to do with policy or polls than skiing, skating and scoring.

Amid the madness swirling over a reason for Canada's opposition trio to defeat the Conservative government --billions more for employment insurance reform? On the eve of an economic recovery?Are they insane? --there is the weirdest consideration of them all.

The 2010 Vancouver Olympic Winter Games, some backroom boys insist, could force a vote this fall to avoid an election conflict early next year.

Ask a Liberal or a New Democrat why this Conservative government has to be defeated just 10 months into its theoretical five-year mandate and they say the Olympic Games complicate the government's logical demise during February or March budget confidence votes.

There are two theories bolstering this seemingly disconnected logic.

Some figure Finance Minister Jim Flaherty might be just Machiavellian enough to drop his budget while the torch relay is still nearing the Olympic cauldron, forcing a campaign during the Games. Seems far-fetched to me, but so was the idea of inserting a ban on political party financing in last fall's economic update.

The second political consideration is that Canada will finally score a bunch of gold medals as the host country. Montreal's Olympics in 1976? Nadda. Calgary's in 1988? Gold medal meltdown.

But Vancouver, well, there are mighty high expectations Canadians will finally hear their national anthem played on a domestic podium. That, the backrooms fret, would send a giddy electorate dancing on happy feet into polling stations where they might be more inclined to vote Conservative, a particularly important consideration in the crucial election battleground of southern British Columbia.

Yes, avoiding an election based on the men's hockey team landing gold is a tad odd, but so were reports Prime Minister Stephen Harper will base his campaign on seeking a majority mandate, at least until a new Ipsos Reid poll came out Friday that showed the Conservatives surging ahead and flirting with 40 per cent voter support.

But has the Olympic afterglow ever influenced an election outcome? The short answer is . . . maybe.

An analysis of prior host countries and subsequent election results does not suggest a good or bad incumbent correlation, but national patriotism and pride are intangibles when voters enter a polling station.

Canada's two Olympic tours of duty clearly did not resonate at the ballot box. The Calgary Games were followed eight months later by a federal election where Brian Mulroney reclaimed a majority from a single-issue campaign over free trade. It would be difficult to link fallout from the Montreal Summer Games of 1976 to an election three years later where a guy named Joe Clark briefly interrupted the Liberal reign.

Look hard and you might see a vague pattern in the American experience. Bill Clinton was handily re-elected following the Atlanta Games of 1996 and Ronald Reagan crushed Walter Mondale after the Los Angeles Games of 1984. But how to explain poor ol' Jimmy Carter, who got thrashed despite the patriotic fallout from the U. S. hockey team's Miracle on Ice at Lake Placid in 1980?

If nothing else, this shows the lunacy that has ensnared Ottawa as a listless political summer limps toward a fall with nothing on the agenda beyond a date for another neverendum referendum on a minority government.

Lost in the election speculation is any consideration of what's best for economically apprehensive Canadians.

This Parliament has, despite its venom-laced bickering, passed a robust agenda to tackle the recession, dealt with law-and-order concerns and kick-started another military equipment buildup.

To force the second $300-million election in a single year over a standardized and enriched employment insurance plan or to avoid a scheduling conflict with next February's Olympics borders on contempt for the voter.

Just in case they need a decent reason to avoid a November vote, consider this: If swine flu returns with a vengeance, any fall campaign would be reduced to empty rallies and door-knocking contact with voters through surgical masks.

When a sports event is an excuse to move up an unnecessary election, surely a pandemic is a reason to delay it.

SANTIAGO, Chile -- Chile said Friday that tests show H1N1 flu has jumped to birds, opening a new chapter in the global epidemic.

Top flu and animal-health experts with the United Nations in Rome and the U.S. Centers for Disease Control and Prevention in Atlanta were monitoring the situation closely, but said the infected turkeys have suffered only mild effects, easing concern about a potentially dangerous development.

Chile's turkey meat remains safe to eat, they said, and so far there have been no signs of a potentially dangerous mutation.

Chile's health ministry said it ordered a quarantine Friday for two turkey farms outside the port city of Valparaiso after genetic tests confirmed sick birds were afflicted with the same virus that has caused a pandemic among humans.

So far, the virus -- a mixture of human, pig and bird genes -- has proved to be very contagious but no more deadly than common seasonal flu. However, virus experts fear a more dangerous and easily transmitted strain could emerge if it combines again with avian flu, which is far more deadly but tougher to pass along.

The farms' owner, Sopraval SA, alerted the agriculture ministry after egg production dropped at the farms.
